What is Cupping Therapy and How Does It Work?

Cupping treatment is an old medicinal practice that includes applying cups to the skin to produce suction. This suction pulls the skin upward into the cup, which can have a variety of therapeutic benefits. Cupping therapy is based on traditional medicine systems such as Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) and has been used for centuries to promote healing and treat a variety of diseases. 

Types of Cupping Therapy:

  • Dry Cupping: Dry cupping involves placing cups on the skin and creating a vacuum to suck the skin upwards into the cup. Cups can be constructed of glass, silicone, plastic, or bamboo. Dry cupping can be done using a variety of techniques, including stationary cupping (when cups are left in place for a few minutes) and movement cupping.

  • Wet Cupping: Wet cupping is the process of making small incisions in the skin following dry cupping. The cups are then reapplied to pull out a small quantity of blood. Wet cupping is thought to help eliminate toxins from the body and promote healing.

Different Types of Cupping: Dry, Wet, and Fire

Cupping therapy has several modalities, each with its unique application and benefits. There are three forms of cupping: dry, moist, and fire. Let's go over each of these cupping treatments in detail:

  1. Dry Cupping: Dry cupping, sometimes called "dry" or "air" cupping, is the most popular type of cupping therapy. It consists of producing a vacuum within a cup and then placing the cup on the skin, causing the skin and superficial muscle layer to be sucked upward into the cup. The benefits of Dry Cupping include improved blood circulation and lymph drainage. Reduces muscle tension and stiffness. It helps to alleviate pain and inflammation. It improves detoxification by stimulating the body's natural cleansing processes.

  2. Wet Cupping (Hijama): Wet cupping, also called "bleeding" or "controlled suction cupping," is a two-step treatment that includes dry cupping with controlled bleeding. The benefits of wet cupping include the removal of stagnated blood and toxins from the body. It supports cleansing and improves blood circulation. It is frequently used in traditional medical systems for a variety of health issues.

  3. Fire Cupping: Fire cupping, sometimes referred to as "traditional cupping" or "heated cupping," is a technique that uses fire to generate a vacuum inside a cup before putting it on the skin. Fire cupping has similar benefits to dry cupping, including improved circulation and muscle relaxation. Some practitioners feel that the heat of fire cupping enhances therapeutic outcomes.

Addressing Safety: Side Effects and Precautions of Cupping Therapy

Cupping therapy is generally regarded as safe when administered by a competent and qualified practitioner. However, like with any treatment technique, cupping has dangers and potential adverse effects. It is critical to be aware of these concerns and take the required measures before beginning cupping therapy. Here's an entire overview of the safety considerations, including side effects and precautions for cupping therapy:

Side Effects of Cupping Therapy:

  • The most common side effect of cupping therapy is transient skin discoloration or bruising around the cupping area. This discoloration is usually painless and disappears in a few days to a week.

  • Cupping can cause circular markings or redness on the skin due to the suction effect. Some people may notice slight skin discomfort or pain in the treated area.

  • Cupping therapy can cause brief dizziness or lightheadedness, especially if the cups are placed to specific acupuncture points or if the treatment is extended.

  • Fire cupping, which uses a flame to create suction, can cause skin irritation or burns if not done correctly. Proper training and supervision are required for fire cupping.

  • Improperly sterilized cupping equipment or unhygienic practices can increase the risk of infection, particularly if the skin is broken during wet cupping (where small incisions are made).

Precautions for Cupping Therapy:

  • Before undergoing cupping therapy, especially if you have underlying health conditions or are pregnant, consult with a healthcare provider to ensure it's safe and appropriate for you.

  • Choose a qualified and experienced cupping practitioner who adheres to hygiene standards and follows proper procedures to minimize risks.

  • Cupping should not be performed over open wounds, irritated skin, or areas with active infections to prevent potential complications.

  • Ensure that the suction pressure and duration of cupping therapy are appropriate for your tolerance level and individual health status.

  • Drink plenty of water before and after cupping therapy to stay hydrated and support detoxification. Allow time to rest and relax after the session.

  • Inform your cupping practitioner about any medical history, allergies, or concerns you may have before starting the treatment

Precautionary Measures: Who Should Avoid Cupping?

While cupping therapy is generally regarded as safe for many people when conducted by a qualified practitioner, there are certain caveats and contraindications to consider. Cupping therapy may not be appropriate for everyone owing to certain health issues or other causes. Here's an explanation of people who should avoid cupping or use caution before getting this treatment:

  1. Bleeding Disorders: Individuals with bleeding disorders (e.g., haemophilia) or those using anticoagulants should avoid cupping therapy since it increases the risk of bleeding or bruising.

  2. Skin Conditions: Cupping should not be used on parts of the skin with open wounds, active infections, dermatitis, or severe skin disorders (e.g., eczema, psoriasis) to avoid further irritation or consequences.

  3. Pregnancy: Pregnant women should avoid cupping therapy, particularly in the stomach or lower back regions, to avoid potentially stimulating uterine contractions and reducing risk.

  4. Severe Health Conditions: Individuals with severe health conditions (e.g., cancer, advanced cardiovascular disease, uncontrolled hypertension) should consult with a healthcare provider before undergoing cupping therapy, as it may worsen certain conditions or interfere with ongoing medical treatments.

  5. Fragile or Sensitive Skin: People with extremely delicate or fragile skin may have more skin irritation, bruising, or discomfort as a result of cupping therapy. It is critical to discuss skin concerns with the practitioner beforehand.

  6. Children and Elderly: Cupping therapy may not be acceptable for small children or the elderly who have delicate skin or underlying health concerns unless properly assessed and supervised by a healthcare professional or dermatologists.
