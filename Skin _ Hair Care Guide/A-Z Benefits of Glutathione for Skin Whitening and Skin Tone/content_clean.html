Understanding melanin and hyperpigmentation in skin

Melanin is the pigment that determines the color of our skin, hair, and eyes. Melanocytes, which are specialized cells found in the skin, produce it. Melanin production is influenced by a variety of factors, including genetics, sun exposure, hormonal changes, and certain medications. When exposed to UV radiation, melanocytes produce more melanin as a protective response to protect the skin. This can result in a tan, but excessive UV exposure can also cause hyperpigmentation or the formation of dark spots and patches on the skin. Other factors, such as acne, inflammation, and certain medical conditions, can also contribute to hyperpigmentation. Melasma, post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation and age spots are the most common types of hyperpigmentation. Melasma is a type of hyperpigmentation that is common in women, particularly during pregnancy or while using hormonal contraception. It appears on the face as brown or grayish-brown patches, most notably on the cheeks, forehead, and upper lip.

Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation is a type of hyperpigmentation caused by skin injuries or inflammation, such as acne or a cut. It appears as dark spots or patches that are frequently red or purple in color. Age spots, also known as liver spots or solar lentigines, are a type of hyperpigmentation that is most common in elderly people. They appear as flat, tanned or brown spots on areas of skin that are frequently exposed to sunlight, such as the face, hands, and arms. Hyperpigmentation can be treated with topical creams, chemical peels, or laser treatments. It is also critical to protect the skin from further damage by wearing sunscreen and participating in sun-safe activities.
