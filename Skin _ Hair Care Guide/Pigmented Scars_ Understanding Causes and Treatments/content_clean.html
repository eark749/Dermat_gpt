Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH)

A common skin disorder called post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) is characterised by darker or discoloured patches that develop on the skin following an inflammatory or traumatic lesion. Increased melanin production as a result of inflammation, damage, or specific skin conditions causes it to happen. In relation to post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation, keep in mind the following:

  1. Cause: Melanin, the pigment responsible for the colour of our skin, hair, and eyes, is overproduced in PIH. Melanin production increases when the skin becomes inflamed or traumatised as a result of acne, cuts, burns, or other skin conditions. This excess melanin builds up in the affected area, resulting in the formation of dark or discoloured patches.

  2. Appearance: PIH typically manifests as flat, hyperpigmented patches that can be pink, red, brown, or even black in colour. The severity of the inflammation or injury as well as the person's skin tone can affect the colour of the PIH. PIH differs from true scars in that it does not result in permanent changes to the skin's texture or contour.

  3. Common Triggers: Acne, eczema, dermatitis, insect bites, cuts, burns, or skin treatments and procedures that cause inflammation can all cause PIH. 

  4. Duration: The duration of PIH can vary among individuals. Most of the time, as the skin naturally renews itself, it gradually fades over time. The pigmented patches may, however, take several weeks to several months to lighten and meld with the surrounding skin. In some instances, PIH may last for a longer period of time, necessitating specialised treatments to help the discoloration fade.

  5. Treatment: PIH frequently gets better on its own with time, but there are medications that can hasten the fading process. Retinoids, vitamin C serums, chemical peels, and skin-lightening products like hydroquinone, kojic acid, and azelaic acid can all be used as topical treatments to lessen the appearance of PIH. The best course of treatment for your unique condition and skin type should be decided in consultation with a dermatologist or skincare expert.

  6. Preventive action: Taking proactive steps can reduce the likelihood of developing PIH. This includes refraining from picking at or squeezing pimples or other skin lesions, taking proper wound care measures, applying sunscreen with a high SPF, and limiting exposure to the sun.

Types of Pigmented Scars

There are various types of pigmented scars that can develop on the skin. Here are some examples of common types:

  1. Post-inflammatory Hyperpigmentation (PIH): This type of pigmented scar develops as a result of skin inflammation or injury. It can happen as a result of acne, insect bites, burns, cuts, or other traumatic skin events. PIH appears on the skin as darkened patches or spots, and the pigmentation can range from light brown to dark brown or even black. PIH usually fades with time, but it can take months or years to completely resolve.

  2. Melasma: A pigmentation disorder that typically affects the face and is characterised by brown or greyish patches of skin. It is more common in women, particularly during pregnancy (chloasma or "the mask of pregnancy") or as a result of hormonal changes caused by contraception or hormone replacement therapy. It is believed that both hormonal and sun exposure factors have an impact on melasma.

  3. Post-inflammatory Erythema (PIE): Although technically not pigmented scars, PIE describes the red or purple discoloration that develops after skin inflammation or trauma. It happens as a result of the blood vessels in the affected area dilating. PIE is frequently observed after skin trauma or acne breakouts, and the redness may last for weeks or months.

  4. Hypopigmentation: When compared to the surrounding skin, hypopigmented areas of the skin appear lighter or have less pigment. It can develop as a side effect of certain treatments like laser therapy or chemical peels, or it can result from certain skin conditions like vitiligo or fungal infections.

  5. Surgical or Traumatic Scars: Pigmented areas can occasionally be found in scars that result from surgical procedures or traumatic injuries. Various factors, including skin type, the healing process, and sun exposure, can affect pigmentation, ranging from light to dark. 

Atrophic scars

Atrophic scars are a particular kind of scar that appears as a result of tissue loss in the skin. Depressed or sunken spots on the skin's surface are what they are known for. Acne, chickenpox, or wounds involving the loss of underlying collagen or fat frequently result in atrophic scars. Atrophic scars can take a variety of forms, including:

  1. Ice Pick Scars: Ice pick scars are deep, slender, and resemble small punctures or holes in the skin. They typically result from severe acne and penetrate deeply into the layers of the skin.

  2. Boxcar Scars: Boxcar scars are more pronounced at the edges and are wider. They appear as shallow to moderately deep depressions with sharp, box-like edges on the skin's surface. Inflammatory acne frequently leaves behind boxcar scars.

  3. Rolling Scars: On the skin, rolling scars appear wavy or undulating. They have an uneven texture because the collagen fibres underneath have been damaged. The skin may look wave-like or like it has "rolling hills" as a result of rolling scars.

  4. Hypertrophic Scars: Although they are frequently connected to raised or elevated scars, hypertrophic scars can also have an atrophic component. The overproduction of collagen during the healing process of a wound causes hypertrophic scars, which can occasionally appear sunken or depressed in the middle.

The severity and type of the scar can influence the treatment options for atrophic scars. Some frequent remedies include:

  • Dermal Fillers: To swell and fill in the hollowed-out areas of atrophic scars and give them a smoother appearance, injectable fillers can be used.

  • Microneedling: A device with tiny needles is used to create controlled micro-injuries in the skin, stimulating collagen production and improving the appearance of atrophic scars.

  • Chemical Peels: A chemical solution is applied to the skin, causing controlled exfoliation and stimulating collagen production, reducing the appearance of atrophic scars.

  • Laser Therapy: Various laser treatments, such as fractional laser resurfacing, can help improve the appearance of atrophic scars by stimulating collagen production and promoting skin remodelling.

Treating Pigmented Scars

The goal of treating pigmented scars is to lessen the appearance of darkening or discoloration in the scar tissue. Inflammation, trauma, or other skin diseases that interfere with the natural pigmentation process might result in scarring that is pigmented. The following are some potential treatments to take into account:

  1. Topical creams or ointments with ingredients like hydroquinone, kojic acid, azelaic acid, or retinoids may help lighten pigmented scars. These creams work by reducing melanin production or encouraging skin exfoliation.

  2. Chemical peels involve the application of a chemical solution to the skin in order to remove the top layers, thereby promoting cell turnover and revealing fresher, more evenly pigmented skin. Chemical peels can help to reduce the visibility of pigmented scars.

  3. Microdermabrasion is a non-invasive procedure that uses tiny exfoliating crystals or a diamond-tipped wand to gently remove the skin's outer layer. It can help to reduce the visibility of scar pigmentation irregularities.

  4. Intense pulsed light (IPL) and fractional laser resurfacing are two examples of laser therapies that can be used to treat pigmented scars. These lasers specifically target and break down melanin in the skin, resulting in a more even skin tone.

  5. Cryotherapy can help lighten the colour of the pigmented scar by freezing it with liquid nitrogen. Some pigmented scar types, like those brought on by acne, are frequently treated with cryotherapy.

  6. Sun protection is essential for preventing further darkening of the scar. Sunscreen with a high SPF should be applied to the scarred area on a daily basis, and protective clothing or accessories should be worn when out in the sun.

  7. The pigmented scar can be temporarily hidden and made less noticeable by using specialised makeup items like colour correctors or concealers.

  4. Gentle massage: Gently massaging the incision after it has healed and is no longer open or scabbed can assist to increase blood circulation and lessen the production of scar tissue. Apply a mild moisturiser or scar-reducing cream, massage the scar in circular motions, and keep your pressure light. For more detailed advice, speak with your dermatologist or medical professional.

  5. Consider silicone sheets or gels: It has been demonstrated that silicone sheets or gels can aid in preventing hyperpigmentation and other aberrant scar formation.   6. Maintain a healthy lifestyle: Maintaining a healthy lifestyle can help with wound healing and skin health. Eat a well-balanced, nutrient-dense diet, stay hydrated, and avoid smoking, which can impede healing and increase the risk of complications.

Common misconceptions about pigmented scars

There are several common misconceptions about pigmented scars that are important to address. These misconceptions can lead to misunderstandings and incorrect treatment approaches. Here are a few of these misconceptions:

  1. Scars that have been pigmented will fade on their own: It is possible for some pigmented scars to fade over time, although this is not always the case. Individual differences in the natural healing process of scars can cause some to last longer than others. To improve the appearance of pigmented scars, active intervention may be required, such as the use of suitable creams and therapies.

  2. Sun exposure will help lighten pigmented scars: This is a common misconception, but it is not true.  Sun exposure can aggravate and darken pigmented scars. UV radiation from the sun stimulates melanocytes (pigment-producing cells), which can result in scar hyperpigmentation.  Sun protection and the use of sunscreen are critical for minimising pigmentation.

  3. Scrubbing or exfoliating will remove pigmented scars: Excessive rubbing or rough exfoliation might irritate the skin and possibly make pigmented scars appear worse. It's crucial to be kind to the scarred region and to refrain from using abrasive cleaners or severe cleaning. For proper exfoliating techniques or treatments made especially for skin with scars, consult a dermatologist.

  4. Only topical treatments can reduce pigmented scars: Although topical treatments can help to lessen the visibility of pigmented scars, they might not be enough to significantly reduce scar size. Other treatment modalities, such as laser therapy, microdermabrasion, or chemical peels, might be required depending on the type and severity of the pigmented scar. A dermatologist's advice can help you choose the best course of action for your particular scar.

  5. It is possible to completely remove all pigmented scars: It's important to have reasonable expectations even though various treatments can help pigmented scars look better. It might not always be possible to completely remove pigmented scars.  The purpose of treatment is to achieve an even skin tone, reduce the scar's visibility, and improve its texture.  A healthcare professional should be consulted for a customised assessment and treatment plan because each scar and person's response to treatment are different. 

Importance of sunscreen for pigmented scars

For many reasons, sun protection is crucial for people with pigmented scars.

  1. Preventing Darkening: Sun exposure can cause hyperpigmentation, which increases the visibility of pigmented scars. UV rays have the ability to stimulate melanocytes, the skin's pigment-producing cells, increasing melanin production and darkening scars. Applying sunscreen helps to create a barrier that hinders damaging UV rays from penetrating scarred skin, lowering the possibility of hyperpigmentation.

  2. Minimising Inflammation: Sun exposure can cause skin inflammation, which may slow healing and conceivably make pigmented scars more noticeable. Scars on inflamed skin may turn darker or redder and are more prone to discoloration. Sunscreen shields the scarred skin from UV-induced inflammation, facilitating a faster healing process.

  3. Promoting Scar Healing: UV radiation can impede the natural healing of scars, possibly resulting in the formation of abnormal scars. Sunscreen shields the sun-damaged skin from its harmful rays, allowing the scar to heal properly. This can result in a more favourable outcome, with improved texture and colour of the scar over time.

  4. Maintaining Treatment Results: Consistent sunscreen use is essential if you're undergoing procedures to lighten or reduce pigmented scars, such as chemical peels, laser therapy, or topical treatments. These procedures may increase the skin's sensitivity to the sun, and unprotected sun exposure can undo any reduction in pigmentation that has already taken place.

  5. Sun Protection: To avoid darkening and worsening pigmentation, it's important to keep pigmented scars out of the sun. Even on overcast days, cover the scarred area with a broad-spectrum sunscreen with a high SPF before venturing outside. Every two hours, reapply sunscreen, and whenever possible, find shade.

  6. Avoid Picking or Scratching: Refrain from picking or scratching the scarred area because doing so can delay healing and possibly cause more scarring or hyperpigmentation. Avoid aggressive behaviour that could harm the skin by keeping your hands away from the scar.

  7. Patience and Consistency: Treating pigmented scars takes time and consistency. The severity, type, and individual factors of the scar may all influence the outcome. Maintain a consistent home care routine and be patient. It may take several weeks or months for the scar's appearance to improve noticeably.

Risks and side effects of pigmented scar treatments

Professional or at-home treatments for pigmented scars may come with some dangers and negative effects. Before beginning any particular treatment, it's crucial to be aware of these possible side effects and discuss them with a dermatologist or other healthcare provider. The followings are some common dangers and adverse reactions related to pigmented scar treatments:

  1. Skin Sensitivity and Irritation: Some treatments, including topical creams or gels, chemical peels, and exfoliating agents, can make the skin irritated and sensitive. If the procedure is too harsh or if you have sensitive skin, this is more likely to happen.  Before using any new product in a larger area, it's crucial to carefully read the directions and carry out a patch test.

  2. Hyperpigmentation or hypopigmentation: Pigmented scar treatments can cause changes in skin pigmentation in some cases. Hyperpigmentation is an increase in skin colour that causes darker patches or spots, whereas hypopigmentation is a decrease in skin colour that causes lighter patches. These pigmentary changes can occur as a result of incorrect treatment techniques, overtreatment, or individual skin reactions.

  3. Allergic Reactions: Some people may experience allergic reactions to certain treatment products or ingredients. Symptoms may include redness, swelling, itching, rash, or hives. It's important to carefully read ingredient labels and stop using anything if you notice any symptoms of an allergic reaction.  If necessary, seek the advice of a healthcare professional for the best course of action. 

  4. Skin Dryness or Peeling: Some therapies, like chemical peels or exfoliating substances, can make the skin temporarily dry, flaky, or peel. This is a common side effect of treatment and can be managed with moisturisers and gentle skincare. However, if the dryness or peeling is severe or persistent, it is critical to seek medical attention.

  5. Infection or scarring: Invasive treatments, such as laser therapy or surgical procedures, pose a minor risk of infection or scarring. Following proper post-treatment care instructions, keeping the treated area clean, and seeking medical attention if you notice signs of infection, such as increased redness, pain, swelling, or pus, is critical.

  6. Sun Sensitivity: Certain treatments can make the skin more sun sensitive.  By wearing sunscreen, looking for shade, and taking the necessary sun protection precautions, you can prevent the treated area from being overexposed to the sun. Lack of sun protection can cause skin damage, increased pigmentation, or a delay in healing.

A dermatologist or other healthcare provider should be consulted so they can assess your particular scar and make tailored recommendations. They can advise you on the possible drawbacks and risks of various therapies, evaluate the suitability of your skin for particular interventions, and help you decide which course of action is best for your pigmented scars.
