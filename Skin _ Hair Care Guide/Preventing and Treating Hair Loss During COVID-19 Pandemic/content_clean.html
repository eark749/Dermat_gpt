Understanding Hair Loss During COVID-19

One typical worry that people may have during the COVID-19 epidemic is hair loss. The stress and lifestyle changes brought on by the pandemic may hasten hair loss or exacerbate pre-existing conditions, even though the virus does not directly cause hair loss. Keep the following in mind regarding hair loss right now:

  1. Telogen Effluvium: Stress, illness, or significant life events can cause telogen effluvium, a condition that is characterized by excessive hair loss. This shedding occurs approximately 2-3 months after the triggering event. The COVID-19 pandemic and the stressors associated with it have the potential to cause telogen effluvium.

  2. Emotional Stress: Many people have experienced increased emotional stress as a result of the pandemic. Emotional stress, such as anxiety, fear, or uncertainty, can cause or exacerbate existing hair loss conditions, especially if you are genetically predisposed to them.

  3. Lifestyle Changes: During the pandemic, adjustments to daily routines, disturbed sleep cycles, dietary changes, and decreased physical activity can all have an effect on overall health, including the health of one's hair. Nutritional imbalances or deficiencies can have an impact on hair growth and cause hair loss.

  4. Recovery from illness: Hair loss may occur for some COVID-19 patients as they recover. The physiological stress a disease places on the body is thought to cause this condition, which is similar to telogen effluvium.

Causes of COVID-19 Hair Loss

Hair loss is not directly attributed to COVID-19. However, there are a number of COVID-19-related variables that might cause hair loss or aggravate pre-existing hair loss disorders. Here are a few potential reasons:

  1. Significant hair loss indicates that this is a temporary case of hair loss. Stress on the body or mind, disease, or significant life events can cause it. The stress and anxiety brought on by the pandemic may cause telogen effluvium, which would accelerate hair loss.

  2. Many people have experienced substantial emotional and psychological hardship as a result of the COVID-19 pandemic. Anxiety and stress can interfere with the hair cycle, which results in hair loss. Emotional stress from unresolved medical issues, unstable financial situations, or social isolation can cause or worsen pre-existing hair loss disorders.

  3. Hair loss is a potential side effect for some COVID-19 patients as they recover. A severe or protracted illness might alter the natural cycle of hair development, resulting in momentary hair shedding.

  4. The pandemic has significantly altered people's way of life and everyday activities. Sleep apnea, dietary changes, and a decline in physical activity can all have an impact on the health of your hair. Inadequate nutrition, insufficient sleep, and sedentary lifestyles can all worsen hair loss.

  5. People with severe COVID-19 symptoms may occasionally need to take drugs or undergo medical procedures that involve adverse effects, such as hair loss. For instance, a side effect of several drugs used to treat COVID-19 or its consequences could be temporary hair loss.

Types of Hair Loss Associated with COVID-19

While COVID-19 does not specifically cause any particular types of hair loss, some people have shown various patterns of hair loss during or after the COVID-19 infection. The following two types of hair loss have been linked to COVID-19:

  1. Telogen Effluvium: Telogen effluvium is a condition that results in temporary hair loss due to disturbances in the hair growth cycle. It frequently results from severe physical or emotional stress, sickness, or big life events. The rate of hair loss from the scalp increases during telogen effluvium. Before the hair growth cycle returns to normal, this shedding can linger for several months and usually starts two to three months after the event that started it. Telogen effluvium has been found in some COVID-19 survivors as a result of the psychological and physiological effects of the sickness.

  2. Alopecia Areata: An autoimmune disorder called alopecia areata results in uneven hair loss. There have been instances of people getting alopecia areata during or after COVID-19 infection, even if the precise relationship between the two conditions is still not entirely established. It is thought that the virus's immunological response may play a role in the development or flare-up of this illness. 

It's important to remember that certain types of hair loss can occur for reasons unrelated to COVID-19. If you have significant hair loss or changes in your hair pattern during or after a COVID-19 infection, you should consult a dermatologist or other healthcare provider who specializes in hair loss. They can assess your specific situation, make an accurate diagnosis, and recommend appropriate management techniques or treatment options based on your specific needs.

When to Seek Medical Attention

If you experience any unsettling health-related symptoms or disorders, such as hair loss, it is critical that you consult a doctor. You should think about contacting a dermatologist or healthcare provider in the following circumstances:

  1. Sudden or Severe Hair Loss: If you notice a significant increase in hair shedding or thinning of your hair, you should consult a healthcare professional. Sudden or severe hair loss may indicate an underlying medical condition that needs to be evaluated and treated.

  2. Hair Loss Associated with Other Symptoms: If you have hair loss along with other troubling symptoms like scalp redness, itching, pain, scaling, or any other skin abnormalities, you should seek medical attention. These symptoms could be the result of an underlying scalp or skin condition that requires treatment.

  3. Patchy or Prolonged Hair Loss: If you notice patchy or complete hair loss (bald patches), it could be an indication of an autoimmune condition such as alopecia areata. A dermatologist who specializes in hair loss can assist with proper diagnosis and management.

  4. Hair Loss After COVID-19: If you have recovered from COVID-19 and are experiencing significant hair loss or changes in your hair pattern, you should see a healthcare professional. They can assess your situation and determine whether there are any underlying causes of your hair loss.

  5. Emotional Impact and Distress: Hair loss can cause significant emotional distress in people. If hair loss is causing you distress or has a negative impact on your quality of life, it's a good idea to seek help from a healthcare professional who can offer advice, reassurance, and appropriate treatment options.

Nutrition for Hair Health

Proper nutrition is essential for maintaining healthy hair. The following are some essential nutrients for hair health:

  1. Protein: Keratin, a type of protein, makes up the majority of hair. Lean meats, poultry, fish, eggs, dairy products, legumes, and nuts are some examples of foods high in protein that can help promote healthy hair growth.

  2. Biotin: Biotin, also referred to as vitamin B7, is necessary for strong hair growth. It can be found in a variety of foods, including eggs, nuts, seeds, whole grains, and leafy greens. Some people may benefit from biotin supplements, but it's best to consult with a doctor before beginning any supplements.

  3. Omega-3 Fatty Acids: Omega-3 fatty acids support the health of the scalp and feed the hair follicles. Increase your intake of omega-3 fatty acids by including fatty fish (like salmon, sardines, and mackerel), flaxseeds, chia seeds, walnuts, and avocados in your diet.

  4. Iron: A lack of iron is a typical contributor to hair loss. Lean meats, poultry, fish, legumes, and leafy greens are some examples of foods high in iron that you should include in your diet. By consuming foods high in vitamin C along with iron-containing foods, iron absorption can be improved.

  5. Vitamins A, C, and E: Due to their antioxidant characteristics, these vitamins aid in preventing free radical damage to hair follicles. Increase your intake of these vitamins by eating things like citrus fruits, berries, tomatoes, spinach, carrots, sweet potatoes, almonds, and sunflower seeds.

  6. Zinc and selenium: These minerals aid in the growth and repair of hair tissue. Oysters, lean meats, poultry, legumes, nuts, and seeds are all good sources of zinc. Brazil nuts, fish, poultry, and whole grains all contain selenium.

  7. Vitamin D: Adequate vitamin D levels are necessary for the proper functioning of hair follicles. Spend time outside in the sun to allow your body to naturally produce vitamin D. Fatty fish, fortified dairy products, and egg yolks are all good sources of vitamin D.

  8. Hydration: Adequate water consumption is essential for overall health, including hair health. It keeps the scalp hydrated and increases circulation to the hair follicles.

Hair Care Routine During COVID-19

To keep your hair healthy and well-groomed during COVID-19, you must continue your regular hair care routine. Here are some recommendations for a hair care regimen at this time:

  1. Regular Washing: Frequently wash your hair with a mild shampoo that is appropriate for your hair type. Cleaning your hair can help get rid of environmental pollutants like dirt, oil, and other impurities. If necessary, aim to wash your hair two to three times per week.

  2. Conditioner: To hydrate and nourish your hair after shampooing, use a conditioner. Apply the conditioner to your hair, avoiding the scalp, from the middle of the length to the ends. Before giving it a thorough rinse, let it sit for a few minutes.

  3. Scalp Care: Keep your scalp clean and well-maintained to maintain its health. While shampooing, give your scalp a light massage to encourage blood flow. Consider using a gentle exfoliating scalp scrub or consulting a dermatologist if you experience dryness or flakiness.

  4. Hair Mask or Deep Conditioning Treatment: To replenish moisture, repair damage, and improve shine, give your hair a deep conditioning treatment or hair mask once a week or as needed. Consider purchasing products that contain nourishing components like argan oil, shea butter, or coconut oil.

  5. Avoid Using Heat Styling Tools: Avoid using heat-styling tools like blow dryers, straighteners, and curling irons. Excessive heat can damage the hair and cause it to become dry. If you must use heat styling, use a heat protectant spray and the lowest heat setting available.

  6. Protective Hairstyles: Consider wearing protective hairstyles that keep your hair secure and minimize manipulation. This can help to prevent breakage and damage. You can also wear a silk or satin scarf or bonnet while you sleep, or you can wear braids, buns, or ponytails.

  7. Hair Trimming: Trim your hair on a regular basis to remove split ends and promote healthy hair growth. Trimming your hair every 6-8 weeks or as needed can help maintain its overall health and appearance. 

  8. Avoid Over-Styling: At this time, embrace natural hairstyles that require little to no styling. Excessive brushing, backcombing, and the use of tight hair accessories can all lead to tension and breakage.

  9. Balanced Diet and Hydration: To support hair health, maintain a healthy diet rich in nutrients, vitamins, and minerals. Stay hydrated by drinking plenty of water, as dehydration can disrupt your hair's moisture balance.

  10. Overall Well-Being: Pay attention to your overall well-being because it can have a positive impact on your hair health. To maintain a healthy body and mind, use stress management techniques, get enough sleep, exercise regularly, and eat a well-balanced diet.

To help reduce inflammation and encourage hair regrowth, these injections are given directly into the damaged areas of the scalp. A dermatologist commonly administers this therapy.

  5. Platelet-Rich Plasma (PRP) Therapy: In PRP therapy, a patient's own blood sample is taken, processed to isolate platelets, and then the platelet-rich plasma is injected back into the scalp. development factors included in platelets can promote hair development and enhance the condition of hair follicles. Medical Treatments for Hair Loss

Depending on the underlying reason and kind of hair loss, there are several medical therapies available. Here are some typical medical procedures:

  1. Minoxidil (Rogaine): Minoxidil is an over-the-counter topical medication that is applied directly to the scalp. It is commonly used to treat both men and women with androgenetic alopecia (pattern hair loss). Minoxidil may aid in stimulating hair growth, extending the growth phase of the hair cycle, and increasing hair thickness. It takes consistent, long-term use to reap the benefits.

  2. Finasteride (Propecia): Finasteride is a prescription medication used to treat male pattern baldness. It works by preventing testosterone from being converted to dihydrotestosterone (DHT), a hormone that can contribute to hair loss. Finasteride may help men with hair loss and regrowth. It is not advised for use by women, particularly those who are pregnant or planning to become pregnant.

  3. Hair Transplantation: Hair transplantation is the process of removing hair follicles from one area of the scalp (typically the back or sides) and transplanting them into areas where hair is thinning or missing. A dermatologist or a hair transplant specialist will perform this surgical procedure. It is frequently used to treat pattern baldness and can provide long-term results. 

  4. Low-Level Laser Therapy (LLLT): LLLT stimulates hair growth and improves the health of hair follicles by using red light wavelengths. It is usually done with the aid of specialized equipment such as laser combs or helmets. LLLT may aid in increasing blood flow to the scalp, promoting hair regrowth, and decreasing hair loss.

  5. Platelet-Rich Plasma (PRP) Therapy: PRP therapy involves extracting a sample of the patient's own blood, processing it to isolate platelets, and then injecting the platelet-rich plasma back into the scalp. Growth factors found in platelets can promote hair growth and enhance the condition of hair follicles. A dermatologist or a specialist in hair restoration typically administers PRP therapy.

  6. Scalp micro pigmentation: Scalp micro pigmentation is a non-surgical technique in which tiny dots are tattooed on the scalp to resemble hair follicles. It can give the appearance of a fuller head of hair and is frequently used to disguise thinning or receding hair.
