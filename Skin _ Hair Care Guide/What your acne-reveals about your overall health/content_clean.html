Acne is a common skin condition that occurs when hair follicles become clogged with oil and dead skin cells. While acne is primarily a skin issue, it can sometimes be an indicator of underlying health problems or imbalances. Some ways in which acne can be linked to overall health are hormonal imbalances, stress and mental health, diet, digestive health, medications, allergies, and sensitivities. It's important to note that while there can be connections between acne and overall health, acne is primarily a skin condition, and not every pimple or breakout should be assumed to have an underlying health issue. 

Different Types of Acne and Their Significance

Acne is a skin condition that can take many different forms. Acne kinds can range in appearance, severity, and underlying reasons. Understanding the different types of acne and their significance can aid in identifying the best treatment and management options. Whiteheads (Closed Comedones), Blackheads (Open Comedones), Papules, Pustules, Nodules, and Cysts are some of the most prevalent kinds of acne. The intensity and propensity for scarring determine the relevance of the various types of acne. When compared to inflammatory acne (papules, pustules, nodules, and cysts), non-inflammatory acne (whiteheads and blackheads) is milder and less prone to produce scarring. Severe inflammatory acne is more likely to leave permanent scars and can have a greater impact on a person's self-esteem and quality of life.

Acne Vulgaris: The Common Form of Acne

Acne vulgaris is the most common type of acne and is a common skin problem that affects people of all ages, particularly adolescents. It happens when hair follicles become clogged with oil, dead skin cells, and germs, resulting in the production of numerous skin lesions. Milder comedones (whiteheads and blackheads) and more inflammatory papules, pustules, nodules, and cysts are examples of these lesions. Acne vulgaris most commonly affects the face, neck, chest, shoulders, and back, which have a high density of sebaceous glands. Its intensity can range from mild, consisting largely of comedones, to moderate to severe, with a greater number of inflammatory lesions and deep, painful nodules and cysts.

Cystic Acne: Deep, Painful Breakout

Cystic acne is a severe form of acne vulgaris that causes painful, inflammatory outbreaks. It's one of the most severe and potentially damaging acne lesions. Cystic acne occurs when the hair follicles become clogged with dead skin cells, oil (sebum), and bacteria, causing big, red or flesh-colored, painful nodules or cysts to form beneath the skin's surface. The following characteristics distinguish cystic acne: 

  1. Deep, Painful Lesions: Cystic acne lesions are deep within the skin and are frequently larger than papules and pustules. They can be extremely painful due to the inflammation and pressure on the surrounding tissues.

  2. Inflammation and Redness: The affected area may become red, swollen, and warm to the touch due to the intense inflammatory response.

  3. Pus-filled Cysts: Cystic acne lesions are typically filled with pus, which consists of dead skin cells, bacteria, and inflammatory substances.

  4. Tendency to Scar: Cystic acne has a higher risk of causing scarring compared to milder forms of acne. The inflammation and deep nature of the lesions can lead to permanent tissue damage if not treated properly.

Hormonal Acne: Influenced by Fluctuating Hormones

Hormonal acne, as the name implies, is a type of acne that is influenced by fluctuations in hormone levels, primarily androgens (male hormones) and oestrogen and progesterone (female hormones). It is most common during puberty, the menstrual cycle, pregnancy, and menopause, when hormone levels are naturally changing.

Here are some of the key characteristics of hormonal acne:

  1. Time: Hormonal acne frequently manifests in predictable patterns linked to hormonal changes. For example, it may worsen before or during menstruation (premenstrual acne), during pregnancy, or when beginning or discontinuing hormonal birth control methods.

  2. Location: It primarily affects the lower face (jawline, chin, and cheeks), occasionally the neck, and the back because of the higher density of oil glands there.

  3. Type of Lesions: Comedones (whiteheads and blackheads) as well as more inflammatory lesions like papules, pustules, nodules, and cysts can be present in hormonal acne.

  4. Severity: Hormonal acne can occasionally be more severe and difficult to treat, especially in people with hormonal imbalances or conditions like polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS).

Acne as a Reflection of Internal Health

Acne can be a reflection of internal health because a variety of internal factors can contribute to its development. While acne is primarily a skin disorder, certain underlying health issues and imbalances can aggravate or trigger acne breakouts. Here are some examples of how acne can be linked to internal health:

  1. Hormonal Imbalances: Hormones are important in the development of acne. Androgens, which are male hormones found in both men and women, can stimulate the sebaceous glands to produce more oil, resulting in clogged pores. Hormone fluctuations during puberty, menstrual cycles, pregnancy, and menopause can all contribute to acne breakouts. Conditions like polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) can also cause hormonal imbalances and worsen acne.

  2. Stress and Mental Health: Excessive stress can cause hormonal changes in the body, including an increase in cortisol (the stress hormone). Elevated cortisol levels may stimulate the sebaceous glands and lead to increased oil production, contributing to acne development or exacerbation. Additionally, emotional stress can sometimes lead to behaviors like touching or picking at the skin, which can worsen acne.

  3. Digestive Health: The gut and skin are connected, and some studies suggest that imbalances in the gut microbiome and digestive issues may be linked to acne development. Poor digestive health and inflammation in the gut can potentially affect the skin's condition.

  4. Dietary Factors: While the direct relationship between diet and acne is complex and not fully understood, some studies have indicated that certain dietary factors may influence acne development. Diets high in refined sugars, unhealthy fats, and dairy products have been associated with an increased risk of acne in some individuals.

  5. Medications and Supplements: Some medications and supplements may have acne as a side effect. For example, certain corticosteroids, anticonvulsants, and hormonal treatments can contribute to acne breakouts.

 Acne and Hormonal Imbalance: Understanding the Connection

The link between acne and hormonal imbalance is significant, as hormones play an important role in acne development.  Hormonal imbalances can cause an increase in sebum (oil) production, changes in the natural shedding process of the skin, and inflammation, all of which contribute to the formation of acne lesions. The main hormones involved in acne development are androgens (male hormones), estrogen, and progesterone (female hormones).

Here are some examples of how hormonal imbalances can contribute to acne:

  1. Androgens and Sebum Production: Androgens, such as testosterone, are found in both males and females, but males have a higher concentration. These hormones cause the skin's sebaceous glands to produce sebum. Sebum production can increase when there is an excess of androgens or if the skin is particularly sensitive to them, resulting in oily skin and clogged pores.

  2. Androgens and Follicular Keratinization: Androgens can also affect the behavior of skin cells known as keratinocytes. In the presence of elevated androgens, these cells can stick together and block the hair follicles, resulting in the formation of comedones (whiteheads and blackheads).

  3. Estrogen and Progesterone: While androgens can promote acne development, estrogen and progesterone can help counterbalance their effects. These hormones have the ability to reduce sebum production while also promoting a more stable skin environment. During certain phases of the menstrual cycle, estrogen levels decrease, and this hormonal shift can trigger acne breakouts in some women.

  4. Puberty: Hormonal changes during puberty, particularly an increase in androgen levels, frequently result in the onset of acne. The sebaceous glands become more active, resulting in oilier skin and a greater chance of clogged pores.

  5. Menstrual Cycle: Premenstrual acne can occur in women due to fluctuations in oestrogen and progesterone levels during their menstrual cycle.  The decrease in oestrogen before menstruation can increase sebum production and cause acne breakouts.

  6. Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS): PCOS is a hormonal disorder that causes androgen and insulin imbalances. These imbalances can contribute to more severe and persistent acne in those who suffer from it.

Diet and Acne: How Food Choices Impact Skin Health

Diet can affect both the development of acne and the health of your skin generally. Some food choices and dietary patterns have been associated with an increased risk of acne or an exacerbation of existing acne, despite the fact that the relationship between diet and acne is complex and individualized. Here are some instances where diet may have an impact on acne and the health of the skin:

  1. High Glycemic Index Foods: Foods with a high glycemic index (GI) can cause rapid spikes in blood sugar levels, leading to an increase in insulin production. Elevated insulin levels may cause the release of other hormones, which can increase sebum production and contribute to acne development. Sugary snacks, white bread, white rice, and sugary beverages all have a high GI.

  2. Dairy Products: Some research suggests that dairy consumption, particularly skim milk, may be linked to acne. Certain milk components, such as hormones or growth factors, are thought to contribute to acne formation. More research, however, is required to demonstrate a convincing link.

  3. Saturated and trans-fat-rich foods: A diet high in unhealthy fats, such as those found in fried foods and processed snacks, may promote inflammation in the body, potentially exacerbating acne.

  4. Omega-6 Fatty Acids: Omega-6 fatty acids are essential fats, but excessive consumption in comparison to omega-3 fatty acids may contribute to inflammation. A healthy omega-6 to omega-3 fatty acid ratio is critical for overall health, including skin health.

  5. Chocolate and Junk Foods: While the evidence is not conclusive, some studies have suggested that eating a lot of chocolate or junk food may cause acne flare-ups in some people.

Some dietary choices, on the other hand, may promote skin health and aid in acne management: 

  1. Low-Glycemic Index (GI) Foods: Low-GI foods, such as whole grains, fruits, vegetables, and legumes, are less likely to cause blood sugar and insulin spikes. These options may be better for skin health.

  2. Antioxidant-Rich Foods: Antioxidants help combat oxidative stress and inflammation, which can be beneficial to skin health. Include a variety of colorful fruits and vegetables, nuts, and seeds in your diet.

  3. Omega-3 Fatty Acids: Foods high in omega-3 fatty acids, such as fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines), flaxseeds, and chia seeds, have anti-inflammatory properties that may aid in acne management.

  4. Hydration: Staying hydrated is essential for overall health, including skin health. Drinking plenty of water keeps the skin hydrated and may contribute to a clearer complexion.

The Role of Stress in Acne Breakouts

Stress can contribute to acne breakouts and exacerbate existing acne. The relationship between stress and acne is complex, involving numerous physiological and hormonal components. When you are stressed, your body releases hormones such as cortisol and adrenaline, which can influence several bodily processes, including those associated with the skin. Here's how stress affects acne:

  1. Increased Sebum Production: Stress causes the release of hormones, particularly cortisol, which can stimulate the sebaceous glands in the skin to produce more sebum (oil). Excess sebum can clog pores, creating an environment favorable to acne formation.

  2. Inflammation: Anxiety and stress can cause inflammation in the body. Inflammatory responses can aggravate existing acne lesions and promote the formation of new inflammatory acne lesions such as papules and pustules.

  3. Skin Barrier Function: Chronic stress can compromise the skin's natural barrier function, making it more vulnerable to irritants and bacteria that can cause acne breakouts.

  4. Skin Picking and Touching: During times of stress, some individuals may engage in behaviors like picking at their skin or touching their face more frequently. These activities can introduce bacteria into the skin and aggravate acne.

  5. Hormonal Influence: Stress has the ability to alter hormone levels, including sex hormones, which are known to contribute to the emergence of acne. Increased stress, for instance, may throw off the equilibrium of hormones like progesterone and estrogen, which may result in flare-ups of hormonal acne.

It is critical to effectively manage stress in order to reduce its impact on acne and overall skin health. Here are some stress-reduction strategies that may help with acne: 

  • Practice Stress-Reducing Techniques: Engage in stress-reduction techniques such as deep breathing, meditation, yoga, or mindfulness.

  • Regular Exercise: Physical activity can help reduce stress and promote overall well-being, which may benefit acne.

  • Adequate Sleep: Getting enough quality sleep is essential for stress management and skin health.

  • Healthy Eating: Consume a well-balanced diet rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and lean proteins, as proper nutrition can help your body cope with stress.

  • Skincare Routine: Follow a gentle skincare routine appropriate for your skin type and avoid harsh products that may irritate the skin further.

  • Avoid Triggers: Whenever possible, identify and avoid stress-inducing triggers.

  • Seek Help: If you are experiencing chronic stress or have difficulty coping with stressors, talk to friends, family, or a mental health professional.

Identifying underlying health concerns that may be contributing to acne might help you establish an effective treatment plan and manage the condition more effectively. While acne is largely a skin disorder, certain interior health conditions or imbalances can increase or complicate acne treatment. When attempting to uncover potential underlying health causes for acne, consider the following factors:

  1. Hormonal Imbalances: Hormonal fluctuations, particularly changes in androgen levels, can influence sebum production and the development of acne. During puberty, the menstrual cycle, pregnancy, and menopause, hormonal imbalances can occur. Conditions like polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) can also cause hormonal acne.

  2. Stress and Mental Health: Stress can cause hormonal changes in the body, which can result in increased sebum production and acne flare-ups. Furthermore, stress can cause certain behaviors such as touching or picking at the skin, which can aggravate acne.

  3. Gut Health: The gut and the skin are inextricably linked, and imbalances in the gut microbiome may contribute to inflammation and acne development. Digestive issues or conditions such as leaky gut syndrome can have an impact on overall skin health.

Acne and Digestive Health: Gut- Skin Connection

The gut-skin connection is the link between digestive health (the gut) and skin conditions such as acne. Emerging research suggests that the health and balance of the gut microbiome can influence various aspects of skin health, including the development and severity of acne. Several mechanisms could explain how gut health affects skin:

  1. Inflammation: Imbalances in the gut microbiome can cause the body to become more inflammatory. Chronic inflammation can cause the release of inflammatory molecules, which can aggravate skin conditions such as acne.

  2. Leaky Gut Syndrome: A weakened gut barrier, also known as leaky gut syndrome, can allow harmful substances, toxins, and bacteria to enter the bloodstream. This could cause systemic inflammation and possibly affect the skin.

  3. Microbial Imbalance: The gut is home to a diverse community of microorganisms that play an important role in gut health. An imbalance in the gut microbiota (dysbiosis) can affect the production of certain compounds, such as short-chain fatty acids, which may have an impact on skin health.

  4. Hormonal Regulation: The gut microbiome can influence the metabolism and elimination of hormones, including androgens, which are involved in acne development. Changes in hormone levels can affect sebum production and contribute to acne breakouts.

  5. Nutrient Absorption: A healthy gut is essential for proper nutrient absorption. Nutrient deficiencies can impact skin health, and inadequate intake of certain vitamins and minerals may contribute to acne.

  6. Immune System Interaction: The gut is closely linked to the immune system. An imbalanced gut microbiome may lead to immune system dysregulation, potentially affecting the skin's immune response.

Consider the following steps to promote a healthy gut-skin connection and potentially improve acne:

  1. Balanced Diet: Consume a well-balanced diet high in fibre, fruits and vegetables, and fermented foods to promote a diverse and healthy gut microbiome.

  2. Probiotics and prebiotics: To support a healthy gut microbiome, think about including probiotics (good bacteria) and prebiotics (food for good bacteria) in your diet or as supplements.

  3. Avoid Triggers: Recognise and steer clear of any foods that may exacerbate gut inflammation and acne. 

  4. Hydration: Drink a lot of water to support digestion and help keep your gut healthy.

  5. Stress management: Prolonged stress can harm gut health.  Practice stress-reduction techniques such as meditation, yoga, or exercise.

  6. Skincare Routine: Maintain a gentle skincare routine tailored to your skin type to avoid further irritation.

  7. Professional Guidance: If you suspect gut health issues or have persistent acne concerns, consult with a healthcare professional, such as a dermatologist or gastroenterologist, for a comprehensive evaluation and personalized advice.

Skincare Routine for Different Types of Acne

Acne skincare should be tailored to the specific type of acne as well as your skin type. Different approaches are needed for various forms of acne. Here is a general rule for skincare regimens based on the various types of acne:

  1. Mild Acne (Whiteheads and Blackheads):

  • Cleanse: Use a mild cleanser twice daily to get rid of impurities, extra oil, and dirt.

  • Exfoliate: To unclog pores and encourage cell turnover, use a chemical exfoliant with salicylic acid or glycolic acid two to three times per week.

  • Spot Treatment: Apply a benzoyl peroxide- or tea-tree-oil-containing product directly to each whitehead or blackhead.

  • Moisturize: Use a non-comedogenic, oil-free moisturizer to keep your skin balanced.

  • Sunscreen: Use a broad-spectrum sunscreen with an SPF of at least 30 to protect your skin.

  1. Inflammatory Acne (Papules and Pustules):

  • Cleanse: Use a gentle cleanser twice daily to keep the skin clean without irritating it further.

  • Spot Treatment: Apply a benzoyl peroxide or salicylic acid spot treatment to inflamed areas.

  • Moisturize: Use a lightweight, non-comedogenic moisturizer to prevent excessive dryness.

  • Sunscreen: Use a broad-spectrum sunscreen with an SPF of at least 30 to protect your skin.

  • Avoid Irritants: Avoid harsh, fragranced, or alcohol-based products that can aggravate inflammation.

  1. Cystic Acne:

  • Cleanse: Use a mild cleanser twice daily without scrubbing too vigorously.

  • Spot Treatment: Apply an over-the-counter or prescription-strength product with benzoyl peroxide or salicylic acid to affected areas.

  • Moisturize: Use an oil-free, non-comedogenic moisturizer to keep your skin hydrated.

  • Avoid Touching: Do not pick or squeeze cystic acne, as it can lead to scarring and worsen inflammation.

  • Seek Professional Help: Consider seeing a dermatologist for more aggressive treatment options such as oral antibiotics or prescription topical medications.

  1. Hormonal Acne:

  • Cleanse: Use a gentle cleanser twice daily to maintain clean skin without over-drying.

  • Spot Treatment: Apply a product with benzoyl peroxide or salicylic acid to individual breakouts.

  • Moisturize: Use an oil-free, non-comedogenic moisturizer to balance skin hydration.

  • Sunscreen: Protect your skin with a broad-spectrum sunscreen with at least SPF 30.

  • Consider Hormonal Management: If hormonal fluctuations are a significant factor, consult a healthcare professional about hormone-regulating treatments such as oral contraceptives or spironolactone.

Preventing Acne Scars and Post-Inflammatory Hyperpigmentation

Acne scar prevention and post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) necessitate meticulous acne control as well as a few additional skincare practices. Early acne treatment, avoiding picking and popping acne, utilizing acne-fighting chemicals, sun protection, a mild skincare regimen, vitamin C serum, topical niacinamide, chemical peels, and microdermabrasion, not overdoing it, and patience are some ways to help prevent these skin troubles. Consider seeing a dermatologist if you already have acne scars or severe PIH that aren't improving with over-the-counter medicines. They can offer you customized treatment alternatives, such as laser therapy, micro needling, or prescription drugs, to address your individual skin conditions.

Professional and Natural Treatments for Acne

Professional and natural acne treatments can both be effective, depending on the severity of the acne and the individual's preferences. Here are some possibilities:

Professional Treatments:

  1. Prescription Topicals: A dermatologist can prescribe stronger topical treatments to target acne more aggressively, such as topical antibiotics, retinoids, or combination therapies.

  2. Oral Medications: In severe cases of acne, oral antibiotics or oral contraceptives (for females) can be prescribed to help control inflammation and hormone-related acne.

  3. Isotretinoin (Accutane): This potent oral medication is reserved for severe, treatment-resistant acne. It works by reducing oil production and can provide long-term results. However, it has serious side effects and must be closely monitored by a dermatologist.

  4. Chemical Peels: A chemical solution is applied to the skin, causing it to exfoliate and peel away, revealing fresher, smoother skin. Chemical peels can help clear up acne and reduce the visibility of acne scars and PIH.

  5. Laser and light therapies: Laser treatments, photodynamic therapy, and intense pulsed light (IPL) can target acne-causing bacteria, reduce inflammation, and promote collagen production to improve skin texture and tone.

  6. Microneedling: This procedure involves the use of fine needles to create controlled micro-injuries in the skin, which stimulates collagen production and improves the appearance of acne scars.

Natural Treatments:

  1. Tea Tree Oil: Tea tree oil, a natural antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory agent, can be applied topically to individual pimples to help reduce redness and inflammation.

  2. Honey and Cinnamon Mask: A honey and cinnamon face mask can be used to soothe and reduce acne-related inflammation.

  3. Aloe Vera Gel: Aloe vera has anti-inflammatory properties and can be used to soothe and reduce redness in acne-prone areas.

  4. Green Tea Extract: Green tea contains antioxidants and anti-inflammatory compounds that, when applied topically or consumed as a beverage, may help reduce acne.

  5. Zinc Supplements: Zinc has anti-inflammatory properties and may aid in acne management when taken as a dietary supplement.

  6. Apple Cider Vinegar: Diluted apple cider vinegar can be used as a toner to help balance the pH of the skin and reduce acne-causing bacteria.

While natural treatments may be beneficial for some people, they may not be as effective as prescription medications and professional treatments, especially in cases of severe acne. If you are considering natural treatments, you must first patch test to ensure that you do not have any adverse reactions. Before attempting any new treatments, consult with a dermatologist. They can give you personalised advice based on your skin type and the severity of your acne, assisting you in selecting the most effective treatment plan.
