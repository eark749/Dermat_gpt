Lysine, an important amino acid, is frequently recommended for its possible use in acne treatment due to its role in collagen production and tissue repair. In this blog, we'll look at why dermatologists don't recommend Lysine for acne.
Understanding Lysine: Its Role in Acne Treatment
Lysine has been shown in studies to help create collagen in the skin. Collagen is the structure that gives your skin flexibility and suppleness. Given these benefits, it's natural to question what lysine might do for
acne
.
The Science Behind Lysine and Acne: What Research Says
Present scientific research does not support the use of lysine as an acne treatment on its own, even though it is necessary for the formation of collagen and may have antiviral properties.
Dermatologists
frequently recommend evidence-based therapies such as topical retinoids, benzoyl peroxide, salicylic acid, and oral antibiotics to treat acne and reduce scarring.

Lysine Myths: Debunking Common Misconceptions
Let's debunk some common myths:
Myth
: Lysine supplements are essential for everyone.
Truth
: Most people get enough lysine from their diets and do not need to take supplements. Supplementation can help people who do not get enough lysine in their diet. Some people may benefit from taking lysine supplements, such as those who have recurrent herpes outbreaks or suffer from anxiety.
Myth
: Lysine can heal cold sores.
Truth
: Several studies have found that eating lysine regularly may help prevent cold sore outbreaks. Others have demonstrated that it can prevent cold sores and help them heal faster. According to one study, lysine relieves symptoms, reduces healing time, and prevents cold sores from recurring.
Myth
: Excessive lysine consumption has no side effects.
Truth
: Most people can consume up to 3 grams (g) of lysine per day without experiencing any negative side effects. Increasing lysine intake to 10-15 g per day may result in digestive issues such as diarrhoea and nausea.
Myth
: Lysine is sufficient for muscle growth.
Truth
: Lysine is a vital amino acid that supports muscle growth, recovery, and protein synthesis. It is therefore a useful ally for athletes who want to improve their performance.
Myth
: Antiviral medications can be substituted with lysine.
Truth
: Lysine may help with viral infection symptoms such as cold sores, but it cannot replace antiviral medications. Effective management of viral infections requires proper medical treatment.
Myth
: All lysine supplements are the same.
Truth
: The quality of lysine supplements varies. To ensure purity and effectiveness, choose supplements from reputable brands that use good manufacturing practices.
Myth
: Lysine is only important in children and adolescents.
Truth
: One of the 12 essential amino acids in the body, lysine helps the body absorb calcium and stimulates the production of
collagen
. This amino acid helps young children develop their eating habits, speeds up the metabolism of nutrients, and supports the growth of their height.
The Risks and Side Effects of Using Lysine for Acne
Lysine, an essential amino acid, is occasionally used as an acne treatment supplement. While it may be beneficial to some people, there are potential risks and side effects to be aware of.
Digestive Problems
: Lysine in high doses can cause nausea, diarrhea, and stomach cramps.
Kidney Issues
: Lysine supplements should be avoided by people who already have kidney problems because they can exacerbate them.
Interaction with Medications
: Lysine can increase calcium absorption, which may result in hypercalcemia if combined with calcium supplements. Lysine may interact with specific antibiotics, such as aminoglycosides, increasing their toxicity.
Allergic Reactions
: Some people may develop allergic reactions, such as rash, itching, or swelling.
Long-Term Use
: There is little long-term safety information on the use of lysine supplements for acne. It is not recommended to use chronically without medical supervision.
Lysine Dosage and Safety: What You Need to Know

Natural Remedies for Acne: Options That Are Safe for Your Skin
Natural acne remedies can be more effective and gentler than traditional treatments. Here are some safe and generally recommended options:
Tea tree oil
: Tea tree oil has natural antibacterial and anti-inflammatory properties and may kill P. acnes, the acne-causing bacteria. Tea tree oil has anti-inflammatory properties, so it can help reduce pimple swelling and redness.
Apple cider vinegar
: Although there is not enough scientific evidence to back up its efficacy, apple cider vinegar is a well-liked natural acne remedy. Citric acid in apple cider vinegar may help kill P.acnes bacteria when combined with other treatments, and additional research suggests that topical application may help reduce acne scarring.
Argan oil
: Argan oil is noncomedogenic, which helps to balance sebum production on oily skin while promoting moisture on
dry skin
. Argan oil may also alleviate skin inflammation and improve the appearance of acne scars.
Jojoba oil
: Extracted from the seeds of the jojoba shrub, jojoba oil is a naturally occurring waxy substance. Some of the compounds in jojoba oil may help to reduce skin inflammation, including redness and swelling around pimples, whiteheads, and other inflamed lesions.
Aloe vera
: Aloe vera is a natural antibacterial and anti-inflammatory, so it may help to reduce the appearance of acne and prevent breakouts as part of a treatment regimen.
Honey
: Honey has been used for thousands of years to treat skin conditions because it contains many
antioxidants
that can help clear clogged pores. However, there is evidence that honey has specific antimicrobial properties.
Zinc
: People often claim that zinc can reduce acne lesions and redness because of its anti-inflammatory qualities. People can use zinc-containing ointments and creams or zinc supplements.
Green tea
: Green tea has high concentrations of catechins, a class of antioxidant polyphenols. Some acne sufferers have inadequate antioxidants and excessive sebum, or natural body oils, in their pores. Antioxidants assist the body in eliminating waste materials and chemicals that may harm healthy cells. In addition, green tea contains chemicals that may lessen inflammation, P. acnes, and the production of sebum on the skin. One can apply green tea extract topically or drink green tea, though current research on these two methods is limited, according to researchers.
Echinacea
: Also known as purple coneflower, echinacea may include substances that aid in destroying bacteria and viruses, including P. acnes. Many people think that echinacea can help prevent or treat infections, such as colds and the flu, by boosting immunity and reducing inflammation. There is not much research available right now, but there has been some historical proof that echinacea can reverse bacterial inflammation and prevent P. acnes from spreading.
Rosemary
: Rosmarinus officinalis, the plant used to extract rosemary, has substances that have anti-inflammatory, antibacterial, and antioxidant qualities. Its efficacy needs to be determined by more research.
Coconut oil
: Coconut oil has antibacterial and anti-inflammatory qualities, just like other natural remedies. These characteristics imply that coconut oil may lessen the redness and swelling associated with pimples and aid in the removal of bacteria that cause acne. Coconut oil's calming and moisturizing properties may help speed up the healing of open acne lesions. However, there has been little focused research on the effectiveness of coconut oil as an acne treatment.
