The Consequences of Deficiency: Folic Acid and Hair Loss
Folic acid deficiency can contribute to hair loss, which is known as telogen effluvium, a type of non-scarring hair loss. Telogen effluvium occurs when the regular hair growth cycle is disrupted, resulting in excessive hair shedding. Folic acid, being a necessary nutrient for cell division, DNA synthesis, and hair follicle health, can help prevent this form of hair loss. Folic acid deficiency can contribute to hair loss in the following ways:
Cell Division Impairment
: Folic acid deficiency impairs the rapid cell division required for hair follicle growth. As a result, hair follicles may enter the resting phase (telogen phase) too soon, resulting in hair loss.
Weakened Hair Follicles
: A lack of folic acid can weaken hair follicles, making them more prone to shedding. Weak follicles can result in thinner, weaker hair strands.
Slowed Hair Growth
: Folic acid promotes active hair growth during the anagen phase of the hair growth cycle. A deficiency can cause this phase to be disrupted, resulting in slower hair growth.
Reduced Nutrient Delivery
: A lack of folic acid can impair blood circulation to the scalp, impairing the delivery of essential nutrients and oxygen to hair follicles. This can aggravate follicle weakness and contribute to hair loss.
Hair Thinning and Density
: As more hair follicles enter the resting phase and shed hair, the overall density of hair on the scalp can decrease, resulting in visible thinning.
It's crucial to remember that, while folic acid deficiency can contribute to hair loss, other factors can as well. Genetics, hormonal imbalances, medical disorders, drugs, and lifestyle factors are among these. If you're having hair loss, you should see a doctor or a dermatologist to figure out what's causing it and get the right therapy.

Folic Acid Supplements: Things to Consider
Folic acid supplements may be advantageous for people who are deficient in folic acid or have difficulties fulfilling their daily folic acid requirements from food sources alone. However, there are a few things to think about before using folic acid supplements:
Consult a Healthcare Professional
: Before beginning any supplement, including folic acid, it is critical to consult a healthcare professional, such as a doctor or registered dietitian. They can assess your individual health status, medical history, and specific needs to determine if a supplement is appropriate for you.
Dosing and timing
: The recommended daily allowance (RDA) for folic acid varies according to age, gender, and life stage. Your healthcare provider can guide you on the appropriate dosage and timing based on your specific requirements.
Underlying Conditions
: Some medical conditions and medications can interfere with folic acid absorption or utilisation. It's important to discuss your health history with your healthcare provider to ensure that folic acid supplementation won't interact negatively with any existing conditions or medications.
Supplement Quality
: Select a reputable brand of folic acid supplements to ensure a high-quality product. Look for supplements that are third-party tested for quality and purity.
Interactions:
Certain drugs, such as anticonvulsants and methotrexate, can interact with folic acid supplementation. To avoid potential interactions, inform your healthcare provider about all medications and supplements you are taking.
Folate vs. Folic Acid:
Some people may have difficulty metabolising folic acid due to genetic variations. In such cases, a supplement containing a different form of folate, such as methylfolate, may be more suitable. Your healthcare provider can advise on the best form for you.
Balanced Nutrition
: Supplements should supplement, not replace, a healthy diet. It is still critical to prioritise a diet high in folic acid and other nutrients.
Considerations for Pregnancy
: Pregnant women frequently require higher levels of folic acid to prevent neural tube defects in developing embryos. If you're pregnant or planning to become pregnant, discuss your folic acid needs with your healthcare provider.
Monitoring and Adjustments
: If you take folic acid supplements, your healthcare provider may recommend periodic blood tests to monitor your folic acid levels and make any necessary supplement adjustments.
Long-Term Use
: While folic acid is generally safe for most people when taken in the recommended dosages, it is critical not to exceed those dosages without consulting a doctor. Long-term excessive intake of folic acid supplements can mask vitamin B12 deficiency.
