Hormonal Fluctuations and Their Impact on Hair
Hormonal fluctuations have a significant impact on hair health, and imbalances can cause a variety of changes, including
hair loss
or thinning. Here's how hormone fluctuations affect hair:
Hormones and Hair Growth Cycle
:
Estrogen
: Estrogen is known to
promote hair growth
and maintain hair in the growth phase (anagen). Higher estrogen levels during pregnancy, for example, can result in thicker, fuller hair. However, fluctuations in estrogen levels, such as during the menstrual cycle or menopause, can lead to changes in hair growth patterns. A drop in estrogen levels may contribute to hair shedding.
Progesterone
:
Progesterone, another female sex hormone, also influences hair growth. It rises during the luteal phase of the menstrual cycle and drops before menstruation. Progesterone can affect the length and thickness of hair follicles. Changes in progesterone levels may contribute to hair loss during specific phases of the menstrual cycle.
Androgens (Testosterone and DHT)
: Often associated with men, androgens are also found in females and can influence hair development. The enzyme 5-alpha reductase can convert testosterone into dihydrotestosterone (DHT). DHT is linked to the shrinkage of hair follicles, which can result in hair loss and thinning, especially in people who are genetically predisposed to it (androgenetic alopecia). In females, conditions such as polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS) can cause increased amounts of androgen, which can contribute to hirsutism, or excessive hair growth.
Impact on Hair Growth
:
Telogen Effluvium:
Hormonal fluctuations, stress, illness, or nutritional deficiencies can disrupt the hair growth cycle, leading to a condition called telogen effluvium. Telogen effluvium results in excessive shedding of hair, often a few months after the triggering event. Hormonal changes, such as those during pregnancy or postpartum, can be a common trigger.
Pattern Hair Loss (Androgenetic Alopecia)
:
Androgenetic alopecia is the most common form of hair loss in both men and women, characterised by a progressive
thinning of hair
, typically at the crown or temples. Genetic predisposition and hormonal factors, including androgen levels and sensitivity to DHT, play a significant role in the development of pattern hair loss.
Identifying Signs of Period-Related Hair Loss
Identifying signs of period-related hair loss can help people understand when hormonal fluctuations during the menstrual cycle are affecting their hair health. Some signs to look for include increased shedding, thinning or sparse areas, changes in hair texture, scalp sensitivity, a shorter hair growth cycle, hairline changes, excessive shedding while showering or brushing, visible scalp, changes in hair density, and stress or emotional triggers. It is important to note that some degree of hair shedding is normal, with most people losing between 50 and 100 hairs per day. However, if you notice significant changes in hair density or shedding patterns that last beyond the menstrual cycle, you should see a healthcare provider or dermatologist for a thorough evaluation and personalised management strategies. They can help determine whether the hair loss is due to hormonal fluctuations during menstruation or if there are other underlying causes.

The Role of Vitamins and Minerals in Hair Strength
Vitamins and minerals are essential for keeping hair strong and healthy. Here's how various nutrients contribute to the strength and vitality of your hair:
Biotin (Vitamin B7)
: Biotin is required for the synthesis of keratin, a protein that forms the structure of hair strands. Adequate biotin levels promote healthy hair growth and may reduce breakage and brittleness. Eggs, nuts, seeds, salmon, avocados, and sweet potatoes are rich in biotin.
Vitamin C
: Vitamin C acts as an antioxidant, protecting hair follicles from free radical damage. It promotes collagen production, which is necessary for hair strength and flexibility. Citrus fruits, strawberries, kiwi, bell peppers, and broccoli are excellent sources of vitamin C.
Vitamin E
: Vitamin E contains antioxidants, which protect hair cells from oxidative stress and damage. It improves scalp circulation, increases nutrient delivery to hair follicles, and promotes healthy hair growth. Nuts, seeds, spinach, kale, and sunflower oil are rich in vitamin E.
Vitamin A
: Vitamin A is required for the formation of sebum, a natural oil that moisturises the scalp and keeps hair strands lubricated. Adequate vitamin A levels prevent scalp dryness and flakiness, which reduces the risk of hair breakage. Sweet potatoes, carrots, spinach, kale, and liver are high in vitamin A.
Vitamin D
: Vitamin D regulates hair follicle cycling and may promote hair growth. Adequate vitamin D levels promote healthy hair follicle function and may reduce hair loss. Vitamin D can be obtained through fatty fish, fortified dairy products, eggs, and sunlight exposure.
Iron
: Iron is necessary for transporting oxygen to the hair follicles, which supports their function and promotes hair growth. Adequate iron intake prevents hair thinning and promotes strong, healthy hair. Red meat, poultry, fish, lentils, spinach, and fortified cereals are all sources of iron.
Zinc
: Zinc is involved in DNA and protein synthesis, including the production of keratin, which is essential for hair strength. Adequate zinc levels promote healthy hair growth and may reduce hair loss. Zinc-rich foods include oysters, beef, pumpkin seeds, lentils, and chickpeas.
Omega-3 Fatty Acids
: These fatty acids nourish the scalp and hair follicles, reducing inflammation and promoting healthy hair growth. They help maintain scalp health, prevent dryness, and reduce hair breakage. Fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines), flaxseeds, chia seeds, and walnuts are rich in omega-3 fatty acids.

Hormonal Treatments and Their Efficacy
Hormonal treatments can be effective in treating hormonal imbalances that cause hair loss, such as menstrual loss. Here are some common hormonal treatments and their efficacy in treating hair loss:
Oral Contraceptives (Birth Control Pills)
: Some oral contraceptives containing oestrogen and progestin can help regulate hormonal fluctuations and reduce androgen levels, which may contribute to hair loss in some people. Oral contraceptives are often prescribed for women with androgenetic alopecia or other hair loss conditions exacerbated by hormonal imbalances. They can help slow down hair loss and promote hair regrowth in some cases.
Anti-Androgen Medications
: Anti-androgen medications, such as spironolactone, work by blocking the effects of androgens (male hormones) on the hair follicles, thereby reducing hair loss and promoting hair growth. Spironolactone is commonly prescribed for women with androgenetic alopecia or hair loss conditions characterised by excessive androgen activity. It can be effective in slowing down hair loss and stimulating hair regrowth, particularly in cases of female pattern hair loss.
Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT)
:
Hormone replacement therapy involves supplementing estrogen and/or progestin to balance hormone levels in women experiencing menopausal or postmenopausal symptoms. HRT may help improve hair loss associated with menopause-related hormonal changes, such as thinning hair or hair loss at the crown. However, the efficacy of HRT for hair loss varies among individuals, and it may not be suitable for everyone due to potential side effects and risks.
Topical Hormonal Treatments
:
Topical formulations containing hormones such as estrogen or progesterone may be applied directly to the scalp to target hair follicles and regulate hormone levels locally. While topical hormonal treatments are less commonly used than oral medications, they may offer localised benefits for certain individuals with hormone-related hair loss. However, more research is needed to determine their overall efficacy and safety.
Nutritional Supplements
: Certain nutritional supplements, such as vitamin D, vitamin B complex, and omega-3 fatty acids, may help support hormonal balance and improve overall hair health. While nutritional supplements alone may not directly address hormonal imbalances, they can complement other treatments for hair loss and contribute to overall hair health and growth.
