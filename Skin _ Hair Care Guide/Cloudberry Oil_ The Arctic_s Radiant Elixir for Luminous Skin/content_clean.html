Rubus chamaemorus, the scientific name for cloudberry, has long been associated with the arctic regions where it thrives, particularly in Scandinavia, Russia, and some parts of North America. The following are some myths and conventional uses for cloudberry:

  1. Indigenous Peoples: Indigenous communities in the Arctic and subarctic regions have a long history of using cloudberry. They saw it as a valuable source of nutrition and sustenance in their diets.

  2. Nutrition and Survival: Cloudberry was an important food source in many northern regions, providing essential vitamins and nutrients, particularly vitamin C, which helped prevent scurvy during the long, harsh winters.

  3. Medicinal Uses: Cloudberry has a long history of use for its medicinal properties. Indigenous peoples used cloudberry leaves, stems, and roots to make herbal remedies for a variety of ailments.

  4. Folklore and Legends: Cloudberry has been linked to folklore and legends in some cultures. Cloudberry, for example, was thought to be the favorite food of the will-o'-the-wisp, a mysterious and mischievous spirit in Finnish mythology.

  5. Traditional Dishes: Cloudberry is a popular ingredient in northern cuisine. It can be found in jams, preserves, desserts, and even alcoholic beverages such as cloudberry liqueur.

  6. Symbol of Resilience: Cloudberry is often regarded as a symbol of resilience due to its ability to survive and thrive in harsh Arctic conditions such as cold temperatures and a short growing season.

  7. Cultural Celebrations: Cloudberry picking is a popular summer activity in some Scandinavian cultures. Families and communities come together to gather cloudberry, which is then used in festive meals and celebrations. 

  8. Commercial Harvesting: Cloudberry has also been commercially harvested for its fruit, particularly in Finland, Norway, and Sweden, where it is sought after for its unique flavor and nutritional value.

Cloudberries continue to play a significant role in northern cultures and cuisines today.
