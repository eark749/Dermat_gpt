The Origins and Science of Azulene
Azulene is an intriguing chemical noted for its vibrant blue color and therapeutic benefits in skincare. In this blog, we will look at its origins and the scientific basis for its distinctive qualities.
Azulene is a bicyclic sesquiterpene molecule with a distinctive molecular structure. It is made up of a fused five- and seven-membered ring structure, which helps to maintain its stability and biological activity.
Matricin is a colorless chemical found naturally in chamomile. When chamomile flowers are steam distilled, matricin undergoes a cyclization reaction that produces chamazulene (and later azulene) as a result of water release and atom rearrangement.
The blue color of azulene is not purely cosmetic; it also indicates its chemical qualities. Azulene has significant anti-inflammatory properties because it inhibits enzymes involved in the inflammatory process, including cyclooxygenase (COX) and lipoxygenase (LOX). This exercise reduces redness, swelling, and irritation in the skin.
Azulene is relatively stable under normal conditions, but it is susceptible to heat, light, and pH changes. It is slightly soluble in water but more soluble in organic solvents such as oils and alcohol, making it appropriate for use in cosmetics formulations.
