Mouth discoloration can be caused by a variety of circumstances, and therapy is based on determining the underlying cause. In this blog, we'll look at some common reasons and remedies for mouth discoloration.
What Causes Discoloration Around the Mouth?
Discoloration of the mouth can be caused by a variety of circumstances, ranging from benign to potentially serious disorders. Some common causes of mouth darkening include perioral dermatitis, post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation, melasma, allergic contact dermatitis, smoking or tobacco usage, excessive sun exposure, vitiligo, nutritional deficiencies, and medication side effects.
Hormonal Changes and Imbalances
Hormonal fluctuations and imbalances can have a variety of consequences on the body, including affecting skin health and even causing discoloration around the lips and other parts of the face. Here's how hormone shifts might cause skin issues:
Melasma
:
Melasma can be caused by hormonal shifts, such as those experienced during pregnancy (also known as "pregnancy mask") or when using oral contraceptives. It manifests as dark or grey-brown patches on the face, particularly around the mouth. Sun protection (wearing
sunscreen
with an SPF of 30 or higher), topical treatments (such as hydroquinone and retinoids), and procedures (such as chemical peels or laser therapy) may all assist in managing melasma.
Acne
:
Hormonal changes, particularly increased androgen levels (such as testosterone), can cause excess sebum production and clogged pores, resulting in acne breakouts around the mouth and jawline. Topical or oral hormone-regulating drugs (such as birth control pills or spironolactone) may be used in combination with topical therapies (benzoyl peroxide, retinoids) and professional skincare regimens.
Menopause
:
Menopausal hormone changes might alter skin elasticity, moisture levels, and collagen formation, resulting in dryness, thinned skin, and perhaps uneven
pigmentation
. Hormone replacement therapy (HRT) or skincare products including peptides,
hyaluronic acid
, and antioxidants can help improve skin health throughout menopause.
Sun Exposure and UV Damage
Sun exposure and UV (ultraviolet) damage can have a substantial impact on skin health and appearance, resulting in discoloration around the lips and other parts of the face. Here's how sun exposure and UV damage affect your skin:
Effects of Sun Exposure
:
Prolonged sun exposure can cause uneven melanin production, resulting in dark spots, freckles, or
hyperpigmentation
around the lips and other sun-exposed areas. Sunscreen with broad-spectrum protection (SPF 30 or greater), sun-protective clothing, and avoiding peak sun hours can all help prevent additional hyperpigmentation. Topical therapies such as hydroquinone, retinoids, and chemical peels may assist in lightening existing dark spots.
Excessive sun exposure without protection can result in sunburn, which is characterized by redness, irritation, and, in some cases, blistering or peeling skin. Cool compresses, moisturizing lotions (aloe vera), and over-the-counter pain medications can help relieve discomfort. Severe sunburn may necessitate medical treatment.
Cumulative UV exposure can cause premature aging indications such as fine lines,
wrinkles
, sagging skin, and uneven skin tone, particularly around the mouth. Daily use of sunscreen, antioxidant-rich
moisturizers
, retinoids, and consistent skincare routines can all help to reduce the effects of photoaging.
UV radiation is a key risk factor for developing skin cancers such as melanoma, basal cell carcinoma, and squamous cell carcinoma. Regular use of broad-spectrum sunscreen, seeking shade, wearing protective gear (wide-brimmed hats, sunglasses), and avoiding tanning beds can all help lower the risk of skin cancer.
UV Damage
:
Apply sunscreen generously (at least SPF 30) to all exposed skin, including lips, 15-30 minutes before sun exposure. Reapply every 2 hours or more frequently if swimming or sweating.
Wear hats, sunglasses, and clothing with UPF (Ultraviolet Protection Factor) to shield skin from UV rays.
Limit outdoor activities during midday hours when UV radiation is strongest (10 AM to 4 PM).
Perform self-examinations of your skin and see a dermatologist annually for professional skin exams to monitor for any changes or suspicious lesions.
Skin Conditions and Inflammation
Skin disorders caused by inflammation can manifest in a variety of ways, affecting both appearance and comfort. The following are some common skin conditions marked by inflammation:
Eczema (Atopic Dermatitis)
:
Eczema is a chronic inflammatory skin condition marked by red, itchy, and dry patches of skin. It can happen anywhere on the body, especially around the lips. Eczema is caused by a combination of genetics, immune system dysfunction, environmental factors, and skin barrier abnormalities. Moisturizing creams, topical corticosteroids, antihistamines, and avoiding triggers (such as harsh soaps or allergies) can assist in treating eczema flare-ups.
Psoriasis
:
Psoriasis is an autoimmune illness characterized by fast skin cell turnover, resulting in thicker, red, scaly regions known as plaques. It most usually affects the elbows, knees, and scalp, but it can also arise near the mouth. Genetic predisposition, immune system malfunction, and environmental stressors. Psoriasis symptoms are managed with topical therapies (such as corticosteroids and retinoids), phototherapy, systemic drugs (such as biologics), and lifestyle changes (such as stress reduction and moisturizing).
Rosacea
:
Rosacea is a chronic inflammatory skin condition characterized by facial redness, flushing, visible blood vessels, and, in some cases, pustules (bumps that look like acne). It may affect the cheekbones, nose, chin, and forehead. Although the specific origin is unknown, heredity, faulty blood vessels, and triggers (such as sun exposure, hot beverages, and alcohol) all play a role. Topical medicines (metronidazole, azelaic acid), oral antibiotics, laser therapy (for visible blood vessels), and lifestyle changes can help to manage rosacea.
Contact Dermatitis
:
Contact dermatitis is a localized skin reaction caused by exposure to an irritant or allergen. It manifests as red, itchy, and occasionally blistering skin. Contact dermatitis is caused by coming into contact with cosmetics, soaps, metals, plants (such as poison ivy), or pharmaceuticals. Contact dermatitis is managed by avoiding the irritant or allergen, using topical corticosteroids, antihistamines (for allergic reactions), and soothing skincare products.
Acne
: Acne is a common skin ailment characterized by clogged pores, irritation, and, in some cases, bacterial infections. It causes pimples, blackheads, whiteheads, and cysts on the face, particularly around the lips and chin. Excessive oil production, plugged pores, bacteria (Propionibacterium acnes), hormonal fluctuations, and inflammation all contribute to acne. Acne is managed with topical therapies (such as benzoyl peroxide and retinoids), oral drugs (antibiotics and oral contraceptives), and lifestyle adjustments (gentle skin care and avoidance of triggers).
Types of Discoloration and Common Symptoms
Skin discoloration can take many forms, each with its own set of traits and potential reasons. The following are some prevalent types of skin discoloration and their usual symptoms:
Hyperpigmentation
: Hyperpigmentation is defined as patches of the skin that seem darker than the surrounding skin due to an overproduction of melanin, the pigment responsible for skin color. There are two types: melasma and post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH).
Hypopigmentation
: Hypopigmentation occurs when portions of the skin look lighter than the surrounding skin due to a decrease in melanin synthesis or the loss of melanocytes. There are two types: vitiligo and post-inflammatory hypopigmentation.
Erythema
:
Erythema is skin redness induced by increased blood flow to the affected area, which is frequently the result of inflammation, irritation, or vascular problems. Rosacea and sunburn are the two types.
Cyanosis
:
Cyanosis is a bluish staining of the skin or mucous membranes caused by insufficient oxygen delivery to tissues. There are two types of cyanosis: peripheral and central.
Melasma: Causes, Symptoms, and Treatment Options
Melasma is a common skin disorder marked by dark, uneven patches or hyperpigmentation on the skin, which usually appears on the face. Here's a thorough explanation of its causes, symptoms, and treatment options:
Melasma can be caused by hormonal factors, sun exposure, a genetic predisposition, and cosmetics or skin care products.
Symptoms of Melasma include hyperpigmented patches, symmetrical appearance, border and distribution, and no physical symptoms.
Melasma treatment options include sun protection (such as sunscreen and protective clothing), topical treatments (such as hydroquinone, retinoids, and azelaic acid), combination therapies (such as triple combination cream and customized formulations), and procedures (such as chemical peels and laser therapy). There's also maintenance therapy.
Post-Inflammatory Hyperpigmentation: Overview and Management
Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) is a common skin disorder defined by darkening patches or spots that appear following an inflammatory skin injury, such as acne lesions, eczema flare-ups, insect bites, or other skin traumas. Below is an overview of PIH, including its causes, symptoms, and treatment options:
Causes of Post-Inflammatory Hyperpigmentation (PIH) include inflammatory skin conditions (such as acne, eczema (atopic dermatitis), psoriasis, allergic reactions, and insect bites or burns), as well as skin type and ethnicity.
Symptoms of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation include darkened patches, irregular borders, localized areas, and no physical symptoms.
Options for managing and treating post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation include topical treatments (hydroquinone, retinoid, and azelaic acid), chemical peels, topical corticosteroids, combination therapies, procedures (laser therapy, microneedling), lifestyle and self-care (gentle skincare, avoiding triggers, etc).
Lip Hyperpigmentation: Understanding the Causes and Solutions
The darkening of the lips brought on by excessive melanin synthesis is known as lip hyperpigmentation. Numerous things, from lifestyle choices to underlying medical issues, might contribute to this illness. Determining the root issues can aid in the development of workable solutions.
Causes
Prolonged exposure to UV rays can stimulate melanin production in the lips, leading to hyperpigmentation.
Nicotine and other chemicals in cigarettes can darken the lips over time.
Certain cosmetics, toothpaste, or lip products may cause allergic reactions that result in hyperpigmentation.
Constantly licking lips can strip them of their natural moisture, leading to irritation and darkening.
Conditions like Addison’s disease, which affects the adrenal glands, can cause hyperpigmentation in various parts of the body, including the lips.
Some medications, such as antimalarials and minocycline, can cause pigmentation changes.
A predisposition to hyperpigmentation can be inherited.
Pregnancy, menopause, and other hormonal changes can affect melanin production.
Solutions
Use lip balms with SPF to protect against UV rays.
Reducing or quitting smoking can prevent further darkening.
Be mindful of ingredients in lip products and switch to hypoallergenic options if necessary.
Drink plenty of water and avoid licking your lips to maintain moisture levels.
Products containing ingredients like kojic acid, glycolic acid, and hydroquinone can lighten pigmentation. Natural remedies like lemon juice, honey, and aloe vera can also be helpful.
For persistent cases, dermatological treatments such as laser therapy, chemical peels, and microdermabrasion may be recommended.
A diet rich in vitamins, especially
vitamins C
and E, can support skin health and reduce hyperpigmentation.

Skincare Products for Hyperpigmentation: What to Look For
Hyperpigmentation, defined as dark patches or spots on the skin, can be efficiently treated with the correct skincare products. When selecting hyperpigmentation products, seek chemicals that have been shown to lighten the skin and reduce pigmentation. Here is a guide of things you should look for:
Vitamin C brightens the complexion, eliminates dark spots, and protects against free radical damage. Frequently found in serums, it should be used in the morning before sunscreen.
One of the most effective substances for decreasing hyperpigmentation is hydroquinone, which inhibits melanin synthesis. Typically available in doses of 2% (otc) or greater (prescription). Use under dermatological supervision to avoid any negative effects.
Niacinamide (Vitamin B3) reduces the appearance of dark spots and enhances skin barrier function. Can be used both morning and night; commonly used in serums and moisturizers.
Retinoids (Retinol, Retin-A) increase cell turnover and decrease pigmentation. Use at night, beginning with smaller dosages to develop tolerance.
Alpha Arbutin reduces melanin formation and lightens dark patches. It is commonly used in serums and can be used twice a day.
Kojic acid inhibits tyrosinase, an enzyme involved in melanin synthesis. Use creams and serums as directed.
Azelaic Acid reduces pigmentation and has anti-inflammatory qualities. Suitable for delicate skin, and commonly used in gels and creams.
Licorice Extract brightens the complexion and decreases discoloration. Serums and creams often contain this ingredient, which is safe to use daily.
Glycolic acid exfoliates the skin, removing pigmented cells and promoting new cell development. Found in chemical peels and exfoliating toners. Begin with lower quantities to prevent irritation.
Salicylic acid exfoliates and enters the pores, which is excellent for post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation. To avoid over-drying, use
cleansers
and spot treatments as advised.
Melasma and other kinds of hyperpigmentation are reduced with tranexamic acid. Found in serums and creams, which are typically administered twice daily.

Sugar and Honey Scrub
:
Ingredients are 1 teaspoon sugar (white or brown) and 1 teaspoon honey. In a small bowl, combine sugar and honey. Apply the mixture to your lips with a clean fingertip. Gently rub in circular strokes for 1-2 minutes. Rinse with lukewarm water, then pat dry. Follow with a moisturizing lip balm.
Baking Soda and Water Paste
:
The ingredients are 1 teaspoon baking soda and a few drops of water. Combine the baking soda and enough water to make a paste. Apply the paste on your lips. Massage gently in circular strokes for one minute. Rinse with lukewarm water, then pat dry. Use a moisturizing lip balm.
Soft Toothbrush Exfoliation
:
The material is a soft-bristled toothbrush. Put a coating of lip balm or petroleum jelly on your lips. Brush your lips softly in circular motions with a gentle toothbrush for about a minute. Rinse with lukewarm water, then pat dry. Reapply lip balm to maintain hydration.
Oatmeal and Milk Scrub
:
The ingredients are 1 teaspoon of ground oats and 1 teaspoon of milk. Combine the ground oatmeal and milk to make a paste. Apply the paste to your lips. Scrub gently in circular motions for 1–2 minutes. Rinse with lukewarm water, then pat dry. Follow with a moisturizing lip balm.
Coconut Oil and Sugar Scrub
:
Ingredients are 1 teaspoon coconut oil, and 1 teaspoon sugar. In a small mixing dish, combine coconut oil and sugar. Apply the mixture to your lips. Gently rub in a circular stroke for 1-2 minutes. Rinse with lukewarm water, then pat dry. Use a moisturizing lip balm.
Coffee Grounds Scrub
