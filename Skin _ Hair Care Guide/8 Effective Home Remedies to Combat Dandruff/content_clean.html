Understanding Dandruff: Causes and Symptoms

Dandruff is often associated with seborrheic dermatitis (SD), which produces itchy and dry skin on the scalp. It is so widespread that it affects around half of the world's adult population. Dandruff can be caused by a variety of circumstances, making it difficult to determine the actual cause of your itchy, flaky scalp. Here are several possible culprits:

  • The oils that your sebaceous glands produce.

  • Fungal colonization on the surface of your skin.

  • Malassezia is a yeast that can irritate the scalp and cause excessive skin cell growth.

  • Insufficient shampooing may cause skin cells to accumulate and create flakes and itching.

  • Shampooing too frequently can cause overdrying of the scalp.

  • Brushing your hair too much or too forcefully can cause friction with the sensitive skin on your scalp.

  • A reaction to personal care products, or contact dermatitis, that causes your scalp to irritate and itchy.

  • Sun damage

  • Hats exert excessive pressure on the scalp.

  • A diet that is deficient in certain nutrients

  • Being exposed to dirt or dust.

Some people may be more susceptible to dandruff than others for unexplained reasons. For example, guys are more likely to have dandruff than females. You may have noticed symptoms throughout puberty, but dandruff can appear at any age. People with oilier hair or who suffer from certain illnesses (such as Parkinson's disease, HIV, or other immune-system-altering medicines) are also at a higher risk.

What Causes Dandruff?

Dandruff can be caused by a variety of factors, including irritated or greasy skin, dry skin. Malassezia is a yeast-like fungus that lives on most adults' scalps and feeds off of oils.

Common Symptoms of Dandruff

The primary symptoms of dandruff are skin flakes and an itchy, scaly, oily scalp. Oily flakes tend to gather in your hair and shoulders, and they generally worsen in the fall and winter when the air is dry. Symptoms may be more severe in certain people. Dandruff symptoms may include:

  • oily flakes

  • Itching

  • Irritated patches on the skin of the scalp and sometimes on the face

  • Eyebrow dandruff

  • Hair loss

  • Dry flakes on the skin of the face

Top Home Remedies for Dandruff

Some people recommend that you try natural home remedies for dandruff before turning to medicated solutions.

  1. Baking soda: Apply baking soda to your scalp in the shower and rinse after a few minutes.

  2. Apple cider vinegar: Combine 1/4 cup vinegar with 1/4 cup water. Apply it to your hair for 15 minutes to an hour, then rinse. Do this twice a week.

  3. Coconut oil: Apply 3 to 5 teaspoons of coconut oil to your scalp and leave for an hour. Then shampoo your hair and rinse it thoroughly.

  4. Lemon juice: Apply two teaspoons of lemon juice to your scalp and rinse. Then, rub in one more teaspoon, mix with one cup of water. Perform this routine every day.

  5. Aloe vera: Apply aloe vera to your scalp before washing your hair.

  6. Olive oil: Massage 10 drops into your scalp. Cover your head with a shower hat and leave it overnight. Shampoo and cleanse your hair in the morning.

Natural ingredients may cause skin reactions in certain people. It is best to apply a new product to a small area and leave it there for at least 24 hours before moving on to a larger area.

Using Apple Cider Vinegar

Washing your scalp with a solution of diluted apple cider vinegar may help reduce dandruff.

  • Mix 1-3 tablespoons (tbsp) of apple cider vinegar with warm water.

  • After shampooing, apply the apple cider vinegar wash to your head.

  • Massage the mixture into the scalp with your fingertips.

  • Allow the mixture to sit for a few minutes before rinsing thoroughly.

Apple cider vinegar has a strong odor that some people may find unpleasant. Adding a few drops of essential oil to the scalp treatment may help to mask the smell.

Proper Hair Washing Techniques

Now that we've covered what not to do, try this stylist-approved, step-by-step tutorial for washing and conditioning your hair.

  • Soak your hair in warm water before shampooing.

  • Start washing your hair at the roots.

  • Scrub your scalp, not your hair.

  • Wash your hands and repeat as needed.

  • Rinse your shampoo thoroughly. 

  • Apply conditioner to the bottom half of the hair. 

  • Rinse the conditioner with a cold solution. 

Choosing the Right Shampoo

Observe how your hair reacts over time after applying a new shampoo. If the shampoo increases texture, volume, gloss, or manageability, it is most likely appropriate for your hair. If any of these symptoms worsen, or if the shampoo causes irritation or itching on your scalp, it may not be appropriate for your hair.
