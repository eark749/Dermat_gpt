Understanding Dandruff: Causes and Symptoms
Dandruff
is often associated with
seborrheic dermatitis
(SD), which produces itchy and dry skin on the scalp. It is so widespread that it affects around half of the world's adult population. Dandruff can be caused by a variety of circumstances, making it difficult to determine the actual cause of your itchy, flaky scalp. Here are several possible culprits:
The oils that your sebaceous glands produce.
Fungal colonization on the surface of your skin.
Malassezia is a yeast that can irritate the scalp and cause excessive skin cell growth.
Insufficient shampooing may cause skin cells to accumulate and create flakes and itching.
Shampooing too frequently can cause overdrying of the scalp.
Brushing your hair too much or too forcefully can cause friction with the
sensitive skin
on your scalp.
A reaction to personal care products, or
contact dermatitis
, that causes your scalp to irritate and itchy.
Sun damage
Hats exert excessive pressure on the scalp.
A diet that is deficient in certain nutrients
Being exposed to dirt or dust.
Some people may be more susceptible to dandruff than others for unexplained reasons. For example, guys are more likely to have dandruff than females. You may have noticed symptoms throughout puberty, but dandruff can appear at any age. People with oilier hair or who suffer from certain illnesses (such as Parkinson's disease, HIV, or other immune-system-altering medicines) are also at a higher risk.

Some people recommend that you try natural home remedies for dandruff before turning to medicated solutions.

Lemon juice

Olive oil

Using Apple Cider Vinegar
Washing your scalp with a solution of diluted apple cider vinegar may help reduce dandruff.
Mix 1-3 tablespoons (tbsp) of apple cider vinegar with warm water.
After shampooing, apply the apple cider vinegar wash to your head.
Massage the mixture into the scalp with your fingertips.
Allow the mixture to sit for a few minutes before rinsing thoroughly.
Apple cider vinegar has a strong odor that some people may find unpleasant. Adding a few drops of essential oil to the scalp treatment may help to mask the smell.

Choosing the Right Shampoo
Observe how your hair reacts over time after applying a new shampoo. If the shampoo increases texture, volume, gloss, or manageability, it is most likely appropriate for your hair. If any of these symptoms worsen, or if the shampoo causes irritation or itching on your scalp, it may not be appropriate for your hair.
Diet and Hydration Tips
Eating correctly is usually beneficial because it boosts your overall immunity and improves your health. However, if you're looking for

: This is an entire category of vitamins that are becoming increasingly vital in your diet. Vitamin B3 (Niacin) is believed to improve blood circulation and reduce cholesterol levels. Reduce sugar
:
This will surely be bad news for those who enjoy sweets. If you want to get rid of your scalp problems, you should cut back on sugar, particularly processed sugar. Sugar has a high glycemic index, which can cause insulin levels to increase. This has a significant impact on your sebum production because it also affects your hormone levels. If you crave something sweet, try coconut sugar, but only in moderation.
Fatty acids
:
If you're seeking nourishment for dry hair and scalp, look for omega 3 and omega 6 fatty acid-rich components. This can help manage your skin's sebum production while also increasing moisture levels. It also helps with inflammation. You can supplement with these foods or eat fish, soybeans, eggs, spinach, almonds, or seeds.
Zinc
:
It is important to consume adequate zinc when attempting to regulate the quantity of moisture on your head. Zinc regulates sebum secretion on the scalp, reducing dryness. However, keep in mind that using too much will cause your scalp to dry out even more. Add biotin, another B vitamin that aids in zinc absorption. Use components such as beans, whole grains, dairy products, almonds, and pumpkin.
Vitamin A
:

Iron
: This mineral is required for the vital function of carrying oxygen and nutrients throughout the body, particularly to the skin cells. It is essential for improving scalp health and ensuring skin function. However, you should exercise caution because excess iron can cause skin infections and other problems. It is present in red meat, shellfish, beans, spinach, apricots, tofu, and peas.
