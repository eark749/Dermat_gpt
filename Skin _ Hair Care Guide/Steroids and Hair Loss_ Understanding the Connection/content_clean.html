How Steroids Affect Hair

Increased levels of dihydrotestosterone (DHT) are particularly damaging to hair follicles. DHT will adhere to the follicles and irritate them. Eventually, these damaged hairs will fall off. Many steroids enhance DHT levels in the body, resulting in substantial hair loss.

Types of Steroids and Their Uses

Steroids are commonly used to treat hair loss disorders and come in a variety of forms. Topical steroids, intralesional steroids, oral steroids, and steroid-sparing agents are examples of steroids often used to treat hair loss and their specialized applications.

Mechanisms of Hair Loss Due to Steroids

Steroid-induced hair loss is caused by the conversion of testosterone into dihydrotestosterone (DHT). This occurs due to the action of an enzyme known as 5-alpha-reductase. DHT binds to hair follicles more strongly than testosterone, causing them to shrink and begin a shorter growth phase. When the hair follicles' growth cycle is disturbed, the hair gets thinner and shorter, resulting in the appearance of balding or thinning hair. Over time, the afflicted hair follicles may cease to create new hair strands, resulting in irreversible hair loss.

Anabolic vs. Corticosteroids: Hair Impact

Anabolic Steroids

  • People who are predisposed to androgenetic alopecia (male or female pattern baldness) may experience an acceleration of hair loss when using anabolic steroids. This is caused by the conversion of testosterone to dihydrotestosterone (DHT), which can decrease hair follicles and shorten the hair growth cycle.

  • Some anabolic steroids may promote excessive hair growth (hirsutism) in women, particularly on the face and body.

Corticosteroids

  • Corticosteroids can help with hair regrowth in disorders such as alopecia areata, in which the immune system assaults hair follicles. They help by lowering inflammation surrounding hair follicles and dampening the immune system.

  • Long-term corticosteroid use, particularly in high doses, can result in hair thinning or loss due to hormonal imbalances and systemic effects on the body.

Symptoms and Diagnosis of Steroid-Induced Hair Loss

Taking anabolic steroids can cause excessive DHT production, resulting in genetic hair loss diseases such as male pattern baldness or female pattern baldness. At this stage, men may use hair loss treatment drugs such as finasteride to reduce the effects of the hormone.

Recognizing Hair Loss Patterns

Male-pattern hair loss (MPHL) usually manifests as a receding front hairline, hair loss on the crown and vertex of the head, or a combination of the two. Female-pattern hair loss (FPHL) usually manifests as a diffuse thinning of the hair on the entire scalp.

Medical Diagnosis and Tests

Diagnosing steroid-induced hair loss necessitates a thorough medical history, a physical examination, and different diagnostic tests. Here's a thorough breakdown of the procedure:

  • Laboratory tests include blood tests and scalp biopsies.

Preventing and treating steroid-induced hair loss requires a variety of techniques, including reducing steroid use, implementing supportive treatments, and maintaining a healthy lifestyle. Here's a complete guide to addressing both prevention and treatment:

  • Talk to your doctor about discontinuing or adjusting the steroid that is causing hair loss.

  • Minoxidil (Rogaine) is a topical treatment that promotes hair growth and slows hair loss. Topical corticosteroids, such as alopecia areata, can reduce inflammation and promote hair regrowth.

  • One treatment option for androgenetic alopecia is finasteride (Propecia), which works by preventing testosterone from being converted to DHT. Women who experience androgenic effects-related hair loss may benefit from taking the anti-androgen drug spirolactone.

  • Make sure your diet contains plenty of the vitamins and minerals—like biotin, zinc, iron, and vitamin C, A, and E—that are necessary for healthy hair.

  • Practice stress-relieving activities such as yoga, meditation, and regular exercise, as stress can worsen hair loss.

  • Using platelets extracted from your blood, platelet-rich plasma (PRP) therapy stimulates the growth of new hair on the scalp. In cases where other treatments have proven ineffective, hair transplant surgery may be considered in severe cases.

  • Consult a dermatologist or trichologist regularly to monitor the condition of your hair and scalp and make necessary treatment adjustments.

Effective Treatments for Steroid-Induced Hair Loss

There are effective treatments for certain types of hair loss. You might be able to reverse or reduce your hair loss. In some cases, such as patchy hair loss (alopecia areata), hair can regenerate without treatment within a year. Medication and surgery are both options for treating hair loss. The most prevalent choices include:

  1. Minoxidil (Rogaine): Minoxidil is available over the counter (without a prescription) in liquid, foam, and shampoo form. For maximum effectiveness, apply the product to the scalp skin once daily for women and twice daily for males. Many people prefer to apply foam while their hair is wet. Many people find that minoxidil-containing products help them regrow their hair, decrease the rate of hair loss, or do both. It will take at least six months of treatment to prevent additional hair loss and begin hair regrowth. It may take several months to determine whether the treatment is working for you. If it is effective, you will need to continue taking the medication indefinitely to get the benefits. Possible adverse effects include scalp irritation and abnormal hair growth on the skin around the face and hands.

  2. Finasteride (Propecia): This is a prescription medication for guys. You take a medication every day. Many men who use finasteride notice a slowdown of hair loss, and others may even see new hair growth. It may take several months to determine whether it is working for you. To maintain the benefits, you must continue to take it. Finasteride may not be as effective for males over 60. Finasteride's rare adverse effects include decreased sex drive and sexual function, as well as an increased risk of developing prostate cancer. Women who are or may be pregnant should avoid handling crushed or broken pills.

  3. Other medications: Other oral alternatives include spironolactone (Carospir, Aldactone) and oral dutasteride (Avodart).

Lifestyle Changes to Support Hair Health

  1. Healthy Diet: The main objective when it comes to nutrients is to have a balanced diet full of essential nutrients. Include foods high in protein, vitamins, and minerals. Consume plenty of water to keep your scalp and hair hydrated.

  2. Exercise Regularly: Regular physical activity enhances blood circulation, thereby providing nourishment to hair follicles. Exercises that boost hair health include swimming, jogging, and walking.

  3. Stress Management: Engage in stress-reducing activities such as yoga, meditation, or deep breathing exercises. Get 7-9 hours of quality sleep per night to promote overall health and hair growth.

  4. Hair Care Routine: Choose a gentle, sulfate-free shampoo that is appropriate for your hair type. Use conditioner to keep the ends of your hair moisturized. Minimize your use of heat styling tools (hairdryers, straighteners). Make sure not to pull or brush too hard. Use a wide-toothed comb to detangle.

  5. Scalp Care: Cleanse your scalp regularly to avoid buildup and dandruff. Use a mild or medicated shampoo if necessary. Regular scalp massages can increase blood flow to the hair follicles.

  6. Avoid Harmful Habits: Quit smoking, as it reduces blood flow to the scalp and contributes to hair loss. Alcohol consumption should be limited, as it can have a negative impact on hair health.

  7. Supplements: If your diet lacks certain nutrients, consider taking supplements such as biotin, iron, vitamin D, and omega-3 fatty acids.

  8. Healthy Lifestyle Choices: Limit the use of harsh hair treatments and dyes. To protect your hair from sun damage, wear a hat or use UV-protective hair products.

  9. Regular check-ups: Treat any underlying health issues that may be affecting your hair, such as thyroid or hormonal imbalances. Consult a dermatologist or trichologist for personalized advice and treatment options.
