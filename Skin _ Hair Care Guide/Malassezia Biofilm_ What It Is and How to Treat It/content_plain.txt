What is Biofilm?
Biofilm is a structured community of microorganisms, including bacteria, fungi, and protists, that adhere to a surface and are surrounded by a matrix of extracellular polymeric substances (EPS). The EPS can be composed of various substances, including proteins, sugars, and DNA, and provide a protective environment for the microorganisms within the biofilm.
Biofilms can form on a variety of surfaces, including medical devices, pipes, and living tissues, such as teeth and skin. They are particularly problematic in healthcare settings, where they can form on medical implants or catheters, leading to infections that are difficult to treat.
Biofilms are known to be more resistant to antibiotics and disinfectants than free-living microorganisms, and can cause persistent infections that are difficult to eradicate. The protective matrix of the biofilm can prevent antibiotics and other treatments from reaching the microorganisms within, and the microorganisms themselves can adapt to the hostile environment and become more resistant to treatment.
Research is ongoing into the mechanisms by which biofilms form and how they can be treated. Approaches to disrupting biofilms include physical removal, chemical treatments, and approaches that target the EPS matrix, such as enzymes or electromagnetic fields.
