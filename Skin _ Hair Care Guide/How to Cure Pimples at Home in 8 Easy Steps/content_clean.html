What causes pimples?

Before you can cure pimples at home, it is vital to understand the reasons behind them! After all, you want to know what you are actually treating. Pimples occur when small pores in the skin get clogged with excess sebum (oil) and dead skin cells. The pores then become infected, resulting in pimples. If you are wondering about the name of the bacteria responsible for causing pimples, it is Propionobacterium acnes. If you have oily skin or are frequently exposed to environmental pollutants, the chances of your skin pores getting clogged might be higher. In such a case, following an anti-pimple skincare routine will help you treat and prevent pimples forever. The following are the primary causes of pimples: 

  1. Sebum Overproduction:  The skin's sebaceous (oil) glands create sebum, a natural oil that helps keep the skin moisturised. When the sebaceous glands create an excessive amount of sebum, the hair follicles might become clogged.

  2. Hair Follicle Clogging: Skin cells, sebum, and hair can combine to form a block in hair follicles, preventing natural sebum flow to the skin's surface. This plug produces an environment conducive to bacterial growth.

  3. Bacterial Growth: Clogged hair follicles provide an ideal environment for the growth of Propionibacterium acnes (P. acnes), a type of bacteria that is normally present on the skin. In clogged follicles, P. acnes can multiply quickly.

  4. Inflammation: When bacteria multiply, the immune system responds with inflammation, resulting in redness, swelling, and pus formation. This inflammatory response is responsible for pimples' distinctive appearance.

  5. Hormonal Changes: Hormonal changes, particularly during puberty, can cause an increase in sebum production, making adolescents more prone to pimples. Acne can also be caused by menstrual cycle hormonal changes, pregnancy, or medical problems including polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS).

  6. Diet and Lifestyle: Certain dietary factors, such as the consumption of high-glycemic-index foods and dairy products, can aggravate pimples in some people. Acne can be aggravated by lifestyle factors such as stress and pollution exposure.

  7. Genetics: A family history of acne can increase the likelihood of developing pimples. Genetic factors can affect the structure and function of the skin, as well as the body's inflammatory response.

  8. Cosmetics and Hair Products: The use of pore-clogging cosmetics or hair products, especially if they are not properly removed, can contribute to the development of pimples.

While these factors all contribute to the development of pimples, acne can vary widely from person to person. Some people are genetically predisposed to acne, while others have acne predominantly as a result of hormone fluctuations or other circumstances. Acne management frequently entails addressing several variables and may necessitate a personalised approach, which may include skincare, lifestyle, changes, and, in some circumstances, medication therapies. If you have acne concerns, consult a dermatologist for expert guidance and treatment choices.

Hormonal acne vs regular pimples

Acne vulgaris and hormonal acne are both kinds of acne, although they differ in their underlying causes, features, and occurrence patterns. The following are the main differences between hormonal acne and ordinary pimples:

Hormonal Acne:

  1. Cause: Hormonal acne is mostly caused by changes in the body's hormones, specifically androgen hormones like testosterone. Hormonal imbalances can increase sebum (skin oil) production and alter its composition, making the skin more susceptible to acne.

  2. Location: The lower face (chin and jawline), neck, chest, and back are common places for hormonal acne to show up. It is less common on the brow and cheeks.

  3. Pattern: Hormonal acne frequently follows a cyclic pattern, with breakouts occurring at specific times during the menstrual cycle in women. It can also occur during pregnancy, menopause, or as a result of conditions such as polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS).

  4. Lesions: Deep, cystic acne lesions that are painful, larger, and more likely to leave scars are common in hormonal acne.  It can also appear as smaller pimples and blackheads.

  5. Age of onset: Adolescence and adulthood are possible times when hormonal acne first appears. It affects adult women more frequently.

  6. Reaction to Topical Treatments: Hormonal acne often responds less well to over-the-counter topicals, prescription medications, such as hormonal therapies like anti-androgens or oral contraceptives, may be required.

Regular Pimples (Acne Vulgaris):

  1. Cause: Acne vulgaris, or recurring pimples, can be caused by an overabundance of sebum production, hair follicle blockage, bacterial development (P. acnes), inflammation, and genetic susceptibility.

  2. Location: The face, neck, chest, back, and shoulders are all possible places for regular pimples to appear. They do not just affect particular regions, such as hormonal acne.

  3. Pattern: Although flare-ups are possible with regular pimples, they do not always follow a cyclical pattern associated with changes in hormone levels.

  4. Types of Lesions: A variety of lesions can be associated with regular pimples, such as whiteheads, blackheads, pustules, papules, and, in certain situations, cysts and nodules. 

  5. Age of Onset: Hormonal changes associated with adolescence are the usual cause of acne vulgaris, although it can strike people at any age.

  6. Reaction to Topical Treatments: Benzoyl peroxide, salicylic acid, or alpha hydroxy acids are among the ingredients found in many over-the-counter topical treatments that can be used to treat frequent breakouts. In more severe cases, prescription medications may be required.

Importance of cleanliness in preventing acne

The significance of hygiene in acne prevention and skin health cannot be emphasised. Acne is commonly linked to clogged pores, increased oil production, and the growth of acne-causing bacteria. Here are a few reasons why cleanliness is essential for acne prevention:

  1. Excess Oil and Impurities: Regular cleansing aids in the removal of excess sebum (skin oil), dirt, and sweat from the skin's surface. When these substances build up, they can clog pores and cause acne breakouts.

  2. Unclogging Pores: Clean skin is less likely to develop clogged pores, which are a major contributor to pimple formation. Blackheads and whiteheads can form as a result of clogged pores.

  3. Bacterial Control: Proper cleansing can help control the population of acne-causing bacteria on the skin's surface, such as Propionibacterium acnes (P. acnes).  Reducing bacterial growth can aid in the prevention of inflammatory acne.

  4. Inflammation Prevention: Clean skin is less likely to become inflamed. Acne is characterised by inflammation, which causes the redness and swelling associated with pimples.

  5. Skincare Product Absorption: Clean skin is more receptive to skincare products, allowing them to penetrate the skin more effectively. This can boost the effectiveness of acne treatments and moisturisers.

  6. Secondary Infection Prevention: Keeping the skin clean aids in the prevention of secondary infections, which can occur when pimples are picked, squeezed, or broken open.

  7. Post-Inflammatory Hyperpigmentation Prevention: Clean skin is less likely to develop post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation, which are dark spots caused by acne breakouts. By preventing acne, you reduce the risk of scarring and dark marks.

  8. Hydration Balance: Proper cleansing can help to keep the skin's natural hydration balance, which is important for overall skin health.

To keep your skin clean and prevent acne, you should:

  • Cleanse your face twice a day (morning and night) with a gentle, non-comedogenic cleanser.

  • Hot water might be too harsh on the skin, so when cleansing, use lukewarm water.

  • Excessive cleansing can strip the skin of its natural oils, leading to increased oil production.

  • To avoid the transfer of oil and bacteria to the skin, use a clean towel and pillowcase.

  • Choose non-comedogenic skincare and makeup to avoid pore clogging.

Cleanliness is a crucial facet of acne prevention, but it should be part of a full skincare programme that includes proper products and a healthy lifestyle. If you have acne concerns, see a dermatologist for personalised advice and treatment choices.

Role of diet in acne and pimples

Some people's diets can influence the development and control of acne and pimples. Diet and acne have a complicated relationship, and not all persons are equally influenced by dietary factors. Certain dietary choices and behaviors, however, have been linked to acne and may play a role in its development. Here are some essential points about nutrition and acne:

  1. High-Glycemic Foods: High-glycemic-index foods and refined carbs (e.g., white bread, sugary snacks, and sugary beverages) may raise blood sugar levels and activate insulin production. High insulin levels can cause an increase in sebum (skin oil), inflammation, and the growth of acne-causing bacteria, all of which contribute to the formation of pimples.

  2. Dairy Products: Some research has suggested that dairy consumption, particularly skim milk, may increase the incidence of acne. Hormones and growth factors included in dairy products are known to have an impact on skin health. The precise mechanisms and effects of dairy on acne can differ from person to person.

  3. Fatty and trans fats: A diet high in saturated and trans fats, which are commonly found in fried and fast foods, may promote inflammation in the body, exacerbating acne.

  4. Omega-6 Fatty Acids: A high intake of omega-6 fatty acids, which are commonly found in vegetable oils, can aggravate inflammation. It is essential to balance omega-6 fatty acid consumption with omega-3 fatty acids, which have anti-inflammatory characteristics, for overall skin health.

  5. Food Sensitivities and Allergies: Some people may experience acne flare-ups as a result of food allergies or sensitivities. Gluten, dairy, and certain additives or preservatives are common culprits.

  6. Antioxidants and Anti-Inflammatory Foods: Fruits and vegetables, which are high in antioxidants, may help reduce inflammation and support general skin health. Anti-inflammatory foods, such as fatty fish (high in omega-3 fatty acids), may also aid skin health.

  7. Hydration: Staying hydrated is essential for overall skin health. Dehydrated skin may produce extra oil to compensate, which may result in acne.

It's essential to remember that everyone's reactions to nutrition differ, and not everyone gets acne or pimples as a result of their eating habits. Acne is also influenced by genetics, hormonal factors, and skincare habits. If you feel that your diet is contributing to your acne, make gradual dietary modifications and monitor how your skin reacts. A dermatologist or other healthcare professional can provide personalised advice on how to treat acne and its associated dietary factors. How to avoid pimple scars

Acne management and gentle skincare practises are the first steps in preventing pimple scars. Here are some pointers to avoid pimple scars:

  • Early Treatment: To reduce the risk of scarring, treat acne as soon as it appears. If you have a tendency to scar easily, seek professional advice and treatment from a dermatologist.

  • Hands Off: To prevent scarring and to worsen inflammation, refrain from popping, squeezing, or picking at pimples. Allow pimples to heal naturally.

  • Gentle Cleansing: To avoid further skin irritation, use a mild, non-comedogenic cleanser and wash your face gently.

  • Sun Protection: Use a broad-spectrum sunscreen on a daily basis to protect your skin from UV rays. Sun exposure can darken and make existing scars more visible.

  • Avoid Harsh Scrubs: Harsh exfoliants and abrasive scrubs can aggravate inflammation and potentially harm the skin.

  • Hydration: Use a non-comedogenic moisturiser to keep your skin hydrated. Scarring is less likely in hydrated skin.

  • Treatment for Early Hyperpigmentation: If you notice dark spots (post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation) after a pimple heals, use products containing niacinamide or vitamin C to help fade these marks.

  • Avoid Using Harsh Acne Treatments: Certain acne treatments, such as strong chemical peels or aggressive extractions, can cause scarring.  Consult a dermatologist about treatment options to ensure the best approach for your skin.

  • Limit Sun Exposure: Prolonged sun exposure can cause scars to darken. Wear protective clothing and apply sunscreen if you must be outside in the sun.

  • Professional Care: If you have severe or cystic acne, or if you are prone to scarring, see a dermatologist as soon as possible. They can provide customised treatments and advice to help reduce scarring.

  • Topical Treatments: If you have a history of acne scarring, use products containing retinoids, alpha hydroxy acids (AHAs), or beta hydroxy acids (BHAs) to promote skin cell turnover and reduce the appearance of scars. 

  • Microneedling and Laser Therapy: Dermatological procedures such as microneedling and laser therapy can be used to improve the appearance of acne scars in some cases.

Although not all scars can be completely avoided, early intervention and proper skincare can significantly reduce the risk and improve the appearance of existing scars. If you are concerned about acne scars, see a dermatologist for the best treatment options for your skin.
