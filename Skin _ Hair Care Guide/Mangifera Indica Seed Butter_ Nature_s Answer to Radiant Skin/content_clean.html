From Tropical Groves to Skincare Shelves: The Journey

Mango butter travels from tropical groves to skincare shelves via several stages of cultivation, extraction, processing, and formulation.  Here's a timeline of mango butter's development:

  • Cultivation: Mangoes (Mangifera indica) are native to tropical and subtropical locations around the world, particularly in India, Thailand, the Philippines, and other portions of Africa and South America. The mango fruit matures and is harvested during its season, usually when it is ripe. Mango fruit seeds or kernels are collected for further processing.

  • Extraction and Processing: The seeds, or kernels, of the mango fruit are removed. These seeds contain the fatty kernels that are used to make mango butter. There is a process used to remove the butter from the kernels. In general, the kernels are cold-pressed or expeller-pressed to extract the natural fat or oil content. To remove contaminants or undesired components, the extracted butter may be refined. Some manufacturers may choose unrefined mango butter in order to preserve more of its natural characteristics.

  • Skincare Formulation: Mango butter is extracted and used as a significant ingredient in a variety of skincare products. It can be found in creams, lotions, balms, serums, masks, and other types of skincare products. Before mixing mango butter with other natural components or compounds to produce skincare formulations, skincare firms thoroughly analyse its quality and purity.

  • Packaging and Distribution: Mango butter-containing skincare products are packaged in accordance with the manufacturer's specifications. These items can be packaged in jars, bottles, tubes, or other appropriate containers. After that, finished skincare products are distributed through a variety of channels, such as retail stores, online platforms, speciality skincare boutiques, and directly through the manufacturer's outlets.

  • Consumer Use: End users buy mango butter-containing skincare products and incorporate them into their everyday skincare routines. These products moisturise, nourish, and provide other skincare advantages.

Comparing Synthetic Moisturisers and Natural Seed Butters


Both natural seed butters, such as mango butter, and synthetic moisturisers have unique qualities and advantages when it comes to skincare ingredients. The two are contrasted as follows:

Synthetic Moisturisers:

  • Ingredients: Lab-created substances such as mineral oils, silicones (dimethicone), glycerin, and different synthetic polymers are frequently found in synthetic moisturisers.

  • Texture and Absorption: They might be formulated with lighter textures for rapid skin absorption, giving you instant hydration without leaving you feeling oily.

  • Emollient Properties: Synthetic moisturisers provide hydration at the surface, softening the skin by creating a barrier that keeps moisture from escaping.

  • Potential Drawbacks: Certain synthetic moisturisers may include chemicals or additives that, in some cases, can irritate or cause skin sensitivity in sensitive skin types. Some synthetic compounds can raise environmental concerns when used over an extended period of time.

Natural Seed Butters (e.g., Mango Butter):

  • Ingredients: Natural seed butters, which come from fruit or seed kernels, are a good source of healthy nutrients like antioxidants, fatty acids, and vitamins A, C, and E.

  • Texture and Absorption: Compared to synthetic moisturisers, natural seed butters often have richer textures and may take longer to absorb into the skin. They give the skin intense hydration and nourishment.

  • Emollient and Nutrient-Rich: These butters provide not only moisture but also a variety of skin-beneficial nutrients, such as antioxidants that fight free radicals, vitamins that support collagen production, and fatty acids that improve skin elasticity.

  • Compatibility: Because of their natural makeup, natural seed butters are typically well-tolerated by a wide range of skin types, including sensitive skin. Nonetheless, patch tests should be done on people who have particular allergies to certain natural ingredients.

While synthetic moisturisers provide fast hydration and a smooth texture, natural seed butter like mango butter provide deep nourishment, antioxidants, and additional skin benefits due to their natural composition. When choosing between the two, it typically comes down to personal choice and skin sensitivity.

DIY Skincare Recipes with Mangifera Indica Seed Butter

Mangifera Indica Seed Butter, often known as mango seed butter, is a nutrient-dense substance that may be utilised in a variety of DIY skincare preparations. Here are a few quick and easy recipes that use mango seed butter:

  1. Mango Seed Butter Body Butter: Ingredients: 1/2 cup mango seed butter, 1/4 cup coconut oil, and 1/4 cup shea butter. Optional: a few drops of your favourite essential oil (lavender, chamomile, etc.). Instructions: Melt mango seed butter, coconut oil, and shea butter in a double boiler until completely melted and combined. Remove from the heat and set aside to cool slightly. Add essential oil if desired for fragrance. Fill a clean container or jar halfway with the mixture. Allow it to solidify at room temperature or in the refrigerator for a few hours. To moisturise dry skin, use this luxurious body butter. Apply as needed.

  2. Nourishing Mango Seed Butter Lip Balm: Ingredients: 2 tablespoons mango seed butter, 1 tablespoon beeswax, and 1 tablespoon coconut oil. Optional: a drop of vitamin E oil or a few drops of essential oil (for a refreshing scent, try peppermint). Instructions: In a heatproof container or jar, combine mango seed butter, beeswax, and coconut oil. Melt the ingredients in a double boiler until thoroughly combined. Remove from heat and, if using, add vitamin E oil or essential oil for additional benefits or fragrance. Fill lip balm containers or small jars halfway with the mixture. Before using, allow it to cool and solidify. Apply this homemade lip balm to your lips to moisturise and protect them. 

  3. Mango Seed Butter Face Mask: Ingredients: 1 tablespoon raw honey, 2 tablespoons mango seed butter, and 1 teaspoon (optional) plain yoghurt. Instructions: In a bowl, soften mango seed butter by gently heating or allowing it to reach room temperature. Mix in the raw honey and yoghurt (if using) until well combined. Allow the mixture to sit on clean, dry skin for 15-20 minutes. Rinse your skin with lukewarm water and pat it dry. This homemade face mask has moisturising and soothing properties.

Before using any new homemade skincare product, always perform a patch test to ensure there are no allergic reactions. These DIY recipes can be adjusted based on personal preferences and skin needs, providing natural and nourishing care for your skin.
