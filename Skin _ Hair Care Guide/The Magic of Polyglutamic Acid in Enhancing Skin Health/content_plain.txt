Introduction to Polyglutamic Acid
Polyglutamic acid (PGA) is a naturally occurring biopolymer that has gained popularity in the beauty sector due to its skin-hydrating and skin-improving characteristics. It is made up of repeating units of the amino acid glutamic acid linked together in a chain-like structure and is also known as gamma-polyglutamic acid, or -PGA. This unique biopolymer is produced through a fermentation process involving particular types of bacteria, including Bacillus subtilis. The resulting polyglutamic acid has a number of excellent qualities that make it a useful component in skincare products. It is also water-soluble.
One of the most amazing properties of polyglutamic acid is its outstanding capacity to retain water. It has a far higher moisture capacity than other regularly used humectants, such as hyaluronic acid. Because of this, PGA is extremely effective at hydrating the skin by locking in moisture, promoting a plump, smooth, and youthful appearance. Furthermore, polyglutamic acid aids in the strengthening of the skin's natural barrier, lowering transepidermal water loss (TEWL). A strong skin barrier is required to protect the skin from external stresses while also maintaining adequate moisture levels.
Defining Polyglutamic Acid
Polyglutamic acid (PGA) is a naturally occurring biopolymer made up of numerous units of glutamic acid linked together in a chain-like form. It has a high molecular weight and is water soluble and biodegradable. PGA is created through the fermentation of particular strains of bacteria, most notably Bacillus subtilis. PGA is synthesised and secreted into the environment by bacteria during fermentation.
Polyglutamic acid has extraordinary water-retaining characteristics due to its unusual structure, capable of holding several times its weight in water. This makes it an effective humectant, which means it can attract and retain moisture. As a result, PGA is extensively utilised in skincare products to offer high hydration to the skin, assisting in the maintenance of plump, smooth, and supple skin. Polyglutamic acid has also been shown to increase the skin's natural barrier function. It lowers transepidermal water loss (TEWL), which is the evaporation of water through the skin, by improving the skin barrier. A stronger skin barrier protects the skin from external environmental elements while also preventing moisture loss, producing healthier and better-moisturized skin.

Similarities and Differences
When it comes to their properties and applications in skincare, polyglutamic acid (PGA) and hyaluronic acid (HA) have significant parallels and distinctions. Let's take a closer look at these:
Similarities
:
Hydration Power
: Both PGA and HA are humectants, which means they can attract and retain moisture. They can hold a lot of water, which provides intense hydration to the skin and keeps it plump and moisturised.
Skin Barrier Support
: Both ingredients help to improve the natural barrier function of the skin. They aid in the reduction of transepidermal water loss (TEWL), or the evaporation of water from the skin's surface, resulting in improved moisture retention and a healthier skin barrier.
Non-Irritating
: Most skin types, including sensitive skin, tolerate both PGA and HA well. They have a low irritancy potential, making them appropriate for people with sensitive or reactive skin.
Lightweight Texture
: Both ingredients have a lightweight, non-greasy texture that allows them to absorb quickly into the skin without leaving a heavy or sticky residue.
Anti-Aging Benefits
: Both PGA and HA can help with anti-aging by improving skin elasticity and reducing the appearance of fine lines and wrinkles.
Differences
:
Molecular Structure
: PGA and HA have different molecular structures. HA is a glycosaminoglycan, which is a large, high-molecular-weight sugar molecule. PGA, on the other hand, is a biopolymer with a relatively high molecular weight composed of multiple units of the amino acid glutamic acid linked together.
Water Retention Capacity
: While both PGA and HA are excellent water-retaining agents, PGA has been reported to hold more water than HA. PGA's water-holding capacity is greater, making it potentially even more effective in providing hydration to the skin.
Origin and Source
: HA is a naturally occurring compound found in the skin, joints, and other tissues of the body. It is commonly derived from rooster combs or produced through bacterial fermentation. PGA, on the other hand, is produced through bacterial fermentation, typically from soybeans or other plant-based materials.
Availability and Use in Skincare
: For a long time, HA has been a well-established and widely used skincare ingredient, appearing in a variety of skincare products such as serums, moisturisers, and masks. Although PGA is a relatively new entrant in the skincare industry and has grown in popularity in recent years, it may still be less common in skincare formulations than HA.
