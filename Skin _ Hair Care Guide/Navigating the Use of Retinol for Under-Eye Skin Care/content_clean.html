Safety Measures for Applying Retinol Under Eyes

Applying retinol under the eyes necessitates specific precautions to ensure its effectiveness without causing irritation or harm to the delicate skin in that area. Here are some precautions to take when using retinol under the eyes: 

  • Begin at a Low Concentration:  Begin with a low-concentration retinol product (around 0.1% or less) designed specifically for the eye area. If your skin tolerates it well, gradually increase the concentration or frequency.

  • Perform a Patch Test: Before applying the retinol product near your eyes, test it on a small area of skin elsewhere on your face to check for any adverse reactions or irritation.

  • Cleanse and Dry Skin: Before applying retinol, make sure the skin around your eyes is clean and completely dry.  This reduces the possibility of irritation.

  • Use a Small Amount: Using your ring finger, gently pat a pea-sized amount of the product into the orbital bone area.  To prevent irritation, avoid getting too close to the eyes or applying directly to the eyelids.

  • Avoid Harsh Rubbing: When applying retinol under the eyes, be gentle.  To avoid irritation, pat or lightly dab the product into the skin rather than vigorously rubbing it in.

  • Moisturise and protect: Apply a gentle, hydrating eye cream or moisturiser after the retinol has been fully absorbed.  During the day, always use sunscreen to protect the treated area from the sun, as retinol can increase skin sensitivity to UV rays.

  • Begin Slowly and Monitor Skin Reaction: Apply retinol to the under-eye area once or twice a week at first, gradually increasing frequency if your skin tolerates it well.  Keep an eye out for any signs of redness, dryness, or irritation on your skin.

  • Consider Alternating Days: To give the skin a break and minimise potential irritation, some people find it helpful to alternate retinol nights with nights when they use a different eye cream or moisturiser.

  • Avoid Combining with Specific Ingredients: Retinol may have a negative interaction with certain ingredients.  To avoid too much irritation, do not use products with benzoyl peroxide, salicylic acid, or other strong active ingredients at the same time as retinol.

  • Seek Advice from an Expert:  If you experience persistent irritation, redness, or discomfort, or if you are unsure about using retinol for your under-eye area, seek advice from a dermatologist or skincare professional. 

Remember that the skin around the eyes is especially sensitive, so proceed with caution when using retinol in this area to avoid adverse reactions.

Benefits and Risks of Retinol for Eye Skin

Retinol has several benefits for the skin around the eyes, but it also has some risks due to the sensitive nature of the area. The following are the advantages and disadvantages of using retinol for eye skin:

Benefits:

  • Diminishment of Fine Lines and Wrinkles: Retinol promotes the formation of collagen and quickens cell turnover, which over time can help reduce the appearance of fine lines and wrinkles around the eyes.

  • Enhancement of Skin Texture: Applying retinol on a regular basis can smooth out rough areas and encourage a more even tone, which helps to minimise the appearance of uneven texture.

  • Reduction of Dark Circle Appearance: Retinol's capacity to promote cell turnover may aid in mitigating the visibility of dark circles or discoloration beneath the eyes.

  • Enhanced Firmness and Elasticity: Retinol can make skin firmer and more elastic by promoting the production of collagen, which can give the skin a more youthful appearance.

Risks:

  • Skin irritation: The delicate, thin skin surrounding the eyes is more vulnerable to irritation when using retinol.  If applied incorrectly or in excess of recommended dosage, it may result in redness, dryness, flakiness, or a burning feeling.

Dispelling Myths About Retinol Use Under the Eyes

There are a number of myths and misconceptions about the use of retinol under the eyes.  Here, I'll dispel some common myths about retinol use in this delicate area:

  • Myth 1: Retinol should not be used under the eyes. Reality: Retinol can be used under the eyes, but it requires a cautious approach due to the area's sensitivity. Lower concentrations specifically formulated for the eye area can be effective when used properly.

  • Myth 2: Retinol causes thinning of the skin under the eyes. Reality: When used in appropriate concentrations and applied correctly, retinol doesn’t significantly thin the skin. Instead, it can stimulate collagen production, improving skin thickness and texture over time.

  • Myth 3: Retinol worsens under-eye puffiness. Reality: Retinol itself doesn't directly cause under-eye puffiness. However, if applied too close to the eyes or in excessive amounts, it can potentially cause irritation, leading to temporary puffiness.

  • Myth 4: Retinol makes the under-eye area more sensitive to light. Reality: While retinol can increase skin sensitivity, there isn't specific evidence that it heightens sensitivity to light in the under-eye area. Nonetheless, it's essential to use sunscreen during the day as retinol can make the skin more susceptible to UV damage.

  • Myth 5: Retinol can't address under-eye concerns like dark circles. Reality: Retinol can indirectly help improve the appearance of dark circles by promoting cell turnover and skin renewal, which may result in a more even skin tone. However, it might not completely eliminate dark circles caused by genetics or other factors.

  • Myth 6: Retinol should not be applied near the lash line. Reality: It's generally advised to avoid applying retinol too close to the lash line to prevent potential irritation or contact with the eyes. Application along the orbital bone area is typically recommended.

  • Myth 7: Retinol should not be used with other eye products. Reality: Retinol can be used alongside hydrating and gentle eye creams or serums. Pairing it with hydrating ingredients can counteract potential dryness or irritation.

  • Myth 8: Retinol is not suitable for all skin types around the eyes. Reality: While retinol might not be suitable for extremely sensitive skin types, using lower concentrations or alternatives like bakuchiol can be beneficial for many individuals around the eye area.

It's important to note that the use of retinol around the eyes should be approached cautiously, and individual skin responses may vary. Always start with a patch test and gradually introduce retinol to minimize potential irritation or adverse reactions. Consulting a dermatologist can provide personalized advice for your specific skin concerns and needs.
