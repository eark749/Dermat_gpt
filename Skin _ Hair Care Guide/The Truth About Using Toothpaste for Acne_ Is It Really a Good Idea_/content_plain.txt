Not designed for the skin

: Toothpaste contains ingredients that can be harsh and irritating to the skin. Potential for skin damage

There are safer options available

Where Did the Toothpaste and Acne Myth Originate?
It is thought that a combination of anecdotal evidence, false information, and a lack of readily available acne remedies in the past gave rise to the toothpaste and acne myth. Word-of-mouth marketing and online discussion boards helped spread the myth that toothpaste may effectively heal acne. However, there is no evidence or credible source to support the claim. The idea might have originated because toothpaste contains substances like alcohol, hydrogen peroxide, and baking soda, which can dry out the skin. These chemicals may momentarily lessen the size and redness of a pimple when administered. Additionally, some people may have thought toothpaste could treat acne due to its capacity to absorb oil. Additionally, toothpaste frequently produces a cooling sensation when applied, which may provide a brief relief from the discomfort caused by inflammatory acne lesions. This sensation might be interpreted as a sign of success. The notion might have been strengthened in the past because there wasn't readily available information about acne remedies. Prior to the widespread availability of the Internet, individuals depended more on homemade cures and word-of-mouth recommendations for common conditions like acne. This helped spread the idea about toothpaste and acne by working in tandem with anecdotal accounts of people who claimed to have seen results.
Why Do People Believe Toothpaste Can Help Acne?
There are a few reasons why people might think toothpaste can treat acne, despite the lack of any supporting scientific data. Here are some explanations that might apply:
Anecdotal evidence
: Individual anecdotes and tales from people who assert that toothpaste helped clear up their acne can be convincing. When people hear or read about someone getting good results, they might be tempted to try it themselves in the hopes of getting similar results.
Temporary drying effect
: Baking soda, hydrogen peroxide, and alcohol are frequently found in toothpaste and can temporarily dry out the skin. These ingredients can temporarily lessen redness and inflammation when applied to a pimple, making the pimple appear less noticeable.  People may assume that toothpaste works well to treat acne based on immediate visual improvement.
Accessibility and cost
: Toothpaste is a typical household item that is readily available and reasonably priced. Before turning to specialized acne treatments, people may turn to readily available home remedies when they experience an acne breakout. In these situations, toothpaste might seem like a practical solution.
Confirmation bias
: When people interpret and recall information in a way that supports their preexisting beliefs or expectations, confirmation bias takes place. If someone thinks toothpaste can treat acne, they might selectively recall the times it seemed to work and ignore or forget the times it didn't.
Lack of knowledge or alternatives
: People may turn to trying unconventional remedies like toothpaste if there isn't readily available and trustworthy information about how to
treat acne
.  Before accurate skincare information was widely available, home remedies and folk remedies were frequently used because there were few other options.
It's important to keep in mind that these justifications for toothpaste's ability to treat acne are based on anecdotal evidence and individual stories.  Dermatologists frequently suggest products with chemicals like
salicylic acid
or
benzoyl peroxide
, which have been scientifically proven to be excellent acne remedies.
Understanding the Ingredients of Toothpaste
Knowing the typical ingredients used in toothpaste formulations will help you better understand the possible effects of toothpaste on the skin. Despite being primarily intended for oral hygiene, toothpaste may contain the following ingredients:
Abrasives
: Materials like hydrated silica, calcium carbonate, or baking soda are frequently found in toothpaste. These components aid in cleaning teeth of plaque, stains, and food residue. They might irritate or even cause microtears on the face because they are too harsh for delicate skin there.
Fluoride
: By fortifying tooth enamel, fluoride, a vital component of toothpaste, helps guard teeth against decay.  Although fluoride is good for oral health, it can be toxic if ingested or absorbed through the skin in toothpaste at high concentrations.
Surfactants
: To create foam and help the product spread, toothpaste contains surfactants like sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS) or sodium laureth sulfate (SLES). Particularly for people with sensitive skin or skin that is prone to acne, these surfactants may be drying and potentially irritating to the skin.
Flavoring agents
: To provide a pleasant taste and fresh breath, toothpaste contains various flavoring agents such as spearmint, peppermint, or fruit extracts. While these additives are safe to consume, they may not be suitable for use on the skin because they can cause irritation or allergic reactions.
Humectants and binders
: Humectants, such as glycerin or sorbitol, are commonly used in toothpaste formulations to help retain moisture and keep the product from drying out. Binders such as carrageenan and cellulose gum are also used to give toothpaste its distinctive texture. While these ingredients are generally safe for oral use, they are not intended for skin application.
How Can Toothpaste Ingredients Affect Your Skin?
Toothpaste ingredients can have a variety of effects on your skin, and it's important to be aware of them. Here are some of the ways toothpaste ingredients can harm your skin:
Skin irritation
: Abrasives and surfactants like sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS) in toothpaste can be harsh and irritating to the skin. They may cause redness, dryness, and itching by interfering with the skin's natural barrier function. These effects may be more pronounced in people with sensitive or easily irritated skin.
Allergic reactions
: Toothpaste formulations frequently contain flavoring agents, preservatives, or other additives that can cause allergic reactions in some people.  These reactions can cause skin redness, itching, swelling, and even rashes.
Dryness and dehydration
: Toothpaste contains ingredients such as abrasives, humectants, and surfactants that can contribute to skin dryness and dehydration. Excessive drying of the skin can disrupt its moisture balance, resulting in increased sensitivity and the possibility of skin barrier damage.
Acne or breakouts
: Some toothpaste ingredients, such as fluoride or surfactants such as SLS, have been linked to acne breakouts in some people.  These ingredients have the potential to clog pores, causing new blemishes to form or exacerbating existing acne.
Skin pH disruption
: The pH level of toothpaste is typically higher (more alkaline) than the skin's optimal pH level. Prolonged or repeated application of toothpaste to the skin can disrupt the skin's natural pH balance, compromising the skin's barrier function and potentially leading to a variety of skin problems.
It's important to remember that everyone reacts differently to the ingredients in toothpaste. While some people may not experience any negative consequences, others may be allergic to or sensitive to certain components. Before applying toothpaste or any other new substance to the face, it is always a good idea to patch-test a tiny area of skin. The best course of action is to stop using the product and get individual guidance from a dermatologist if you have any unpleasant reactions or concerns.

Professional Alternatives to Home Remedies for Acne
Dermatologists advise using one of the many medical options for home treatments for acne. These therapies have been shown to be effective in treating acne and are backed up by scientific evidence. Here are a few professional acne treatments that are frequently recommended:
Topical Retinoids
: Retinoids derived from vitamin A, such as tretinoin, adapalene, and tazarotene, are known for their ability to unclog pores,
reduce inflammation

Benzoyl peroxide
: Because of its antibacterial properties, benzoyl peroxide is a useful component in the treatment of acne. It helps eliminate acne-causing bacteria, lessens inflammation, and stops new breakouts from developing. It comes in a variety of formulations and concentrations, such as gels, creams, and cleansers.
Salicylic Acid
: Salicylic acid is a beta-hydroxy acid that exfoliates the skin and unclogs clogged pores.  It assists with dead skin cell removal, oiliness reduction, and the prevention of new acne lesions. In acne treatment products like cleansers, toners, and spot treatments, salicylic acid is frequently present.
Oral medications
: To help control acne-causing bacteria or hormonal imbalances, dermatologists may prescribe oral medications for patients with moderate to severe acne, such as oral antibiotics (such as tetracycline, doxycycline) or hormonal therapies (such as oral contraceptives, spironolactone).
Chemical Peels
: A chemical solution is applied to the skin during a chemical peel to exfoliate the top layer and help clear clogged pores. They can lessen acne lesions, enhance the texture of the skin, and encourage a more uniform complexion. The usual setting for chemical peels is a dermatologist's office.
Laser and Light Therapies
: A number of laser and light-based treatments, like photodynamic therapy or blue light therapy, can target the bacteria that cause acne, lessen swelling, and speed up the healing process of the skin. The usual setting for these procedures is a dermatologist's office.

How to Properly Care for Acne-Prone Skin
A dependable and gentle skincare routine is necessary for acne-prone skin care. Here are some recommendations for effective acne-prone skin care:
Cleaning
: Use a mild, non-comedogenic cleanser to wash your face twice daily. Avoid using abrasive cleansers or using harsh rubbing motions that can aggravate acne and irritate the skin. Look for cleansers with ingredients like salicylic acid or benzoyl peroxide, which can aid in clearing clogged pores and lowering acne-causing bacteria.
Moisturizing
: To keep your skin hydrated, use a non-comedogenic moisturizer, even if you have oily or acne-prone skin. Look for moisturizers that won't clog pores that are oil-free or gel-based. A healthy skin barrier can be preserved and excessive oil production can be avoided with hydrated skin.
Avoid abrasive products
: Avoid abrasive or harsh products that can irritate the skin. This includes abrasive exfoliators, toners with alcohol, and rich, greasy moisturizers. Choose gentle, fragrance-free products designed especially for skin that is prone to acne or is sensitive.
Sun protection
: Use a broad-spectrum sunscreen with at least SPF 30 to shield your skin from the sun's harmful rays.  Look for formulas that are oil-free and non-comedogenic to avoid clogged pores.  Sunscreen is necessary because some acne medications can make people more sensitive to the sun and because prolonged exposure to the sun without protection can cause post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation and other skin damage.
Hands off
: Refrain from touching or picking at acne lesions as doing so can exacerbate inflammation, spread bacteria, and raise the possibility of scarring. In order to get rid of any bacteria that might contact your skin, make sure to regularly clean your phone.
Avoid pore-clogging ingredients
: Avoid using skincare or cosmetic products containing pore-clogging ingredients such as lanolin, mineral oil, or certain silicones. These have the potential to aggravate acne. Look for non-comedogenic or oil-free products.
Professional advice
: If over-the-counter acne treatments aren't working, consider seeing a dermatologist. They can give personalized advice, prescribe medications, and recommend professional treatments like chemical peels or laser therapies.
Patience and consistency
: Your skincare routine requires patience. Results may take time, and using the recommended products consistently is essential. Avoid switching products all the time because it can upset your skin's balance and make it difficult to figure out what works for you.
Since every individual has a different type of skin, what works for one person might not work for another. It's better to speak with a dermatologist for specialized guidance and suggestions catered to your individual skin needs and issues.
Tips for Preventing Acne Breakouts
Acne prevention entails following a consistent skincare routine and making certain lifestyle changes. Here are some pointers to help you avoid acne breakouts:
Cleanse your face twice a day
: Wash your face in the morning and evening with a gentle, non-comedogenic cleanser. Scrubbing too hard can irritate the skin and cause breakouts.
Avoid touching your face
: Touching your face could spread bacteria and oils from your hands to your skin, clogging pores and possibly causing breakouts. Throughout the day, try to avoid touching your face.
Keep your hair clean
: Oily hair, particularly along the hairline, can contribute to acne breakouts. Wash your hair on a regular basis, especially if it is greasy, and keep it away from your face.
Use non-comedogenic products
: Look for skincare and cosmetics that are
non-comedogenic
, which means they won't clog pores. To help prevent breakouts, look for oil-free or water-based formulas.
Remove your makeup before going to bed
: Sleeping with makeup on can clog pores and cause breakouts. Use a gentle cleanser or makeup remover to thoroughly remove all makeup before going to bed.
Maintain a healthy diet
: While the relationship between diet and acne is complex and varies from person to person, some studies suggest that certain foods, such as high glycemic index foods and dairy products, may contribute to acne breakouts. Keep an eye on how your skin reacts to different foods and consider making dietary changes if you notice a link.
Manage your stress levels
: Stress can aggravate acne breakouts.  To help reduce stress, use stress-management techniques such as exercise, meditation, deep breathing, or engaging in activities you enjoy.
Avoid overexposure to the sun
: While sun exposure can temporarily improve acne, prolonged exposure can cause skin damage and increased oil production. Wear sunscreen and avoid excessive sun exposure to protect your skin.
Do not pick or squeeze acne lesions
: Picking or squeezing acne lesions can cause inflammation, scarring, and the spread of bacteria. It is best to leave them alone and allow them to heal naturally, or to seek professional extraction assistance.
Consult a dermatologist
: If you have persistent or severe acne breakouts, you should see a dermatologist. They can make personalized recommendations, prescribe medication if necessary, and recommend professional acne treatments.
Keep in mind that preventing acne outbreaks requires perseverance and consistency. It might take some time to see results, so be patient and make the necessary lifestyle changes.

Over-the-Counter Products for Acne
Acne can be treated with a number of over-the-counter (OTC) medications. These products can still be useful for treating mild to moderate acne, even if they might not be as potent as prescription drugs. Here are several popular over-the-counter acne remedies:
Benzoyl peroxide
: This substance is frequently used in acne treatments. It eliminates acne-causing bacteria and regulates excessive oil production. Cleansers, creams, gels, and spot treatments are just a few of the different strengths and forms of benzoyl peroxide that are offered (2.5% to 10%). To avoid any potential dryness or irritation, start with a lower concentration and increase it gradually as tolerated.
Salicylic Acid
: Salicylic acid, a beta-hydroxy acid, aids in skin exfoliation and pore cleaning. Blackheads and whiteheads can be effectively treated with it in particular. Numerous over-the-counter (OTC) items, such as cleansers, toners, pads, and spot treatments, contain salicylic acid. To prevent excessive dryness or irritation, use the product in accordance with the directions.
Sulfur
: A natural substance with antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties is sulfur. It aids in pore cleaning, oil control, and acne improvement. Sulfur can be found in a variety of products, including cleansers, masks, and spot treatments. Although some products may have a distinctive smell, most people can tolerate it.
Alpha Hydroxy Acids (AHAs)
: Exfoliating agents that help remove dead skin cells and improve skin texture include glycolic acid and lactic acid. They can help unclog pores and reduce the appearance of acne. AHAs can be found in many over-the-counter products, including cleansers, toners, serums, and peels.  Increase the concentration gradually as tolerated after a lower starting point.
Tea tree oil
: Tea tree oil contains antimicrobial qualities and can help lessen acne-related irritation. It comes in a range of skincare products or can be used as a spot treatment in diluted form. Tea tree oil should always be used diluted, as it can be harsh and irritate the skin when used undiluted.
Retinol
: When compared to retinoids prescribed by a doctor, retinol is a milder form of vitamin A.  It encourages
skin cell renewal
, clears clogged pores, and lessens acne outbreaks. Many over-the-counter (OTC) items, including serums, creams, and treatments, contain retinol. Retinol can make you more sensitive to the sun, so start with a lower concentration and gradually increase as tolerated.

A dermatologist should be consulted if your acne is severe, chronic, or not responding to over-the-counter remedies for further assessment and advice.
