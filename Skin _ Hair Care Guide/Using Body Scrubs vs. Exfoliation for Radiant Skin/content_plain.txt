Why is Exfoliation Important for Skin?
Exfoliation, the process of shedding old skin cells, is essential for skin health. As the largest organ of the body, the skin undergoes a natural renewal cycle, moving the cells from lower layers of skin to the surface. This process slows down with age and it leads to a buildup of dead cells, often exacerbated by the consistent use of topical skincare products, resulting in clogged pores and potential skin issues like acne.
Old, unhealthy skin cells contribute to a lacklustre, dry, and rough appearance, whereas exfoliated skin appears smoother, tighter, and more vibrant. With daily application of makeup and skincare products, the face can undergo stress, accelerating signs of ageing like fine lines, wrinkles, and a dull complexion. Moreover, dry skin can trigger increased oil production as the body attempts to compensate for the lack of moisture, potentially leading to clogged pores and acne.
Therefore, regular exfoliation is key to maintaining a healthy skin cell turnover, ensuring a radiant, smooth complexion while also aiding to mitigate common skin concerns.

How to Choose Frequency & Timing of Exfoliation
To optimise the benefits of exfoliation while minimising potential drawbacks, it's often a matter of trial and error to find the right balance for your skin type. Here's an expanded guide on how to approach exfoliation based on different skin types:
Normal Skin:
Individuals with normal skin are generally recommended to begin exfoliating about two to three times a week. This frequency typically provides the benefits of exfoliation, such as smoother skin and unclogged pores, without causing irritation.
Dry Skin:
People with dry or sensitive skin should exercise caution and limit exfoliation to a maximum of twice a week so as to prevent excess drying out of the already sensitive skin. If using products like
retinoids
that can further dry out and irritate the skin, consider reducing the frequency to once a week or even every other week.
Sensitive Skin:
For individuals with rosacea, eczema, exfoliation may often cause more harm than good. In such cases, dermatologists typically advise against exfoliating to prevent exacerbating redness, irritation, or stinging associated with the condition.
Combination Skin:
Those with combination skin, which features areas of both oiliness and dryness, can usually safely exfoliate twice a week. This helps in balancing the skin, addressing both the oily zones and the drier patches effectively.
Oily Skin:
People with oily skin may find that they can exfoliate more frequently, approximately two to three times a week. In some cases, depending on the reaction of their skin and the methods or products used, they might even increase the frequency slightly.
However, if you are using an exfoliating cleanser daily, such as
Cetaphil Daily Exfoliating Cleanser
, you might not need additional exfoliation. It is recommended to always observe your skin’s response to exfoliation. If you notice dryness, redness, irritation, or breakouts, it is indicative of indulging in excessive exfoliation. In such a scenario, it is recommended to step down on the exfoliation. Furthermore, it’s crucial to avoid exfoliating areas with sunburn, rashes, or open cuts, as this can lead to further skin damage.
If you’re unsure, or if your skin has specific conditions like rosacea, eczema,
consulting with a dermatologist
is always the best choice as they can provide personalised advice and help you find the most suitable exfoliation routine for your skin type.
Understanding Body Scrubs
The body undergoes a process called Desquamation wherein the cells of the upper layer of the skin naturally get loosened and in turn get shed off. To facilitate the removal of these dead skin cells, usually physical scrubs are employed.
Physical scrubs are an ideal choice for exfoliation of the body as the microbeads and particles present in these scrubs ensure effective removal of the dead skin cells. The body has relatively thicker skin than that of the face. As a result, the chances of micro tears due to abrasive action of physical scrubs is quite minimal. Also, due to continuous friction in various body parts such as elbows and knees, hyperpigmentation generally develops. In such a scenario, regular exfoliation becomes more important, so as to remove dead and keratinized skin.
Types of Body Scrubs and Their Benefits
All the body scrubs, though similar in way they act, yet perform differently due to differences in the basic composition.
Salt Scrubs:
Salt is a natural abrasive and it removes dead skin cells very gently. Also, few researches point out in the direction that Magnesium present in dead sea water scrub enhances the barrier function of the skin. As a result, hydration level of the skin improved significantly, and transepidermal water loss decreased, especially in people suffering from Atopic Dermatitis or Eczema.
Sugar Scrubs:
Sugar particles are a natural abrasive that ensure dead cell removal in a mild way. Also, sugar, being a humectant, tends to absorb water from the surroundings, as a result, it not only removes the dead skin cells but also hydrates and
moisturises the skin.
Coffee Scrubs:
Coffee is a reservoir of antioxidants. When used as a scrub, it not only removes the dead skin cells in a delicate way, but also decreases the damage caused by free radicals on the skin surface. A component present in coffee, referred to as caffeic acid, exhibits antibacterial properties. Apart from that, few researches also point towards exhibition of skin protective action against damage caused by UV rays to the skin. It also has cellulite reducing properties as it drastically improves microcirculation and collagen building.
Lavender Scrubs:
Micro particles of lavender apart from removing dead skin cells, also exhibit nerve calming properties. It also exhibits skin soothing and anti-inflammatory action.
Charcoal Body Scrubs:
Activated charcoal shows the property of skin adsorption. It refers to drawing out the skin impurities from the clogged pores to the skin surface, thereby ensuring its effective removal. Apart from that it also absorbs body odour.
How to Choose the Right Body Scrub Ingredients
Not all body scrubs are the same. Their basic ingredient often determines the skin type for which that body scrub is ideal for.
If you have dry and sensitive skin, usually salt scrubs are recommended as these are proven to improve the skin barrier function, hence making it a great choice for people suffering with eczema.
If you have dry skin, usually a milder physical exfoliant, such as lavender scrub is recommended. It is very gentle on the skin as it not only removes the dead skin cells but also moisturises and nourishes the skin due to the presence of lavender oil. It also exhibits skin and nerve calming properties.
If you have an oily skin, charcoal body scrub can add huge value to your skincare regimen due to its adsorption action. It gently pulls out the sebum, dirt and other particles from the clogged pores, thereby reducing the chances of acne breakouts.
If you are blessed with a normal and resilient skin, coffee scrubs are an ideal choice, as these not only gently remove the dead skin cells but also provide an instant and radiant glow to the skin.

Scrubbing vs. Exfoliating: Finding Your Best Fit
Choosing between physical and chemical exfoliation can be a task. To ensure that you pick up the right option, below are a few tips and tricks to aid you in determining the right fit.
Chemical exfoliation ensures gentle removal of debris and dead skin cells. However, choosing the correct chemical is of utmost importance as it has a huge impact on the skin.
It is recommended to do a patch test before using a chemical exfoliant so as to avoid any adverse reaction.
Physical scrubs effectively remove dead skin cells. However, these may cause microtears due to the abrasive nature. Hence, it is recommended to use it on thick skin.
Also, generally skin exfoliation should be done once in 4-7 days. Depending on the type of product, it can be done twice a week or once a week. If the product is mild such as lactic acid based scrub or lavender scrub, it can be used twice in a week. However, if the product contains strong chemicals such as salicylic acid or has coarse granules such as apricot based scrub, it is best to use it once in a week.

Only granular scrub is the actual scrub.
No, while the scrubs containing granules and beads feel great on skin and are a perfect physical scrub, there is an upstream in the influx of chemical scrubs lately. These are usually mild acids, referred to as Alpha Hydroxy-Acids (AHA’s) and Beta Hydroxy-Acids (BHA’s) that remove dead skin cells by weakening the bonds between the skin cells and thereby shedding it off.
Scrubbing and exfoliation should be done twice daily to remove dead skin cells.
No, to ensure daily hygiene, it is recommended to cleanse the face with a face cleanser twice daily. However, scrubbing or exfoliation should only be done twice or thrice in a week as excess scrubbing and exfoliation can compromise the skin barrier.
3. Only the face needs to be scrubbed.
No, while the face is the most visible thing and is to be scrubbed/ exfoliated regularly; other body parts also require some tender love-care. Sun and pollution exposure, friction, physical activities like swimming etc. also take a toll on other body parts such as feet, legs, elbows etc.
4. Body scrubs can be used on the face.
No, the scrubs used on the body are harsh as compared to the facial scrubs. The skin of the face is thin and delicate, hence milder scrubs are recommended for usage
.
5.All the face scrubs are the same.
It’s just the sales stunt of the companies to sell different products for different skin types.No, it is a wrong perception. Using the chemical exfoliant containing salicylic acid on dry skin will further deteriorate the skin barrier and more harm will be done.
