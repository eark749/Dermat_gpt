What is argan oil?

Argan oil is a natural oil that is extracted from the kernels of the argan tree. It is a rare oil that is found only in Morocco and has been used for centuries for its numerous health benefits. Argan oil is rich in antioxidants, fatty acids, and vitamin E, which makes it an excellent natural ingredient for promoting healthy hair growth.

Origins of argan oil

Argan oil is derived from the argan tree (Argania spinosa), which is native to Morocco. The argan tree thrives in the arid conditions of southwestern Morocco, where it is primarily found near the Sous Valley and the Atlas Mountains' foothills. The indigenous Berber people of Morocco have been producing argan oil for centuries through a labor-intensive process. The argan tree's fruit contains kernels from which oil is extracted. Traditionally, local women would use stones to crack open the argan nuts and remove the kernels from their hard shells. This extraction process was completed by hand. The oil is then extracted from the kernels by grinding and pressing them.

Argan oil has been used in a variety of ways throughout history, including in culinary, medicinal, and cosmetic applications. In Moroccan cuisine, argan oil is prized for its nutty flavor and is used in dishes such as couscous, salads, and tagines. The Berber people used argan oil to treat skin conditions, wounds, and rheumatic problems. In recent years, argan oil has gained popularity worldwide for its numerous cosmetic benefits. It contains essential fatty acids, antioxidants, and vitamins, making it extremely nourishing and moisturising to the skin and hair. Argan oil is used in a variety of beauty products, including moisturisers, hair conditioners, serums, and facial oils, because of its ability to hydrate, soften, and protect the skin and hair.

Composition of argan oil

Argan oil is valued for its rich and diverse nutrient profile, which includes fatty acids, vitamins, antioxidants, and other bioactive compounds. Here's an overview of the main components found in argan oil. 

  • Fatty Acids: Argan oil is primarily made up of oleic acid, a monounsaturated omega-9 fatty acid. Oleic acid helps moisturize and nourish the skin, leaving it soft and supple. Argan oil also contains linoleic acid, an essential omega-6 fatty acid. Linoleic acid supports skin barrier function, promotes hydration, and has anti-inflammatory properties. Stearic acid is a saturated fatty acid found in smaller amounts in argan oil. It helps stabilize the oil and contributes to its creamy texture.

  • Tocopherols (Vitamin E): Argan oil is high in tocopherols, including alpha-tocopherol, a kind of vitamin E. Vitamin E is an effective antioxidant that shields the skin from oxidative stress caused by free radicals, UV radiation, and environmental contaminants. It also promotes skin healing and renewal.

  • Polyphenols: Argan oil contains polyphenolic compounds like flavonoids and phenolic acids, which are antioxidants and anti-inflammatory. These compounds help to neutralise free radicals, reduce inflammation, and improve skin health.

  • Phytosterols: Plant-derived compounds that resemble cholesterol and have skin-protective properties. Argan oil contains a variety of phytosterols, including beta-sitosterol, which helps to maintain skin barrier function, increase skin hydration, and reduce inflammation.

  • Squalene: Argan oil contains squalene, a natural moisturiser. It enhances skin hydration, softness, and smoothness by replenishing the lipid barrier and preventing moisture loss.

  • Carotenoids: Carotenoids are pigmented compounds found in plants that have antioxidant properties. Argan oil contains carotenoids like beta-carotene, which protects the skin from oxidative damage and contributes to its vibrant colour.

  • Sterols: Argan oil contains sterols like schottenol and spinasterol, which have anti-inflammatory and emollient effects. Sterols soothe and moisturise the skin, making argan oil ideal for dry, sensitive, or irritated skin.

Extraction process of argan oil

Argan oil extraction is a multi-stage process that Moroccan communities have traditionally carried out by hand. The indigenous Berber populations of Morocco have been extracting argan oil for generations, and it is profoundly interwoven in their cultural legacy. Today, while some portions of the process are still done by hand using ancient methods, modern techniques and machinery are being employed to expedite production and meet the expanding global demand for argan oil. Old methods, on the other hand, continue to be prized for their authenticity and preservation of old knowledge and abilities.

Disadvantages of argan oil for hair

While argan oil is generally considered safe for most people when applied topically, it may cause side effects in some people, particularly those with sensitive skin or allergies. Here are some potential side effects of using argan oil.

  • Skin Irritation: Some people may experience skin irritation or allergic reactions after using argan oil, particularly if they have sensitive skin or are allergic to nuts. Skin irritation symptoms can include redness, itching, burning, or a rash. Before using argan oil, perform a patch test to rule out any adverse reactions.

  • Acne Breakouts: Although argan oil is non-comedogenic and can help regulate oil production in the skin, it may cause acne breakouts in some people, especially if used in excess or if the skin is predisposed to acne. If you have acne-prone skin, use argan oil sparingly and monitor how your skin reacts.

  • Allergic Reactions: Individuals with nut allergies may be allergic to argan oil, as it is derived from the kernels of the argan tree. Argan oil allergies can range from mild to severe, with symptoms including hives, swelling, difficulty breathing, and anaphylaxis. If you have a known nut allergy, it's essential to avoid using argan oil or consult with an allergist before using it.

  • Greasy Hair: Using too much argan oil or applying it too close to the scalp can result in greasy or oily hair, particularly in people with fine or oily hair. Argan oil should be used sparingly and applied to the hair's lengths and ends rather than the roots.

  • Eye Irritation: Avoid putting argan oil in your eyes because it can cause irritation and discomfort. If accidental contact occurs, thoroughly rinse your eyes with water and seek medical attention if the irritation persists.

  • Digestive Issues (Oral Consumption): Although argan oil is typically applied topically, some people may consume it orally for its potential health benefits. However, consuming large amounts of argan oil may result in digestive problems such as nausea, diarrhoea, or abdominal discomfort. Before consuming argan oil orally, exercise caution and seek the advice of a healthcare professional.

  • Sun Sensitivity: When using argan oil topically, some people may become more sensitive to sunlight, particularly if it is applied to exposed skin areas. This may cause sunburn or skin damage. If you plan to use argan oil during the day and will be spending time outside, you should wear protective clothing and sunscreen.

If you use argan oil and encounter any negative reactions or side effects, stop using it right away and seek additional advice from a dermatologist or other healthcare provider. It's essential to listen to your body and adjust your skincare routine accordingly to ensure the safety and effectiveness of argan oil usage for your individual needs.
