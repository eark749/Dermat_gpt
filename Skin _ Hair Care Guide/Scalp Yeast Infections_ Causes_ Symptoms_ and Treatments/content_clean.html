Causes of Scalp Yeast Infections

Candida yeast causes the majority of scalp yeast infections or candidiasis. Candida can thrive in warm, damp conditions. Your scalp contains a natural equilibrium of yeast and bacteria. Bacteria help prevent yeast overgrowth on your skin. Sometimes your body's natural balance of yeast and bacteria becomes disrupted. When this occurs, Candida gets an opportunity to collect. Candida can grow under the following conditions:

  • Climates with high temperatures and humidity.

  • Certain personal grooming products may contain harsh chemicals.

  • Small cuts on your scalp serve as an entryway.

  • Skin conditions include intertrigo and psoriasis.

  • Follicular occlusion syndrome occurs when your hair follicles become clogged with keratin, a protein in your hair. When follicles become blocked, they rupture, resulting in inflammation.

  • Extreme sweating (hyperhidrosis).

What Leads to Yeast Overgrowth on the Scalp?

Fungus or bacteria can enter the scalp through damaged skin or hair follicles, causing infection. Ringworm, folliculitis, and impetigo are among the possible causes. Bacteria cause some common infections, including folliculitis and impetigo.

Recognizing the Symptoms of Scalp Yeast Infections

A scalp yeast infection leads to an itchy rash on the scalp. The rash could be red or purple and take a variety of shapes. The rash may crack and develop crusts. This crusting may lead to hair loss. Additionally, you might notice:

  • The scales are flaky and white.

  • Shedding.

  • Thick, greasy patches in white or yellow.

  • Moisturized areas that are soft and white.

  • Pimples with pus in them, both white and red.

Dietary Considerations for Preventing Yeast Overgrowth

The candida diet limits sweets, wheat, alcohol, and dairy products. Avoid high-sugar fruits, processed or refined fats and oils, and a variety of condiments. The diet seeks to reestablish a healthy balance of yeast and bacteria within your body. Long-term adherence to this restrictive diet can result in dietary shortages. As a result, the candida diet is typically recommended as a short-term therapy, until reintroducing the restricted foods causes symptoms to flare up again.

The candida diet focuses on limiting your carbohydrate intake, including sugar and grain. Candida relies on carbs for energy, and some research suggests that eating a lot of carbohydrates increases the number of Candida in your body. The assumption behind reducing carbs, particularly sugar, is that you will be able to deplete the yeast's energy source. As a result, when following the candida diet, you should limit or avoid the foods listed below:

  • All types of sugar, including honey, maple syrup, and molasses.

  • Fruits high in sugar, such as bananas, mangos, grapes, and dates.

  • Starchy vegetables (potatoes and carrots)

  • Gluten contains wheat, spelt, and rye grains.

  • Certain dairy products, such as cheese and milk

  • Meat that has been processed

  • Packaged foods (such as frozen meals and chips)

  • Fried or junk food.

  • Soda, coffee, fruit juice, energy drinks, caffeine-containing tea, and alcohol

The candida diet, on the other hand, favors low-sugar, low-carb foods that fight inflammation. These foods include:

  • Avocados, lemons, and limes are examples of fruits with low sugar content.

  • Non-starchy vegetables include artichokes, broccoli, kale, and tomatoes.

  • Gluten-free grains include teff, quinoa, and oat bran.

  • Ghee, Kefir, and Probiotic Yogurt

  • Lean meats, like chicken and turkey

  • Salmon and Sardines

  • Eggs

  • Avocado and olives.

  • Low-mold nuts and seeds, including hazelnuts, almonds, and sunflower seeds

  • Coconut, olive, sesame, and flaxseed oils

  • Herbs and spice

  • Herbal teas.

Some research suggests that probiotics are beneficial, so you may want to consider adding probiotic supplements or other probiotic foods to your diet.
