Understanding Trehalose and Its Origin

Trehalose is a disaccharide, which means it is made up of two sugar molecules, and it is found naturally. Numerous creatures, including plants, fungi, bacteria, and some invertebrates, contain it. The term "trehalose" comes from the substance called "trehala manna," which is produced by scale insects and was the original source from which trehalose was extracted.  Trehalose functions as a protective chemical and an essential energy source in nature. It enables organisms to endure extremely stressful situations, such as dehydration, high temperatures, and freezing. Trehalose protects and maintains the integrity of proteins and cellular structures by acting as a stabiliser.

In response to stresses including drought, salt, and low temperatures, plants synthesise trehalose. By preserving cellular water equilibrium and averting protein denaturation, it serves as a protective agent. Numerous plant sources, such as seeds, leaves, fruits, and fungi, contain trehalose. Trehalose is a reserve carbohydrate that fungi, particularly yeast, produce when there is a lack of nutrients or other stress. Fungi may endure adverse conditions thanks to trehalose and continue to thrive whenever favourable conditions are once again present. Additionally, it supports the defence mechanisms of fungal spores.

Natural Sources of Trehalose

Trehalose can be found in a variety of natural sources, most notably plants, fungi, bacteria, and certain invertebrates. The following are a few instances of trehalose found naturally:

  1. Plants: Seeds, leaves, fruits, roots, and other parts of plants are among the sources of trehalose. Trehalose is found in plants such as Selaginella lepidophylla, Myrothamnus flabellifolia, beans, potatoes, and mushrooms.

  2. Fungi: It is known that fungi, including yeast, produce trehalose as a reserve carbohydrate during times of stress or nutrient scarcity. It helps fungi survive harsh conditions and grows when favourable conditions return. Trehalose is found in many fungi, including baker's yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae), mushrooms (such as shiitake and oyster mushrooms), and truffles.

  3. Bacteria: Some bacteria produce and store trehalose as a protective molecule. Examples include certain species of bacteria in the genus Deinococcus, which are known for their resistance to extreme radiation and desiccation. Trehalose enables these bacteria to withstand such harsh conditions.

  4. Invertebrates: Some invertebrates, particularly those that are exposed to environmental stressors like desiccation, contain trehalose. For instance, some insects, like brine shrimp (Artemia), have the capacity to store trehalose as a defence against dehydration.
