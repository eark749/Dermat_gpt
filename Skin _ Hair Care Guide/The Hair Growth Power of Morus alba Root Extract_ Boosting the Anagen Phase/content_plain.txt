Understanding the Anagen Phase and Hair Growth
The anagen phase is one of the most important stages in the

cycle, determining hair length and health. To understand Morus alba root extract's effect on hair growth, you must first understand the anagen phase and the entire hair growth cycle.
Hair grows in three major phases:
Anagen Phase (Growth Phase)
: This phase can last anywhere from 2 to 7 years, depending on genetics, health, and hormonal effects. During the anagen phase, hair follicles actively produce new hair cells. The cells in the root of the hair (the dermal papilla) divide rapidly, resulting in the growth of the hair shaft. The length of the anagen phase determines how long the hair can grow. Approximately 85% of hair on the scalp is in the anagen phase at any given moment.
Catagen Phase (Transition Phase)
: This phase lasts around two to three weeks. Hair development stops and the hair follicle shrinks and separates from the dermal papilla. This is a brief time in which the hair changes from active growth to resting.
Telogen Phase (Resting Phase)
: This phase lasts around three months. Hair does not develop during this time but instead remains in the follicle. Eventually, the old hair falls out, and new hair begins to grow, ushering in a new anagen stage. Normally, 10-15% of hair is in the telogen phase at all times.

Key Features of the Anagen Phase
The anagen phase is the time when hair follicles actively produce hair. This process involves the rapid division of cells in the hair root (dermal papilla), which pulls the hair shaft up and out of the follicle.
The length of the anagen period varies greatly across individuals. It normally lasts between two and seven years. The duration of this phase can be influenced by genetics, health, age, and hormone levels. The length of the anagen phase determines how long hair can grow. This is why some people can grow their hair to extremely long lengths, whereas others may discover that it stops growing at a shorter length.
At any given time, around 85-90% of the hairs on a healthy human scalp are in the anagen phase. This high percentage guarantees continual hair growth and renewal.
The anagen phase has a substantial impact on both hair quality and length. Hair that stays in the anagen phase for an extended period is usually longer and healthier. Shorter anagen stages lead to shorter hair lengths.
The Role of Dermal Papilla Cells in Hair Growth
Dermal papilla cells (DPCs) are responsible for hair development and cycle management. These specialised cells are found at the base of the hair follicle, within the dermal papilla, which is a structure at the follicle's bulb. Understanding the function and significance of dermal papilla cells is critical for understanding how hair grows and how different therapies affect hair growth.
Key Functions of Dermal Papilla Cells
Regulation of Hair Follicle Development
: Dermal papilla cells play an important role in the establishment and growth of hair follicles. During embryogenesis, they interact with epidermal cells to stimulate the formation of hair follicles. These interactions involve signalling pathways that control the differentiation and proliferation of the cells required to generate hair follicles.
Control of the Hair Growth Cycle
: DPCs regulate the hair development cycle, which consists of three phases: anagen (growing), catagen (transition), and telogen (resting). They generate and secrete a variety of signalling chemicals, including growth factors, cytokines, and extracellular matrix components, which impact the behaviour of other cells inside the hair follicle.
Induction of the Anagen Phase
: Dermal papilla cells serve an important role in generating and maintaining the anagen phase. They release growth factors such as IGF-1, FGF, and VEGF, which stimulate the development of keratinocytes in the hair matrix. The anagen phase is distinguished by aggressive hair growth, which is caused by the rapid division and differentiation of these keratinocytes.
Regulation of Hair Shaft Production
: DPCs play a role in regulating hair shaft formation during the anagen phase, which includes the synthesis of keratin proteins and other structural components. The size and shape of the dermal papilla are thought to influence the thickness and texture of hair.
Interaction with Stem Cells
: Dermal papilla cells interact with stem cells found in the bulge region of the hair follicles. These interactions are essential for starting hair regeneration and cycling. They stimulate stem cells to multiply and develop into the numerous cell types required for new hair growth.
Factors Influencing the Anagen Phase
Anagen is the active growth stage of hair follicles, during which the hair shaft develops. This time might last many years and is influenced by a variety of factors. Understanding these factors is important for managing hair growth and addressing issues like hair loss. Here are the primary elements influencing the anagen phase:
Genetics
: Genetics influences the length of the anagen period. People inherit the length of their hair growth cycles from their parents. Those with a longer anagen phase have naturally longer hair, whilst those with a shorter anagen phase have shorter hair.
Hormones
: Hormones, particularly androgens like testosterone and dihydrotestosterone (DHT), have a considerable impact on hair growth. High androgen levels can abbreviate the anagen phase, resulting in disorders such as androgenetic alopecia (pattern
baldness
). In contrast, hormones such as oestrogen can prolong the anagen phase, resulting in longer and denser hair.
Age
: As people get older, the duration of the anagen phase reduces. This leads to shorter hair growth cycles and weaker hair. This shortening is caused by age-related hormonal changes and diminished cellular activity in hair follicles.
Nutritional Status
:
Adequate nutrition is required to maintain a healthy anagen phase. Key nutrients are: Proteins are essential for the synthesis of keratin, the primary component of human hair. Vitamins include A, B-complex vitamins (especially biotin),D, E and
vitamin C

Scalp Health
:
The anagen phase can be altered by the scalp's condition.
Dandruff
,
psoriasis
, and fungal infections can all disrupt natural hair growth by changing the environment on the scalp. A healthy scalp with good blood circulation promotes a longer anagen phase.
Stress
:
Chronic stress can cause hair loss, including telogen effluvium, which happens when stress hormones prematurely transfer hair follicles from the anagen to the resting phase. Stress management techniques such as meditation, exercise, and adequate sleep can all contribute to a healthy anagen phase.
Medications
:
Certain drugs can disrupt the anagen phase. For example, chemotherapy medications frequently induce hair follicles to enter the resting phase, resulting in hair loss. Hormonal therapies, such as birth control pills, can either shorten or lengthen the anagen phase, depending on their hormonal effects. Beta-blockers and antidepressants can both influence hair growth cycles.
Environmental Factors
: Environmental contaminants,
UV
radiation, and extreme weather conditions can damage hair follicles and disrupt the anagen phase. Protecting hair from external stimuli can aid in its healthy growth cycle.
Hair Care Practices
:
Frequent use of heat styling equipment, chemical treatments, and tight hairstyles can harm hair follicles and decrease the anagen period. Gentle hair care procedures that reduce physical and chemical harm promote longer hair growth cycles.
Medical Conditions
: Thyroid disorders, polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS), and autoimmune diseases can all influence hormone levels and immunological responses, interfering with normal hair development. Treating these issues can assist in restoring a healthy anagen phase.
