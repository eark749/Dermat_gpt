SPF level and Broad-Spectrum Protection

The SPF level and whether it provides broad-spectrum protection are two important factors to consider when selecting a sunscreen. Let us take a closer look at these elements: 

  1. SPF Level: SPF, or Sun Protection Factor, is a measurement of how well a sunscreen protects against UVB rays, which cause sunburn.  The level of protection provided by the sunscreen is indicated by the SPF number.

  • SPF 15: Provides basic protection by blocking approximately 93% of UVB rays.

  • SPF 30: Provides moderate protection and blocks approximately 97% of UVB rays.

  • SPF 50: Provides excellent protection, filtering out approximately 98% of UVB rays.

  • SPF 50+ or higher: Offers the most protection, blocking out approximately 99% of UVB rays.

It's worth noting that the increase in protection between SPF values is not linear. SPF 30 does not, for example, provide twice the protection of SPF 15. The greater the SPF, the lesser the incremental protection gain. Regardless, for optimal protection, it is normally recommended to use a sunscreen with an SPF of 30 or higher.

  1. Broad-Spectrum Protection: While SPF indicates the level of UVB ray protection, it is also critical to ensure that the sunscreen provides broad-spectrum protection. Broad-spectrum sunscreens shield the skin from both UVA and UVB rays.

  • UVA rays: These rays penetrate deeper into the skin and are primarily responsible for premature aging, wrinkles, and sunspots.

  • UVB rays: These rays are responsible for sunburns because they primarily affect the top layers of the skin.

To provide complete protection, search for sunscreens labeled "broad-spectrum" or those that mention UVA and UVB ray protection. To signify UVA protection, the label may also include the UVA symbol (a circle with the word "UVA" inside). Using a broad-spectrum sunscreen protects against sunburn, premature aging, and the risk of skin cancer.

  5. Build Coverage as Necessary: If you want more protection, you can add another layer of tinted sunscreen to troublesome spots. Build up the coverage gradually to prevent it from looking heavy or cakey.

  6. Do not Forget the Undereye Area: Use your ring finger to dab a little tinted sunscreen under your eyes. To apply for sheer coverage and safeguard sensitive skin, gently pat and blend the product in.

  7. Apply Tinted Sunscreen to Neck and Ears: Apply tinted sunscreen to your neck and seamlessly blend it in. Don't forget to apply sunscreen to your ears as well, as they are often exposed to the sun.

  8. Reapply as Necessary: It is important to remember to reapply tinted sunscreen every two hours or as instructed on the product label, particularly if you are spending a lot of time outside, perspiring, or wiping your face.

  9. Finish with Extra Makeup (Optional): If you would like to enhance your desired look, use extra makeup items like concealer, powder, blush, or bronzer. Just make sure that any products you use will not interfere with the tinted sunscreen's ability to block out the sun.

  10. Sun Protection Beyond Makeup: Remember that tinted sunscreen is only one step in your overall sun safety regimen. Along with it, other precautions should be taken, such as putting on a wide-brimmed hat, some protective clothing, and eyewear; looking for cover when you can; and limiting your time in the sun during peak hours.

These instructions will help you apply tinted sunscreen effectively, get a finish that looks natural, and protect your skin from the sun's damaging rays.
