Understanding Triethanolamine: An Overview of its Uses and Risks

Triethanolamine (TEA) is a compound commonly used in various industries, including cosmetics. It is formed as a byproduct of the chemical reaction between ethylene oxide and ammonia. TEA serves multiple functions, including emulsifier, surfactant, pH adjuster, and corrosion inhibitor. It is a stabilizing agent in cosmetics found in creams, lotions, shampoos, soaps, makeup, and other skincare and personal care products. The use of Triethanolamine in cosmetics provides several benefits. It helps to stabilize the consistency, improves the texture, and makes the products easier to spread. TEA also acts as a buffer, ensuring that the cosmetic formulation maintains the desired pH level. It is important to note, however, that some people may be sensitive to triethanolamine due to their skin type. To determine compatibility, perform a patch test on a small area of the skin, such as the neck, before using a TEA-containing product.

Triethanolamine in Skincare: Exploring its Role in Cosmetic Formulations

TEA can be used in cosmetics like eyeliner, mascara, eyeshadow, blush, and foundation. It is also found in perfumes, hair care products, hair dyes, wave sets, shaving creams, sunscreens, and skincare/cleansing products. In cosmetics and personal care products, TEA is used as a surfactant, pH adjuster, and fragrance ingredient. The related TEA-containing compounds have been shown to act as surfactants, hair conditioners, and skin conditioners. TEA-sorbate is only used as a preservative.

Triethanolamine Toxicity: Examining Its Effects on Skin Health

Triethanolamine is safe for the skin and has no known side effects, as most products keep its concentration between 1% and 3%. At this concentration, triethanolamine is safe for topical application and does not cause skin irritation or other side effects. When used in cosmetic products at concentrations greater than 5%, it can cause allergic reactions and skin irritations. However, the irritation can subside once you stop using the product. So far, there is no evidence of any other long-term side effects from triethanolamine. Triethanolamine is not an ingredient you should actively look for in skin care products. However, its stabilizing properties can be useful in a variety of skin formulations.

Triethanolamine Myths Debunked: Separating Facts from Fiction

Some common myths about TEA and the facts to debunk them:

  • Myth: Triethanolamine is carcinogenic.

  • Truth: When used at cosmetic levels, the substance is not harmful to humans and is safe to use in conjunction with a conventional cosmetic product.

  • Myth: Triethanolamine is not safe for all skin types. 

  • Truth: It is a small component that helps to keep a cosmetic product's pH stable. It is extremely alkaline (the opposite of acidic): a 1% solution has a pH of approximately 10. It does not have the best safety reputation, but there is no need to be concerned.

  • Myth : Triethanolamine penetrates deeply into the skin. 

  • Truth: Triethanolamine (TEA) is a non-active ingredient that has no effect on the skin and is only used to balance and stabilise formulation pH. This component enhances the product's sensation and interaction with the skin.

Triethanolamine Formulations: Choosing Products with Care

When choosing skincare products containing triethanolamine (TEA), it is important to understand the formulations and the potential effects on your skin. Here is a guide to help you carefully select TEA-containing products:

  1. Emulsifier: Adding triethanolamine to an ingredient causes chemical bonds to change, allowing fluids that are difficult to mix to be thoroughly processed into a single product. Consider an oil and water salad dressing. Adding triethanolamine causes the immiscible ingredients to mix thoroughly and homogeneously, eliminating the need to shake the container before each use.

  2. pH adjuster: Triethanolamine has been shown to work effectively with acidic compounds, lowering their pH to a level suitable for sensitive skin. However, it also works in the opposite direction, stabilizing higher pH levels to make them more suitable for skin care. 

  3. Stabilizer: Creating a creamy texture for shaving cream or gel that lasts every time it is used is difficult unless triethanolamine is added to stabilize the mixture and extend its shelf life. 

  4. Surfactant: Triethanolamine reduces a liquid's surface tension, allowing it to blend more easily with another liquid, solid, or gas. 

  5. Thickener: When viscous triethanolamine is added to a product, it gives the formula more body and density. This factor reinforces triethanolamine's use in hand sanitizer, sunscreen, lotions, and ear drops to improve consistency.
