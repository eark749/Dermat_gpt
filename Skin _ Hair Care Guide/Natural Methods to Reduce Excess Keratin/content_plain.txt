Excess keratin production can have a variety of impacts on the skin, hair, and general body processes. Here are the main impacts:
One of the most common effects of excess keratin is keratosis pilaris, characterized by small, rough bumps on the skin, often on the upper arms, thighs, buttocks, or face. These bumps result from keratin clogging the hair follicles.
Thickened areas of skin, such as calluses on hands or feet and corns on toes, are caused by excessive keratin buildup in response to repetitive friction or pressure.
This refers to a general thickening of the outer layer of the skin due to excessive keratinization. It can lead to rough, scaly patches and a loss of skin elasticity.
Excessive keratin production can make hair feel coarse, rough, or brittle. This can affect hair manageability and styling.

Thickened skin due to excess keratin can reduce sensitivity to touch or temperature changes in affected areas.

Keratin in Skin Health: Impact on Skin Conditions and Aging
Keratin is essential for skin health and has a substantial impact on numerous skin diseases and the aging process. Here's how keratin affects skin diseases and aging.
Structural Support
: Keratin is a fibrous protein that forms the structural framework of the outermost layer of the skin (epidermis) and its appendages (hair, and nails). It provides strength and resilience to the skin, protecting it from mechanical stressors and environmental damage.
Barrier Function
:
Keratinocytes, the primary cells of the epidermis, produce keratin and contribute to the skin barrier function. This barrier regulates water loss, protects against pathogens, and maintains overall skin hydration.
Skin Conditions Influenced by Keratin
:
Keratosis Pilaris is characterized by rough, bumpy skin (often on the arms or thighs) produced by keratin accumulation around hair follicles. Calluses and corns are thickened patches of skin on the hands or feet caused by repeated friction or pressure, which increases keratin production. Psoriasis is an inflammatory skin condition characterized by fast turnover of skin cells, resulting in thicker, scaly patches (plaques) and aberrant keratinocyte growth.
Aging and Keratin
:
Keratin and other structural proteins, such as collagen, are produced less as we age, resulting in less suppleness and firmness of the skin. The loss of keratin and collagen contributes to the production of wrinkles and fine lines, particularly in areas prone to repeated facial expressions. Age-related changes can damage the skin barrier, resulting in dryness and a harsh texture from decreased keratinocyte turnover.
Impact of Environmental Factors
: Excessive sun exposure promotes aging by destroying keratinocytes and collagen fibres, resulting in premature wrinkles and drooping skin. Environmental contaminants can alter keratin formation and impair skin barrier function, resulting in skin aging and sensitivity.
