Why Myomectomy can Lead to Scarring

Myomectomy is a surgical treatment that removes uterine fibroids while leaving the uterus intact. Depending on the method used (abdominal, laparoscopic, or hysteroscopic), incisions are made in the abdominal wall or vaginal canal. These incisions allow access to the fibroids so that they can be removed. Scarring is a normal component of the healing process following surgery, and there are various reasons why myomectomy may result in scarring:

  1. Incisions: The very nature of surgery requires cutting through the skin and underlying tissues to reach the targeted area. These incisions damage the skin and surrounding tissues, causing the body's healing mechanisms to kick in. The healing process involves the formation of scar tissue as the body repairs the incisions.

  2. Tissue Healing: The body responds to an incision by sending blood cells, platelets, and other healing substances to the spot. Collagen, a protein that aids in wound healing, builds up in the area, generating scar tissue. This tissue frequently has a different feel and appearance than the surrounding skin.

  3. Inflammation: Inflammation is a normal reaction to tissue damage. It aids in the removal of damaged cells and the repair process. However, inflammation also contributes to the formation of scar tissue.

  4. Tension on Wound: Scar tissue formation can be influenced by the tension and stress exerted on the wound during the healing process. Areas that experience more tension or movement during healing are more likely to develop thicker and more visible scars.

  5. Individual Healing Response: Every person's body heals differently. Genetics, age, skin type, and overall health can all influence how a person's body responds to the healing process. Some people may be predisposed to acquiring visible scars.

  6. Surgical Technique: The technique used during the myomectomy can affect the extent and visibility of scarring. In comparison to open abdominal myomectomy, laparoscopic and hysteroscopic approaches involve smaller incisions and may result in less visible scarring.

  7. Scar Maturation: Scars frequently change over time. Scars may appear red, elevated, and more visible at first. Scars normally flatten, soften, and fade in color as the healing process progresses. The degree of development, however, differs from person to person.

The Healing Process of Myomectomy Scars

Myomectomy scars recover in a variety of stages, each with its own specific characteristics. Understanding these stages might help people know what to expect and how to properly care for their scars. Individual healing experiences will vary, but the general process will include the following stages:

  • Inflammatory Phase (0-5 days): The body begins an inflammatory response following the myomectomy procedure. To allow immune cells and healing elements to reach the wound site, blood vessels dilate. The region may appear red, bloated, and sensitive during this stage. The body begins to clear away surgical debris, which might result in scabbing or crust formation around the incision sites.

  • Proliferative Phase (5 days - 3 weeks): This phase entails the development of new tissue to bridge the wound and strengthen it. Collagen production increases, and granulation tissue—a mix of new blood vessels, fibroblasts (collagen-producing cells), and immune cells—forms. Scar tissue begins to form and heal the wound site. During this stage, the scars may still be red or pink.

  • Remodeling Phase (3 weeks - 2 years): The body continues to rebuild and improve the scar tissue throughout this phase. Collagen fibres reorganise, and scar tissue grows stronger over time. Scars may begin to flatten and become less visible. Scars may appear pink at first, but they will fade to a lighter color with time, usually closer to the natural skin tone.

  • Maturation Phase (up to 2 years or more): Scars are further faded, flattened, and softened during the final stage of healing. Scar tissue should have greatly improved in look and texture by this point. It is important to note, however, that total scar maturation might take up to two years or longer.

The Stages of Scar Healing Post-Myomectomy

Scar healing after a myomectomy operation occurs in stages, each with its own set of characteristics and timetable. While everyone's experiences vary, recognising these stages will help you know what to expect during the recovery process. Scar healing stages after myomectomy are as follows: immediate post-operative stage (0-2 weeks), inflammatory stage (2 days - 2 weeks), proliferative stage (2 weeks - 6 weeks), remodeling stage (6 weeks - 1 year), and maturation stage (1 year - 2 years or longer).

Techniques to Minimize Myomectomy Scarring

A combination of good wound care, scar management measures, and healthy practices is required to reduce myomectomy scarring. While complete scar removal is unlikely, these methods can considerably minimise scarring and enhance the overall appearance of incision sites. Consider the following excellent techniques:

  1. Wound Care: Proper wound care is critical for minimising scarring during the immediate post-operative period. The incision sites should be cleaned, dressings should be changed, and the area should be kept dry and clean according to your surgeon's instructions.

  2. Scar Massage: A light scar massage can aid in reducing scar tissue, enhancing blood flow, and assisting collagen alignment. Apply a moisturiser, vitamin E oil, or silicone gel to the scar and massage it in a circular motion.

  3. Silicone Sheets or Gel: Silicone sheets or gels are known to hydrate the skin and promote healing. They can help reduce scar thickness, color, and texture. Apply them according to the manufacturer's instructions.

  5. Sun protection: UV rays can darken scars and make them more obvious. Use a sunscreen (SPF 30 or higher) or cover your scars with clothing when exposed to sunlight to protect them from the sun's rays.

  6. Avoid Smoking: Smoking can impair blood circulation and slow healing, potentially resulting in more pronounced scarring. If you smoke, consider quitting or cutting back during the healing period.

  7. Eat a Balanced Diet: A diet rich in vitamins, minerals, and nutrients promotes overall skin health and wound healing. Foods high in vitamin C, zinc, and antioxidants are especially beneficial.

  8. Hydration: Staying hydrated keeps your skin supple and promotes healing. Drink plenty of water and use a gentle moisturiser to prevent dryness around the incision sites.

  9. Laser Therapy: Speak with a dermatologist or plastic surgeon about the potential benefits of laser therapy for improving scar texture, color, and appearance.

  10. Steroid Injections: A healthcare provider may recommend corticosteroid injections in some cases to help reduce inflammation and flatten raised scars.

  11. Scar Prevention Creams: Some scar prevention creams and gels are intended to be applied immediately after the wound has been closed. These items are intended to reduce scar formation and improve healing outcomes.

  12. Professional Advice: Discuss scar management options with your healthcare provider. Dermatologists and plastic surgeons can offer professional guidance on individualised scar care methods.
