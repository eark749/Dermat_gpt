Why Myomectomy can Lead to Scarring
Myomectomy is a surgical treatment that removes uterine fibroids while leaving the uterus intact. Depending on the method used (abdominal, laparoscopic, or hysteroscopic), incisions are made in the abdominal wall or vaginal canal. These incisions allow access to the fibroids so that they can be removed. Scarring is a normal component of the healing process following surgery, and there are various reasons why myomectomy may result in scarring:
Incisions
: The very nature of surgery requires cutting through the skin and underlying tissues to reach the targeted area. These incisions damage the skin and surrounding tissues, causing the body's healing mechanisms to kick in. The healing process involves the formation of scar tissue as the body repairs the incisions.
Tissue Healing
: The body responds to an incision by sending blood cells, platelets, and other healing substances to the spot. Collagen, a protein that aids in wound healing, builds up in the area, generating scar tissue. This tissue frequently has a different feel and appearance than the surrounding skin.
Inflammation:
Inflammation is a normal reaction to tissue damage. It aids in the removal of damaged cells and the repair process. However, inflammation also contributes to the formation of scar tissue.
Tension on Wound
: Scar tissue formation can be influenced by the tension and stress exerted on the wound during the healing process. Areas that experience more tension or movement during healing are more likely to develop thicker and more visible scars.
Individual Healing Response
: Every person's body heals differently. Genetics, age, skin type, and overall health can all influence how a person's body responds to the healing process. Some people may be predisposed to acquiring visible scars.
Surgical Technique
: The technique used during the myomectomy can affect the extent and visibility of scarring. In comparison to open abdominal myomectomy, laparoscopic and hysteroscopic approaches involve smaller incisions and may result in less visible scarring.
Scar Maturation
: Scars frequently change over time. Scars may appear red, elevated, and more visible at first. Scars normally flatten, soften, and fade in color as the healing process progresses. The degree of development, however, differs from person to person.
The Healing Process of Myomectomy Scars
Myomectomy scars recover in a variety of stages, each with its own specific characteristics. Understanding these stages might help people know what to expect and how to properly care for their scars. Individual healing experiences will vary, but the general process will include the following stages:
Inflammatory Phase (0-5 days):
The body begins an inflammatory response following the myomectomy procedure. To allow immune cells and healing elements to reach the wound site, blood vessels dilate. The region may appear red, bloated, and sensitive during this stage. The body begins to clear away surgical debris, which might result in scabbing or crust formation around the incision sites.
Proliferative Phase (5 days - 3 weeks)
: This phase entails the development of new tissue to bridge the wound and strengthen it. Collagen production increases, and granulation tissue—a mix of new blood vessels, fibroblasts (collagen-producing cells), and immune cells—forms. Scar tissue begins to form and heal the wound site. During this stage, the scars may still be red or pink.
Remodeling Phase (3 weeks - 2 years)
: The body continues to rebuild and improve the scar tissue throughout this phase. Collagen fibres reorganise, and scar tissue grows stronger over time. Scars may begin to flatten and become less visible. Scars may appear pink at first, but they will fade to a lighter color with time, usually closer to the natural skin tone.
Maturation Phase (up to 2 years or more)
: Scars are further faded, flattened, and softened during the final stage of healing. Scar tissue should have greatly improved in look and texture by this point. It is important to note, however, that total scar maturation might take up to two years or longer.

The Stages of Scar Healing Post-Myomectomy
Scar healing after a myomectomy operation occurs in stages, each with its own set of characteristics and timetable. While everyone's experiences vary, recognising these stages will help you know what to expect during the recovery process. Scar healing stages after myomectomy are as follows: immediate post-operative stage (0-2 weeks), inflammatory stage (2 days - 2 weeks), proliferative stage (2 weeks - 6 weeks), remodeling stage (6 weeks - 1 year), and maturation stage (1 year - 2 years or longer).
