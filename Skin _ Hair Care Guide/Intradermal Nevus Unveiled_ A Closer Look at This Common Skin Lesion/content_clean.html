What is an Intradermal Nevus?

An intradermal nevus, also known as a mole, is a type of benign skin growth that develops from melanocytes, cells in the skin responsible for producing pigmentation. Intradermal nevi are the most common type of nevus and typically appear as small, smooth, dome-shaped bumps with a slightly darker color than the surrounding skin. They can range in size from a few millimetres to several centimetres in diameter and can occur on any part of the body.

Types of Nevi and Their Origins

There are several different types of nevi, each with their own distinct appearance and origin. In addition to intradermal nevi, other types include junctional nevi, which develop at the junction between the epidermis and dermis, and compound nevi, which involve both the epidermis and dermis.

Nevi can develop for a variety of reasons, including genetics, exposure to sunlight, and hormonal changes. They can also appear spontaneously without any known cause.

Intradermal Nevus Characteristics and Appearance

Intradermal nevi are typically raised, smooth, and dome-shaped with a slightly darker color than the surrounding skin. They are most commonly found on the face, neck, and torso, but can appear anywhere on the body.

As they age, intradermal nevi may become slightly larger and more elevated, but they typically do not change in shape or color. In rare cases, they may become cancerous and develop into melanoma, a serious form of skin cancer.

Diagnosing and Monitoring Intradermal Nevi

While intradermal nevi are generally harmless, it is important to monitor them for changes in appearance or other signs of skin cancer. There are several methods for diagnosing and monitoring nevi, including dermoscopy, biopsy, and skin self-examination.

Dermoscopy and Biopsy for Nevus Diagnosis

Dermoscopy, also known as skin surface microscopy, is a non-invasive imaging technique that allows dermatologists to examine nevi in detail. During a dermoscopy exam, a dermatologist will use a specialized instrument called a dermoscopy to examine the nevus under high magnification.

If the dermatologist detects any suspicious features during a dermoscopy exam, they may recommend a biopsy, a procedure in which a small sample of the nevus is removed for laboratory analysis. Biopsy results can help determine whether a nevus is benign or malignant and guide further treatment options.

Skin Self-Examination and Routine Check-ups

In addition to dermoscopy and biopsy, it is important to perform regular skin self-examinations and schedule routine check-ups with a dermatologist. Skin self-examinations involve checking the skin for any changes in appearance or new growths, including intradermal nevi. If any changes are detected, it is important to consult a dermatologist for further evaluation.

Intradermal Nevus Removal and Treatment Options

In most cases, intradermal nevi do not require treatment and can be left alone. However, if the nevus is causing discomfort or is cosmetically bothersome, or if there is concern that the nevus may be cancerous, removal may be necessary. There are several methods for removing intradermal nevi, including surgical excision, laser treatment, and cryotherapy.

Surgical Excision for Intradermal Nevi

Surgical excision involves removing the nevus and surrounding tissue under local anaesthesia. This method is typically recommended for nevi that are large, have irregular borders, or are suspected of being cancerous.

After the nevus is removed, the wound is closed with stitches, and a small scar may be left behind. While surgical excision is generally safe, it does carry some risk of bleeding, infection, and scarring.

Laser Treatment and Cryotherapy

Laser treatment involves using a focused beam of light to destroy the nevus tissue. This method is typically used for smaller nevi that are located in areas where scarring may be a concern, such as the face.

Cryotherapy involves freezing the nevus tissue with liquid nitrogen. This method is typically used for small nevi that are located in areas where scarring is not a concern.

While both laser treatment and cryotherapy are generally safe, they can cause temporary redness, swelling, and blistering. In rare cases, they may also cause scarring or changes in skin pigmentation.

Skin Care and Cancer Prevention for Nevi

In addition to monitoring and treating intradermal nevi, it is important to take steps to prevent skin cancer and manage other skin care concerns.

Skin Care Tips for Nevi Management

To minimize the risk of skin cancer and other skin care concerns, it is important to practice good skin care habits, including:

  • Wearing protective clothing, such as long-sleeved shirts and wide-brimmed hats, when outdoors

  • Applying sunscreen with at least SPF 30 to all exposed skin, including the face and neck, and reapplying every 2 hours

  • Avoiding tanning beds and other sources of artificial UV radiation

  • Moisturizing the skin regularly to prevent dryness and irritation

  • Avoiding picking or scratching at nevi or other skin lesions

Skin Cancer Prevention and Education

In addition to these basic skin care habits, it is important to educate yourself about skin cancer and its risk factors. Risk factors for skin cancer include:

  • Fair skin

  • A history of sunburns or excessive sun exposure

  • A family history of skin cancer

  • A history of indoor tanning

  • A weakened immune system

By understanding these risk factors and taking steps to protect your skin, you can minimize your risk of developing skin cancer and other skin care concerns.
