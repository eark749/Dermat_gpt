How Does Minoxidil Work for Hair Loss?
The specific mechanism of action of minoxidil in treating hair loss is unknown, but there are various hypotheses about its efficacy. The following is a breakdown of how minoxidil is thought to work:
Vasodilation
: Minoxidil is a powerful vasodilator, causing blood vessels to open wider and blood flow to be increased. Minoxidil is believed to improve the delivery of oxygen, nutrients, and growth factors to the hair follicles by widening the blood vessels surrounding them when applied topically to the scalp. This improved blood flow might support the function of the hair follicles, nourish them, and stimulate hair growth.
Anagen (Growth) Phase Prolongation
: The active growth phase of the hair cycle, known as the anagen phase, may last longer when minoxidil is used. The hair follicles remain in an active growth state for a longer period of time because minoxidil prolongs this phase. The result is hair shafts that are thicker and longer.
Stimulating Hair Follicles
: Minoxidil is thought to stimulate the hair follicles' resting phase (telogen) to transition into the growth phase (anagen). It may stimulate dormant hair follicles, promoting new hair growth and increasing hair density.
The effectiveness of minoxidil varies from person to person and may be affected by a variety of factors, including the underlying cause of hair loss, the degree of hair loss, and the person's response to treatment. It is also important to note that the precise mechanisms underlying minoxidil's activity may involve other, as yet unidentified, components. The specific chemical processes and cellular interactions that minoxidil uses to affect hair growth are still being investigated.
Assessing the Side Effects of Minoxidil
Minoxidil is widely regarded as safe for topical application in the treatment of hair loss, and most people handle it well. However, minoxidil, like any medicine, has the potential for adverse effects. Here are some of the most prevalent negative effects of minoxidil:
Scalp Irritation
: When using minoxidil, some people may experience mild scalp irritation, redness, itching, or dryness.  This is usually a temporary side effect that goes away as the scalp adjusts to the medication.  Using the recommended amount and avoiding overuse can help to reduce scalp irritation.
Increased Hair Shedding
: Some people may experience a temporary increase in hair shedding during the first few weeks of using minoxidil. This shedding is commonly associated with the hair growth cycle and is referred to as "minoxidil-induced shedding." It is usually followed by the development of newer, healthier hair.  This effect is usually only temporary and fades over time.
Unwanted Hair Growth
: Minoxidil may cause unwanted hair growth in areas where it is applied in some cases. This includes female facial hair growth as well as hair growth on other parts of the body. If unwanted hair growth occurs, adjust the application technique or use a lower concentration of minoxidil to control it.
Skin Irritation and Allergic Reactions
: Although uncommon, some people may develop more severe skin reactions, such as severe itching, rash, hives, or swelling. These are symptoms of an allergic reaction. If you experience any of these symptoms, stop using minoxidil and seek medical attention right away.
Systemic Side Effects
: There is no evidence that topical minoxidil causes systemic side effects. However, in rare cases, if excessive absorption occurs through the scalp, minoxidil can cause systemic effects such as low blood pressure, rapid heartbeat, or dizziness. These systemic side effects are more commonly associated with oral minoxidil, which is used to treat a variety of medical conditions and must be administered under close medical supervision.
