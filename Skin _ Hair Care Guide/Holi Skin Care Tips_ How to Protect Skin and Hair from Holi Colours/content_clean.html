Identifying Natural vs. Chemical Colours

Identifying natural colours from chemical colours can sometimes be difficult, but there are several indicators you can look for to differentiate between the two:

  • Ingredients: A list of ingredients can be found on the colours powder's packaging. Natural colours typically contain plant-based ingredients like turmeric, beetroot, indigo, or flower extracts. 

  • colour Appearance: Natural colours have more muted or earthy tones than chemical colours, which are often bright and intense. Chemical colours may have a more artificial or neon appearance.

  • Texture: The presence of plant-based particles or extracts can make natural colours appear coarser or grainier. Chemical colours may have a finer and smoother texture because they are frequently made with synthetic pigments and additives.

  • Scent: Natural colours may emit a faint or subtle scent resembling the plant-based ingredients used. Chemical colours may have a stronger, more chemical-like odour as a result of the presence of synthetic additives.

  • Staining Properties: Natural colours are less likely to stain skin or clothing than synthetic colours. Chemical colours can leave more intense stains that are difficult to remove, especially from fabrics and porous surfaces.

  • Certifications: Look for certifications or labels indicating that the colour powder is natural, organic, or free of synthetic additives. Organic certifications from reputable organisations can verify that the colours are derived from natural sources and do not contain harmful chemicals.

  • Water Solubility: Natural colours are often water soluble and can be easily removed with water and mild soap. Chemical colours may be less water-soluble and more difficult to remove, especially from the skin and hair.

  • Regulatory Approval: Check to see if the colours powder has been approved or certified by regulatory authorities such as the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) or equivalent agencies in your country. Approved natural colours are generally thought to be safer for skin application than unregulated chemical colours.

By considering these factors and looking for signs of natural ingredients and formulations, you can better distinguish between natural and chemical colours and make informed choices when selecting colour powders for Holi celebrations.

DIY Natural Colours for a Safe Holi

Creating DIY natural colours for Holi is a fun and eco-friendly way to celebrate the festival while protecting your skin and the environment. Here are some easy recipes for creating natural colours at home with common ingredients:

  1. Yellow: Turmeric, a natural source of yellow, has been used in Indian culture for centuries. To make yellow powder, combine turmeric powder and a small amount of flour or cornflour to form a fine, dry powder. You can adjust the colour intensity by adding more or less turmeric powder. 

  2. Red: Beetroot is an excellent natural source of red colour. To make red powder, grate or blend fresh beetroot and strain the juice. Combine the beetroot juice and flour or cornflour to make a smooth paste. Spread the paste thinly onto a tray and let it dry completely. Once dry, grind the paste into a fine powder to produce a red powder.

  3. Green: Spinach or spinach leaves can be used to achieve a bright green colour. Blanch fresh spinach leaves in boiling water for a few minutes before transferring them to an ice bath to cool. Make a smooth paste by blending blanched spinach leaves with a small amount of water. Mix the spinach paste with flour or cornflour to make a green powder. Spread the paste thinly on a tray and let it dry completely before grinding to a fine powder.

  4. Blue: Blueberries or blueberry juice can be used to achieve a natural blue colour. Blend fresh or frozen blueberries with a small amount of water, then strain out any solids. Combine the blueberry juice and flour or cornflour to make a smooth paste. Spread the paste thinly onto a tray and let it dry completely. Once dry, grind the paste into a fine powder to produce blue powder.

  5. Orange: To create an orange colour, use carrots or carrot juice. Blend fresh carrots with a small amount of water, then strain out any solids. Combine the carrot juice and flour or cornflour to make a smooth paste. Spread the paste thinly onto a tray and let it dry completely. Once dry, grind the paste into a fine powder to produce orange powder.

  6. Pink: Hibiscus flowers or hibiscus tea can be used to achieve a natural pink colour. Steep dried hibiscus flowers or tea bags in hot water to extract the colour. Combine the hibiscus extract and flour or cornflour to make a smooth paste. Spread the paste thinly onto a tray and let it dry completely. Once dry, grind the paste into a fine powder to produce pink powder.

To use these natural Holi colours, sprinkle or apply them gently to the skin, or mix them with water to make a coloured water solution. With these eco-friendly alternatives, you can celebrate colour safely and vibrantly!
