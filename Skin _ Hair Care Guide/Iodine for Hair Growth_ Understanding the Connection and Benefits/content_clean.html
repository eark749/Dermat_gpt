The Role of Iodine in the Body

Iodine is a trace element that naturally occurs in your body and plays a crucial part in normal operation. Iodine is required for optimal cellular activity; your cells use iodine to convert food into energy, and your thyroid requires a steady supply of iodine for proper operation. Iodine can be obtained from pharmaceuticals, but it can also be obtained from iodine-rich foods, which can help you achieve your daily iodine requirements. Some dairy products, iodized salt, and certain fish (think cod, sea bass, haddock, and perch), as well as kelp and land-dwelling plants produced in iodine-rich soil, can provide you with the iodine you need each day. The Food and Nutrition Board states that individuals can have roughly 150 micrograms per day, while nursing or pregnant women can consume up to 290 micrograms safely. Numerous trace elements in your body are essential for normal function; selenium is one that you will frequently hear about. In tiny doses, it is essential for good function. A trace element shortage has repercussions.

What is Iodine?  

Iodine is a mineral found in certain diets. The body requires iodine to produce thyroid hormones. These hormones regulate the body's metabolism and other vital activities. The body also requires thyroid hormones for optimal bone and brain development during pregnancy and infancy. Everyone needs iodine, especially newborns and pregnant women.

Importance of Iodine for Thyroid Health

Iodine's primary duty is to maintain optimal thyroid function. It contributes to the regulation of the thyroid hormones thyroxine (T4) and triiodothyronine (T3). Getting enough iodine is essential for avoiding low thyroid hormone levels and hypothyroidism.

How Iodine Affects Overall Health

Scientists are investigating iodine to learn how it affects health. Here are some examples of what this research revealed.

  1. Fetal and infant development: Women who are pregnant or breastfeeding require adequate iodine intake for their babies' normal growth and development. Breastfed babies absorb iodine from breast milk. However, breast milk's iodine content is determined by the mother's iodine intake. To ensure adequate iodine levels for normal fetal and infant development, various national and international organizations recommend that pregnant and breastfeeding women, as well as newborns, take iodine supplements. The American Thyroid Association recommends that pregnant, trying to conceive, or breastfeeding women take a daily supplement containing 150 mcg of iodine in the form of potassium iodide. The American Academy of Pediatrics offers similar guidelines. Only about half of the prenatal multivitamins available in the United States contain iodine.

  2. Cognitive function during childhood: A severe iodine shortage in childhood impairs brain and nervous system development. The effects of a modest iodine shortage in youth are more difficult to assess, yet they may create minor difficulties with neurological development. Giving iodine supplements to youngsters with modest iodine shortages boosts their reasoning skills and overall cognitive performance. Iodine supplements appear to benefit both physical and mental development in children from iodine-deficient areas. More research is needed to completely understand the impact of mild iodine shortages and iodine supplementation on cognitive function.

  3. Fibrocystic breast disease: Although not hazardous, fibrocystic breast disease results in lumpy, uncomfortable breasts. It primarily affects women of reproductive age, but it can also develop during menopause. Very high doses of iodine supplements may alleviate the pain and other symptoms of fibrocystic breast disease, but further research is needed to prove this. Consult your doctor or dermatologists before taking iodine for this ailment, as large doses might be dangerous.

  4. Radiation-induced thyroid cancer: Nuclear accidents can emit radioactive iodine into the environment, raising the risk of thyroid cancer in those exposed to it, particularly youngsters. People with iodine shortage who are exposed to radioactive iodine are at an increased risk of acquiring thyroid cancer. The United States Food and Drug Administration has licensed potassium iodide as a thyroid-blocking medication to lower the risk of thyroid cancer during radiation crises.

  5. Low iodine diets for radioactive iodine treatment: Certain people with thyroid cancer are treated with radioactive iodine. Patients preparing for this treatment are usually advised to eat a low-iodine diet (50 mcg or less per day) for 1 to 2 weeks before beginning treatment.

Iodine and Hair Growth

When looking into the complex relationship between iodine and hair development, the focus shifts. Iodine is essential for thyroid hormone production, which regulates the hair growth cycle. Hair follicles are sensitive to these hormones, and abnormalities can cause disturbances in typical development patterns. Ensuring an adequate intake of iodine for thyroid hormone production appears to be a strategic approach, not just for overall health but also for promoting lush and healthy hair growth.

How Iodine Contributes to Hair Health

The link between iodine and hair loss is not due to iodine deficiency; instead, excessive iodine may have a tertiary association with hair loss.  What's the link between the two? Your thyroid gland. Hyperthyroidism is a disorder in which your thyroid overproduces thyroid hormones, creating a variety of complications.  In the most extreme cases of untreated thyroid dysfunction, the illness might eventually cause irreversible damage to the hair structure. So, where does iodine fit into this? Excessive iodine might be one of the leading causes of hyperthyroidism.  Excess iodine from food or pharmaceuticals is one of the most common causes of hyperthyroidism, second only to genetics, age, and gender.

Iodine Deficiency and Hair Loss

Hair loss may occur gradually in hypothyroidism and hyperthyroidism. You will not necessarily detect missing patches or bald places. Instead, your hair may appear thinner overall. Every day, you lose 50 to 100 hairs from your head. If regular hair development is disrupted, hairs are not regenerated, and uniform hair loss may result. The good news is that hair loss due to thyroid issues is usually transitory. Even if you start taking medications to treat your disease, you may lose your hair. Some people are concerned that the medications would cause hair loss, but this could be due to the hair's natural life cycle.

Iodine Supplements: What to Know

Iodine supplements can help persons who are iodine deficient, pregnant or breastfeeding or suffer from certain thyroid problems. However, ingesting too much iodine might be dangerous, therefore follow recommended dosage guidelines:

  • Children's daily doses should not exceed 200 mcg for children aged 1-3, 300 mcg for children aged 4-8, 600 mcg for children aged 9-13, and 900 mcg for adolescents.

  • Adults should avoid taking more than 150 mcg per day.

Recommended Dosage for Hair Health

The average recommended iodine dose for most adults is 150 mcg daily. Iodine is most effectively absorbed on an empty stomach. It is best to take it half an hour before or two hours after meals to maximize absorption.
