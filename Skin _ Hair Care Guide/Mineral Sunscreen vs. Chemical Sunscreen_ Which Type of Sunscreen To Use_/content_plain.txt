Chemical sunscreen
Chemical sunscreens are made from chemicals called organic compounds, which absorb the UV rays before they reach your skin. These include ingredients like oxybenzone,
avobenzone
and octocrylene. Chemical sunscreens are available in lotions and sprays as well as cream forms.
Pros of using chemical sunscreen
Chemical sunscreens absorb UV rays before they reach the skin's surface by reacting with them. It absorbs the sun's rays, turns them into heat, and then releases them through the skin. They are popular as they are lighter and more sheer than mineral sunscreens and don't feel sticky or greasy. As they don't leave a visible layer on the skin, they can be applied to a broader range of skin tones.
Cons of using chemical sunscreen
The active ingredients used in chemical sunscreens, like oxybenzone and
benzophenone
, have been shown to have adverse impacts on the environment. They can also irritate the skin of those with sensitive skin and acne-prone skin types.
How chemical sunscreens penetrate the skin
Chemical sunscreens are not the same as mineral sunscreens. Chemical sunscreens, rather than producing a physical barrier on the skin's surface, contain organic substances that are absorbed into the skin and operate by absorbing UV radiation before they can cause damage. When exposed to UV radiation, the active chemicals in chemical sunscreens, such as avobenzone, oxybenzone, octocrylene, and others, undergo a chemical reaction. The heat that is produced by these molecules when UV photons are absorbed is subsequently released by the skin.
These chemical compounds produce a thin coating on the skin and are absorbed into the higher layers of the skin (stratum corneum) after application. They become activated after being absorbed and form a protective barrier within the skin, absorbing and neutralising UV radiation before it can permeate deeper layers of the skin and cause damage. While chemical sunscreens are excellent at blocking UV rays, some people are concerned about the potential absorption of these chemicals into the body and their consequences on hormone disruption. However, research in this area is ongoing, and regulatory organisations are continuing to assess the safety of these sunscreen chemicals. Applying the recommended amount of sunscreen and reapplying it as directed is crucial for its effectiveness, regardless of whether it's a chemical or mineral sunscreen. Additionally, following the manufacturer's instructions and using sunscreen in conjunction with other sun-protective measures (like seeking shade, wearing protective clothing, and avoiding peak sun hours) can further enhance sun protection.
Chemical sunscreen and long-term skin health
There is continuous investigation and debate regarding the use of chemical sunscreens and their effects on the long-term health of the skin. Chemical sunscreens efficiently absorb and neutralise UV radiation, but some people are worried about what might happen to their skin and the environment if they are used too often. The following things to think about about chemical sunscreens and long-term skin health:
Skin Sensitivity and Allergies
: Certain chemical sunscreen ingredients may cause skin sensitivity or allergic reactions in some people. In sensitive individuals, ingredients such as oxybenzone or avobenzone may cause irritation or allergic reactions.
Hormone Disruption
: The potential for certain chemical sunscreen ingredients, such as oxybenzone, to disrupt hormones is still being debated and researched. Some research suggests that these compounds may mimic hormones and interfere with the endocrine system; however, the extent and significance of this effect in real-world scenarios is still being investigated.
Environmental Impact
: When certain chemical sunscreen ingredients are washed into oceans and bodies of water, they have been linked to negative effects on marine life and coral reefs. Oxybenzone and octinoxate, for example, have been linked to coral bleaching and can harm aquatic ecosystems.
Sun Protection Efficacy
: When used correctly and reapplied as directed, chemical sunscreens provide effective UV ray protection. However, some chemical sunscreen ingredients may degrade when exposed to sunlight, necessitating more frequent reapplication.
Regulatory Inquiry
: Regulatory bodies are constantly assessing the safety and efficacy of sunscreen ingredients. Some nations or areas have prohibited or restricted the use of specific chemical sunscreen ingredients because they are known to have negative environmental effects.
Manufacturers are investigating new sunscreen formulations that include various UV-filtering ingredients or technologies to address these concerns and offer alternatives. Additionally, for people with sensitive skin or those who want to stay away from specific chemical ingredients, mineral sunscreens—physical blockers like zinc oxide and titanium dioxide—are thought to be safer options.

Choosing the right SPF for your face
Several factors influence the right SPF (Sun Protection Factor) for your face, including your skin type, how much time you spend outside, and how much sun exposure you anticipate. Consider the following guidelines when choosing an SPF for your face:
Understand SPF Levels
:
SPF indicates the level of protection against UVB radiation. Higher SPF values offer better protection. Dermatologists normally prescribe a minimum of SPF 30 for daily use. SPF 30 blocks approximately 97% of UVB radiation, whereas SPF 50 blocks approximately 98%. Because no sunscreen provides complete protection, reapplication and other protective measures are essential.
Consider Your Skin Type
: If you have fair or sensitive skin that burns easily, or if you have a history of skin cancer, you might consider a higher SPF. SPF 30 may be sufficient for daily use for people with darker skin tones or less sun sensitivity.
Daily Use vs. Outdoor Activities
: SPF 30 in your moisturiser or makeup may be sufficient for daily wear when you are mostly indoors and exposed to little sunlight. Consider a higher SPF (e.g., SPF 50 or higher) and reapply sunscreen every two hours when spending extended time outdoors or participating in outdoor activities, especially if you are sweating or swimming.
Sunscreen Ingredients and Formulation
:
Choose a sunscreen designed specifically for the face to avoid clogging pores and causing breakouts. For oily or acne-prone skin, non-comedogenic or oil-free options are available. Look for sunscreens that offer broad-spectrum protection against both UVA and UVB rays. Some facial sunscreens contain additional skincare ingredients that are beneficial to the skin, such as antioxidants or moisturisers.
Consistency and Application
:
Regardless of the SPF level chosen, consistent and proper application is critical. Apply sunscreen liberally to all exposed areas of the face 15-30 minutes before exposure to the sun. Reapply sunscreen every two hours, or sooner if you are swimming, sweating, or wiping your face with a towel.
Consult a Dermatologist
: If you have specific skin concerns or conditions, or if you are not sure which SPF level is best for your skin type, a dermatologist can provide personalised recommendations.
Aside from sunscreen, extra precautions such as wearing hats, sunglasses, and seeking shade during peak sun hours can help protect your face from the sun's harmful rays. Choosing sunscreen based on your skin type and anticipated sun exposure will help ensure effective face protection.

The importance of broad-spectrum protection
Broad-spectrum protection refers to a product's or system's ability to provide defence or coverage against a diverse set of components or dangers. This notion is used in a variety of sectors, including medicine, technology, and environmental science. Here are some instances where broad-spectrum protection is especially important:
Sunscreen and Skin Care
: Broad-spectrum protection is critical in sunscreen and skincare products for protecting the skin from both UVA and UVB rays. UVA rays can cause premature ageing of the skin, while UVB rays cause sunburn. A broad-spectrum sunscreen helps prevent skin damage and lowers the risk of skin cancer.
Antibiotics and Medications
: broad-spectrum antibiotics in medicine are meant to target a large range of bacteria, as opposed to narrow-spectrum antibiotics that target specific species. Broad-spectrum antibiotics are useful when the origin of an infection is unknown or when dealing with various bacterial strains.
Vaccines
: Broad-spectrum vaccines are intended to immunise against a wide range of linked diseases. The influenza vaccine, for example, targets several strains of the flu virus, protecting multiple types.
Computer Security
: Broad-spectrum protection in cybersecurity means guarding against a wide range of cyber threats, such as viruses, malware, ransomware, and phishing assaults. To protect the safety and integrity of digital systems, comprehensive security solutions are required.
Environmental Conservation
: Environmental science relies on broad-spectrum protection to solve a variety of ecological challenges. Conservation initiatives that take into account several species, ecosystems, and environmental conditions lead to a more comprehensive and successful approach to protecting biodiversity and ecosystem health.
Immune System Response
: By recognising and defending against a wide range of pathogens, including bacteria, viruses, and fungus, the human immune system provides broad-spectrum protection. The diversity of the immune response is critical to general health.
Financial Security
: Diversification is a type of broad-spectrum protection in the financial sector. Investors can minimise risk and improve portfolio resilience by diversifying their investments across asset classes, industries, and geographic locations.
Data Protection
: Broad-spectrum protection in data security entails preserving information against a variety of risks, such as hacking, data breaches, and unauthorised access. Strong cybersecurity procedures are required to protect sensitive data's confidentiality, integrity, and availability.
Sunscreen and sensitive skin: finding a gentle formula
The chemicals and composition of a sunscreen for sensitive skin must be carefully considered. Many sunscreens contain chemicals or scents that cause allergic reactions in those with sensitive skin. Here are some guidelines for selecting a mild sunscreen formula:
Look for Physical Sunscreens
: Active ingredients in physical or mineral sunscreens include zinc oxide or titanium dioxide. These minerals sit on the surface of the skin and reflect UV rays. They are generally well tolerated by sensitive skin and are less likely to irritate when compared to chemical sunscreens.
Avoid Harsh Chemicals
: Check the ingredient list and avoid sunscreens that contain harsh chemicals like oxybenzone, avobenzone, octocrylene, and octinoxate. These chemicals have the potential to irritate sensitive skin.
Fragrance-Free Formulas
: Fragrances are a common cause of irritation in people with sensitive skin. Choose a sunscreen that is marked as "fragrance-free" or "scent-free" to reduce the possibility of irritation.
Sunscreens labelled "hypoallergenic"
: Look for sunscreens labelled "hypoallergenic," as these are designed to reduce the risk of allergic reactions. However, it is important to note that the term "hypoallergenic" is not regulated, so checking the ingredient list is still essential.
Oil-Free and Non-Comedogenic
: If you have sensitive or acne-prone skin, look for an oil-free and non-comedogenic sunscreen. These formulations have a lower chance of clogging pores or causing breakouts.
Dermatologist-Tested
: Dermatologist-tested products may be a better option for sensitive skin. Sunscreens that have been dermatologist-tested have been evaluated to ensure that they are suitable for a variety of skin types, including sensitive skin.
SPF and Broad-Spectrum Protection
: Check that the sunscreen has both broad-spectrum (UVA and UVB) protection and an acceptable Sun Protection Factor (SPF). People with delicate skin should avoid both forms of UV radiation.
Patch Test
: Before applying sunscreen to your entire face or body, perform a patch test. Apply a small amount of the product to a small area of your skin and wait 24 hours to see if you have any adverse reactions.
Water-Resistant Formulas
: Choose a water-resistant formula if you intend to be in the water or sweat a lot. However, remember to reapply after swimming or excessive sweating.
See a Dermatologist
: You should think about seeing a dermatologist if you have any particular skin issues or conditions. Based on your skin type and any current skin conditions, they can offer tailored recommendations.
In the end, experimenting may be necessary to determine the best sunscreen for sensitive skin. Consider how your skin feels and select a sunscreen that offers the required protection without aggravating or bothering it.
