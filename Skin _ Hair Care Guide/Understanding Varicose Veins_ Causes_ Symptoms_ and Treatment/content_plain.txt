What are Varicose Veins and What Causes Them?
Varicose veins are a common condition that affects many people, particularly as they age. They occur when the veins in the legs become enlarged, twisted, and swollen, causing discomfort, pain, and sometimes serious complications. The underlying cause of varicose veins is weakened or damaged valves in the veins that prevent blood from flowing properly. As a result, blood pools in the veins, causing them to swell and become visible under the skin. Role of Blood Vessels and Vein Walls in Varicose Veins
Blood vessels play a crucial role in our circulatory system, carrying oxygen and nutrients throughout the body. Veins are blood vessels responsible for carrying blood back to the heart. To facilitate this process, veins have one-way valves to prevent blood from flowing backward. When these valves become damaged or weakened, blood can pool in the veins, causing the vein walls to bulge and stretch. This results in the formation of varicose veins.
Factors Contributing to Varicose Veins
There are several factors that contribute to the development of varicose veins, including:
Genetics
: A family history of varicose veins increases the risk of developing the condition.
Pregnancy
: Hormonal changes and increased blood volume during pregnancy can lead to varicose veins.
Obesity
: Excess body weight puts additional pressure on the veins, increasing the risk of varicose veins.
Standing or sitting for prolonged periods
: This can cause blood to pool in the legs, leading to varicose veins.

Common Symptoms of Varicose Veins
The symptoms of varicose veins vary from person to person, but may include:
Swelling
: The affected area may become swollen, particularly after long periods of standing or sitting.
Achiness
: A dull, aching sensation may be felt in the legs, especially after standing for a long time.
Heaviness in legs
: The legs may feel heavy or tired, particularly at the end of the day.
Skin changes
: Skin near the affected veins may become discoloured, itchy, or inflamed.
Ulcers
: In severe cases, skin ulcers may develop near the varicose veins.
How are Varicose Veins Diagnosed?
A dermatologist or vascular specialist can diagnose varicose veins through a physical examination and by reviewing your medical history. Additional tests, such as an ultrasound or venogram, may be conducted to assess the severity of the condition and determine the best course of treatment.

Non-Invasive Treatment Options for Varicose Veins
Compression stockings
: Wearing compression stockings can help alleviate the symptoms of varicose veins by improving blood flow in the legs.
Lifestyle changes
: Losing weight, exercising regularly, and avoiding standing or sitting for long periods can help reduce the symptoms of varicose veins.
Minimally-Invasive Treatment Options for Varicose Veins
Sclerotherapy
: A solution is injected into the affected vein, causing it to collapse and eventually fade away.
Endovenous ablation therapy
: Laser or radiofrequency energy is used to close off the affected vein.
Laser therapy
: Light energy is directed at the affected vein, causing it to collapse and gradually disappear.
Surgical Treatment Options for Varicose Veins
Radiofrequency ablation
: A catheter is inserted into the vein, and radiofrequency energy is used to heat and close the vein.
Vein stripping
: The affected vein is surgically removed through small incisions.
Phlebectomy
: Small incisions are made along the affected vein, and the vein is removed using specialized tools.
